# Introduction to Psychology

## Overview
Psychology is the scientific study of behavior and mental processes. It uses the scientific method to understand why people think, feel, and act the way they do. The field encompasses multiple perspectives (behavioral, cognitive, biological, psychodynamic, humanistic, sociocultural) that each offer different lenses for understanding human behavior. Research methods include experiments, surveys, case studies, and correlational studies.

## Key Terms
- **Psychology**: The scientific study of behavior (observable actions) and mental processes (thoughts, feelings, perceptions).
- **Behavioral Perspective**: Focuses on observable behavior and how it's shaped by environment and learning (Pavlov, Skinner, Watson).
- **Cognitive Perspective**: Studies mental processes -- thinking, memory, perception, problem-solving, language.
- **Biological Perspective**: Examines behavior through genetics, brain structures, neurotransmitters, and evolution.
- **Psychodynamic Perspective**: Emphasizes unconscious motivations and early childhood experiences (Freud).
- **Humanistic Perspective**: Focuses on personal growth, free will, and self-actualization (Maslow, Rogers).
- **Sociocultural Perspective**: Examines how social and cultural factors influence behavior.
- **Nature vs Nurture**: The debate over whether behavior is determined by genetics (nature) or environment (nurture). Modern view: interaction of both.
- **Scientific Method**: Observation -> Hypothesis -> Experiment -> Analysis -> Conclusion. The foundation of psychology as a science.
- **Independent Variable (IV)**: The variable manipulated by the researcher.
- **Dependent Variable (DV)**: The variable measured as an outcome.
- **Correlation**: A relationship between two variables. Correlation does NOT imply causation.
- **Random Assignment**: Assigning participants randomly to groups to control for confounding variables.
- **Placebo Effect**: Improvement due to expectation of treatment, not the treatment itself.
- **Informed Consent**: Ethical requirement that participants understand and agree to the study's procedures.

## Core Concepts

### The Major Perspectives
Each perspective asks different questions: Behavioral -- "What reinforces this behavior?" Cognitive -- "How does the person think about this?" Biological -- "What brain activity underlies this?" Psychodynamic -- "What unconscious conflicts drive this?" Humanistic -- "How is the person striving to grow?" No single perspective is "right" -- they complement each other.

### Research Methods
Experiments: control variables, establish causation. Surveys: large samples, self-reported data, can't establish causation. Case studies: deep analysis of one individual, not generalizable. Correlational studies: identify relationships, can't determine causation. Longitudinal: study same people over time. Cross-sectional: compare different groups at one time.

### Ethics in Research
APA Ethical Principles: informed consent, minimal deception, debriefing, right to withdraw, confidentiality, no harm. Institutional Review Boards (IRBs) approve research involving human participants.

## Examples

### Nature vs Nurture: Twin Studies
Identical twins raised apart share many traits (nature), but also show differences due to different environments (nurture). Twin studies estimate heritability -- the proportion of variation due to genetics. Most traits show significant contributions from both.

### The Placebo Effect
In drug trials, some patients receive a sugar pill but show improvement because they believe they're being treated. This demonstrates the power of expectations (cognitive perspective) on physical outcomes (biological perspective).

## Relationships
- Scientific method -> Empirical evidence -> Psychological knowledge
- Nature (genetics) + Nurture (environment) -> Behavior
- Correlation ≠ Causation (only experiments establish causation)
- Multiple perspectives -> More complete understanding of behavior
- Ethical guidelines -> Protect research participants

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Psychology is just common sense"**: Many findings are counterintuitive (bystander effect, cognitive dissonance). Common sense often provides contradictory advice.
- **"Correlation means causation"**: Ice cream sales and drowning are correlated -- but ice cream doesn't cause drowning. A third variable (summer/heat) explains both.
- **"Psychology isn't a real science"**: Psychology uses the scientific method, statistical analysis, and peer review just like biology or chemistry.

## Practice Angles

- Identify: IV and DV in a given experiment description
- Explain: why correlation does not imply causation with a real example
