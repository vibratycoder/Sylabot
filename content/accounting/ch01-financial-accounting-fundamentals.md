# Financial Accounting Fundamentals

## Overview
Financial accounting records, classifies, and summarizes business transactions to produce financial statements for external users (investors, creditors, regulators). The system is built on the accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity), double-entry bookkeeping (every transaction affects at least two accounts), and Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP). Understanding debits and credits is the foundation of all accounting.

## Key Terms
- **GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles)**: The standard framework for financial reporting in the US. Set by FASB (Financial Accounting Standards Board).
- **Accounting Equation**: Assets = Liabilities + Stockholders' Equity. Must always balance.
- **Assets**: Resources owned by the company (cash, receivables, inventory, equipment, buildings).
- **Liabilities**: Obligations owed to others (accounts payable, loans, bonds payable).
- **Stockholders' Equity**: Owners' residual interest. Common stock + Retained earnings. Retained earnings = accumulated net income - dividends.
- **Double-Entry Bookkeeping**: Every transaction is recorded with equal debits and credits, maintaining the accounting equation balance.
- **Debit (Dr)**: Left side of an account. Increases assets and expenses; decreases liabilities, equity, and revenue.
- **Credit (Cr)**: Right side of an account. Increases liabilities, equity, and revenue; decreases assets and expenses.
- **Journal Entry**: The record of a transaction showing accounts, debits, and credits with a date and description.
- **T-Account**: A simplified visual representation of an account with debits on the left and credits on the right.
- **Trial Balance**: A list of all accounts and their balances to verify total debits equal total credits.
- **Accrual Basis Accounting**: Revenue recognized when earned (not when cash received); expenses recognized when incurred (not when paid). Required by GAAP.
- **Cash Basis Accounting**: Revenue recognized when cash received; expenses when paid. Not GAAP-compliant but simpler.
- **Revenue Recognition Principle**: Recognize revenue when the performance obligation is satisfied (goods delivered/services performed).
- **Matching Principle**: Expenses should be recorded in the same period as the revenues they helped generate.

## Core Concepts

### The Debit/Credit System
Normal balances: Assets = Debit, Liabilities = Credit, Equity = Credit, Revenue = Credit, Expenses = Debit. To increase an account, enter on its normal balance side. To decrease, enter on the opposite side. Every transaction must have equal total debits and credits.

### The Accounting Cycle
1. Analyze transactions -> 2. Record journal entries -> 3. Post to ledger (T-accounts) -> 4. Prepare trial balance -> 5. Make adjusting entries -> 6. Prepare adjusted trial balance -> 7. Prepare financial statements -> 8. Close temporary accounts -> 9. Post-closing trial balance.

### Adjusting Entries
Made at period end to ensure accrual-basis accuracy. Four types: (1) Prepaid expenses (defer costs already paid), (2) Unearned revenues (defer income already received), (3) Accrued expenses (record costs incurred but not yet paid), (4) Accrued revenues (record income earned but not yet received).

## Examples

### Recording a Sale on Credit
Company sells $5,000 of goods on account. Debit: Accounts Receivable $5,000 (asset increases). Credit: Sales Revenue $5,000 (revenue increases). When customer pays later: Debit Cash $5,000, Credit Accounts Receivable $5,000.

### Adjusting for Prepaid Insurance
Company pays $12,000 for a 12-month insurance policy on Jan 1. Initial entry: Debit Prepaid Insurance $12,000, Credit Cash $12,000. Monthly adjusting entry: Debit Insurance Expense $1,000, Credit Prepaid Insurance $1,000 (1 month used up).

## Relationships
- Assets = Liabilities + Equity (always)
- Revenues increase equity (through retained earnings)
- Expenses decrease equity (through retained earnings)
- Debits = Credits (always, for every transaction)
- Accrual basis -> Revenue when earned, expenses when incurred

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Debit means decrease and credit means increase"**: Depends on the account type. Debits increase assets and expenses; credits increase liabilities, equity, and revenue.
- **"Cash basis is more accurate"**: Accrual basis better reflects economic reality. Cash basis can be misleading (revenue recognized before service delivered, or expenses hidden).
- **"The trial balance proves accuracy"**: It only proves debits equal credits. Errors like posting to the wrong account won't be caught.

## Practice Angles
- Record: journal entries for common transactions (sales, purchases, payments)
- Prepare: T-accounts and a trial balance from journal entries
- Classify: accounts as assets, liabilities, equity, revenue, or expense
- Explain: why adjusting entries are necessary at period end
- Compare and contrast: accrual vs cash basis accounting
