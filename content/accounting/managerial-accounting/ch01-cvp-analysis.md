# Cost-Volume-Profit Analysis

## Overview
Cost-Volume-Profit (CVP) analysis examines the relationships between costs, volume (activity level), and profit to help managers make decisions about pricing, product mix, and production levels. The central question is: how many units must we sell (or how much revenue must we generate) to break even or reach a target profit? CVP relies on classifying all costs as either fixed or variable and computing the contribution margin -- the amount each unit sold contributes toward covering fixed costs and generating profit.

CVP analysis is one of the most practical tools in managerial accounting. It is used for breakeven analysis, what-if scenarios, pricing decisions, and evaluating the risk of different cost structures.

## Key Terms
- **Variable Costs**: Costs that change in total in direct proportion to changes in activity level. Variable cost per unit remains constant. Examples: raw materials, direct labor (if paid per unit), sales commissions.
- **Fixed Costs**: Costs that remain constant in total regardless of changes in activity level within the relevant range. Fixed cost per unit decreases as volume increases (spreading fixed costs). Examples: rent, salaries, insurance, depreciation (straight-line).
- **Mixed Costs (Semi-Variable)**: Costs with both fixed and variable components. Example: a utility bill with a base charge plus a per-usage charge. Separated using the high-low method or regression analysis.
- **Contribution Margin (CM)**: Sales Revenue - Variable Costs. The amount available to cover fixed costs and generate profit.
- **Contribution Margin per Unit**: Selling Price per Unit - Variable Cost per Unit.
- **Contribution Margin Ratio (CM Ratio)**: CM / Sales Revenue, or CM per unit / Selling price per unit. The percentage of each sales dollar available to cover fixed costs and profit.
- **Breakeven Point**: The level of sales at which total revenue equals total costs (profit = 0). No profit, no loss.
- **Breakeven in Units**: Fixed Costs / CM per Unit.
- **Breakeven in Dollars**: Fixed Costs / CM Ratio.
- **Target Profit**: The desired level of profit. Units for target profit = (Fixed Costs + Target Profit) / CM per Unit.
- **Margin of Safety**: The amount by which actual or expected sales exceed breakeven sales. Margin of Safety = Actual Sales - Breakeven Sales. It measures the cushion before losses begin.
- **Operating Leverage**: The degree to which a firm uses fixed costs in its cost structure. Higher fixed costs relative to variable costs = higher operating leverage = greater sensitivity of profit to changes in volume.
- **Degree of Operating Leverage (DOL)**: CM / Operating Income. Tells you the percentage change in operating income for a 1% change in sales.
- **Relevant Range**: The range of activity over which the assumptions of CVP hold (i.e., fixed costs remain fixed and variable cost per unit remains constant).
- **High-Low Method**: A technique for separating mixed costs into fixed and variable components using the highest and lowest activity levels.

## Core Concepts

### The CVP Equation
**Profit = (Price x Quantity) - (Variable Cost per Unit x Quantity) - Fixed Costs**
**Profit = (CM per Unit x Quantity) - Fixed Costs**

At breakeven, Profit = 0:
**Breakeven Units = Fixed Costs / CM per Unit**
**Breakeven Dollars = Fixed Costs / CM Ratio**

### Detailed Example
A company sells a product for $50 per unit. Variable cost = $30 per unit. Fixed costs = $100,000.

CM per unit = $50 - $30 = $20
CM Ratio = $20 / $50 = 40%

Breakeven in units = $100,000 / $20 = 5,000 units
Breakeven in dollars = $100,000 / 0.40 = $250,000

To earn a target profit of $60,000:
Required units = ($100,000 + $60,000) / $20 = 8,000 units
Required revenue = 8,000 x $50 = $400,000

### Margin of Safety
If actual sales = 7,000 units ($350,000):
Margin of Safety (units) = 7,000 - 5,000 = 2,000 units
Margin of Safety ($) = $350,000 - $250,000 = $100,000
Margin of Safety (%) = $100,000 / $350,000 = 28.6%
The company can lose 28.6% of its sales before hitting breakeven.

### Operating Leverage
At 7,000 units: CM = 7,000 x $20 = $140,000; Operating Income = $140,000 - $100,000 = $40,000
DOL = $140,000 / $40,000 = 3.5

A 10% increase in sales -> 35% increase in operating income (10% x 3.5).
A 10% decrease in sales -> 35% decrease in operating income.
Higher operating leverage = higher risk and higher reward.

### Multi-Product CVP
When a company sells multiple products, use the weighted-average CM per unit or weighted-average CM ratio based on the sales mix.

Weighted Avg CM = sum of (CM_i x Mix_i) for all products
Breakeven = Fixed Costs / Weighted Avg CM

Example: Product A (CM = $15, 60% of sales mix), Product B (CM = $25, 40% of sales mix).
Weighted CM = $15 x 0.60 + $25 x 0.40 = $9 + $10 = $19
If fixed costs = $95,000: Breakeven = $95,000 / $19 = 5,000 units (of which 3,000 are A and 2,000 are B).

### High-Low Method for Mixed Costs
Variable cost per unit = (Highest total cost - Lowest total cost) / (Highest activity - Lowest activity)
Fixed cost = Total cost at any point - (Variable rate x Activity at that point)

Example: Jan: 1,000 units, $8,000 cost. June: 3,000 units, $14,000 cost.
Variable rate = ($14,000 - $8,000) / (3,000 - 1,000) = $6,000 / 2,000 = $3/unit
Fixed cost = $8,000 - ($3 x 1,000) = $5,000

### After-Tax Target Profit
If the target is an after-tax profit:
Required pretax profit = After-tax target / (1 - Tax Rate)
Then: Units = (Fixed Costs + Pretax Target) / CM per Unit

Example: Target after-tax profit = $45,000, tax rate = 25%.
Pretax target = $45,000 / (1 - 0.25) = $60,000
Units = ($100,000 + $60,000) / $20 = 8,000 units

## Examples

### Example 1: Pricing Decision
Current: Price $50, VC $30, FC $100K, Volume 6,000. Profit = 6,000 x $20 - $100K = $20K.
Proposal: Cut price to $45 to increase volume to 8,000.
New CM = $45 - $30 = $15. New profit = 8,000 x $15 - $100K = $20K.
Same profit. Not worth the effort and risk of a price cut.

### Example 2: Cost Structure Comparison
Firm A: High fixed ($200K), low variable ($10/unit). CM = $40/unit. DOL high.
Firm B: Low fixed ($50K), high variable ($25/unit). CM = $25/unit. DOL low.
Firm A has higher breakeven (5,000 vs 2,000 units) but profits grow faster once past breakeven.

## Common Misconceptions
- **All costs are either purely fixed or purely variable**: Many costs are mixed. The high-low method and regression help separate them, but these are approximations.
- **CVP assumptions hold at all volumes**: They hold only within the relevant range. Beyond that range, fixed costs may change (e.g., need a new factory) and variable cost per unit may change (e.g., bulk discounts).
- **A higher contribution margin ratio is always better**: It depends on the cost structure. A high CM ratio often means high fixed costs, which means higher breakeven and more risk.
- **Breakeven is the goal**: Breakeven is the minimum. The goal is to exceed breakeven and earn profit. Margin of safety measures how far above breakeven you are.
- **CVP works perfectly for multi-product firms**: CVP requires assuming a constant sales mix. If the mix changes, the breakeven point changes.
