# Budgeting and Variance Analysis

## Overview
A budget is a detailed quantitative plan for acquiring and using resources over a specified period. Budgets serve as tools for planning (deciding what to do), coordination (aligning departments), and control (evaluating performance). The master budget integrates all departmental budgets into a comprehensive financial plan including projected income statement, balance sheet, and cash budget.

Variance analysis compares actual results to budgeted amounts to identify where performance deviated from plan. Variances are classified as favorable (actual is better than budget) or unfavorable (actual is worse). By decomposing variances into price and quantity components, managers can pinpoint whether problems stem from paying too much (price) or using too much (quantity).

## Key Terms
- **Budget**: A formal written plan expressed in quantitative terms, covering a specific future period.
- **Master Budget**: The comprehensive budget that includes all operating budgets and financial budgets. It is the firm's overall plan for the period.
- **Operating Budgets**: Budgets related to the income-generating activities: sales budget, production budget, direct materials budget, direct labor budget, manufacturing overhead budget, selling and administrative expense budget.
- **Financial Budgets**: Budgets related to cash, capital expenditures, and projected financial statements: cash budget, capital expenditure budget, budgeted income statement, budgeted balance sheet.
- **Sales Budget**: The starting point for the master budget. Forecasts the number of units to be sold and the expected revenue.
- **Production Budget**: Determines how many units to produce. Production = Budgeted Sales + Desired Ending Inventory - Beginning Inventory.
- **Cash Budget**: Projects cash receipts and disbursements to ensure the company maintains adequate cash balances. Identifies when borrowing is needed or excess cash is available for investment.
- **Static (Fixed) Budget**: A budget prepared for one specific level of activity. Does not adjust when actual volume differs from planned volume.
- **Flexible Budget**: A budget that adjusts for the actual level of activity achieved. Allows a valid comparison by comparing actual costs at the actual volume to budgeted costs at the actual volume.
- **Standard Costs**: Predetermined per-unit costs that serve as benchmarks. Standard cost = Standard price x Standard quantity. Used for variance analysis.
- **Variance**: The difference between actual results and budgeted/standard amounts. Can be favorable or unfavorable.
- **Favorable Variance (F)**: Actual results are better than budget -- actual revenue > budgeted, or actual cost < budgeted. Increases profit relative to plan.
- **Unfavorable Variance (U)**: Actual results are worse than budget -- actual revenue < budgeted, or actual cost > budgeted. Decreases profit relative to plan.
- **Price Variance (Rate Variance)**: The portion of total variance caused by paying a different price than standard. Price Variance = (Actual Price - Standard Price) x Actual Quantity.
- **Quantity Variance (Efficiency Variance)**: The portion of total variance caused by using a different quantity than standard. Quantity Variance = (Actual Quantity - Standard Quantity) x Standard Price.
- **Direct Materials Price Variance**: (Actual Price - Standard Price) x Actual Quantity Purchased.
- **Direct Materials Quantity Variance**: (Actual Quantity Used - Standard Quantity Allowed) x Standard Price.
- **Direct Labor Rate Variance**: (Actual Rate - Standard Rate) x Actual Hours Worked.
- **Direct Labor Efficiency Variance**: (Actual Hours - Standard Hours Allowed) x Standard Rate.
- **Overhead Spending Variance**: Actual Overhead - Budgeted Overhead for Actual Activity.
- **Overhead Volume Variance**: Budgeted Overhead at Actual Activity - Applied Overhead. Reflects underutilization or overutilization of capacity.
- **Management by Exception**: The practice of investigating only significant variances rather than every deviation. Focuses managerial attention on areas most in need of correction.

## Core Concepts

### The Master Budget Sequence
1. **Sales Budget** -> drives everything else
2. **Production Budget** -> based on sales + desired ending inventory
3. **Direct Materials Budget** -> based on production requirements
4. **Direct Labor Budget** -> based on production requirements
5. **Manufacturing Overhead Budget** -> based on activity level
6. **Selling & Admin Expense Budget**
7. **Cash Budget** -> timing of all receipts and payments
8. **Budgeted Income Statement**
9. **Budgeted Balance Sheet**

### Production Budget
**Units to Produce = Budgeted Sales + Desired Ending Inventory - Beginning Inventory**

Example: Budgeted sales = 10,000 units. Desired ending inventory = 1,500 units. Beginning inventory = 1,000 units.
Production = 10,000 + 1,500 - 1,000 = 10,500 units

### Flexible Budget
A flexible budget recalculates expected costs at the actual activity level:
Flexible Budget Cost = (Variable Cost per Unit x Actual Units) + Fixed Costs

Example: Budget: 10,000 units, VC $5/unit, FC $50,000. Actual: 12,000 units.
Static budget total = $50,000 + $50,000 = $100,000
Flexible budget total = $5 x 12,000 + $50,000 = $110,000
Without the flexible budget, you might think costs were $10,000 over budget, when really they were exactly on track for the higher volume.

### Variance Analysis Framework

**Total Variance = Actual Cost - Standard Cost**
**Total Variance = Price Variance + Quantity Variance**

**Price Variance = (AP - SP) x AQ**
**Quantity Variance = (AQ - SQ) x SP**

Where: AP = Actual Price, SP = Standard Price, AQ = Actual Quantity, SQ = Standard Quantity Allowed for actual output.

### Direct Materials Variances Example
Standard: 2 lbs per unit at $3/lb. Actual production: 5,000 units.
Standard quantity allowed = 5,000 x 2 = 10,000 lbs
Actual: Purchased and used 10,500 lbs at $3.20/lb

Materials Price Variance = ($3.20 - $3.00) x 10,500 = $2,100 Unfavorable (paid more than standard)
Materials Quantity Variance = (10,500 - 10,000) x $3.00 = $1,500 Unfavorable (used more than standard)
Total Materials Variance = $2,100 + $1,500 = $3,600 Unfavorable

### Direct Labor Variances Example
Standard: 0.5 hours per unit at $20/hour. Actual production: 5,000 units.
Standard hours allowed = 5,000 x 0.5 = 2,500 hours
Actual: 2,600 hours at $19.50/hour

Labor Rate Variance = ($19.50 - $20.00) x 2,600 = -$1,300 = $1,300 Favorable (paid less per hour)
Labor Efficiency Variance = (2,600 - 2,500) x $20.00 = $2,000 Unfavorable (used more hours)
Total Labor Variance = -$1,300 + $2,000 = $700 Unfavorable

### Investigating Variances
Not all variances warrant investigation. Managers consider:
- **Magnitude**: Is it a significant dollar amount or percentage?
- **Trend**: Is it recurring or one-time?
- **Controllability**: Can management influence the cause?
- **Interrelationship**: Did a favorable price variance (buying cheaper materials) cause an unfavorable quantity variance (more waste from lower quality)?

## Examples

### Example 1: Cash Budget
January: Cash sales $40K, Collections from December credit sales $30K, Pay suppliers $25K, Pay wages $20K, Pay rent $5K, Beginning cash $10K, Minimum cash required $8K.
Cash in = $40K + $30K = $70K. Cash out = $25K + $20K + $5K = $50K.
Net = +$20K. Ending cash = $10K + $20K = $30K. Above minimum; no borrowing needed.

### Example 2: Flexible Budget Performance Report
|  | Actual (12K units) | Flexible Budget (12K units) | Variance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue | $132,000 | $120,000 | $12,000 F |
| Variable costs | $65,000 | $60,000 | $5,000 U |
| Fixed costs | $52,000 | $50,000 | $2,000 U |
| Operating income | $15,000 | $10,000 | $5,000 F |

The flexible budget isolates volume effects. Revenue exceeded plan by $12K (sold at higher price), but variable and fixed costs were also over budget.

## Common Misconceptions
- **Favorable variances are always good**: A favorable materials price variance from buying cheap materials may cause unfavorable quality and efficiency variances. Variances must be analyzed together.
- **Unfavorable variances always indicate poor management**: Some variances are caused by external factors (market price increases, supply shortages) beyond management's control.
- **Static budgets are sufficient for performance evaluation**: Static budgets do not account for volume differences. A manager who produces 20% more units will naturally have higher total costs. Flexible budgets provide a fair comparison.
- **All variances should be investigated**: Management by exception focuses on material variances. Investigating every small variance wastes time and resources.
- **Budgets are set-it-and-forget-it**: Good budgeting involves regular comparison to actuals, revision of forecasts, and continuous improvement.
