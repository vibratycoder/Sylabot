# The Financial Statements

## Overview
The four financial statements provide a comprehensive picture of a company's financial health. The income statement shows profitability over a period. The balance sheet shows financial position at a point in time. The statement of retained earnings connects net income to equity. The cash flow statement shows where cash came from and where it went. These statements are interconnected -- net income flows to retained earnings, which appears on the balance sheet.

## Key Terms
- **Income Statement**: Reports revenues and expenses over a period. Bottom line = Net Income. Format: Revenue - COGS = Gross Profit - Operating Expenses = Operating Income - Interest - Taxes = Net Income.
- **Balance Sheet**: Reports assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific date. Assets = Liabilities + Equity.
- **Statement of Retained Earnings**: Beginning retained earnings + Net income - Dividends = Ending retained earnings.
- **Cash Flow Statement**: Three sections: Operating (day-to-day business), Investing (buying/selling long-term assets), Financing (debt and equity transactions). Shows net change in cash.
- **Current Assets**: Assets expected to be converted to cash within one year (cash, receivables, inventory, prepaid expenses).
- **Non-Current Assets**: Long-term assets (property, plant, equipment, intangibles, investments).
- **Current Liabilities**: Obligations due within one year (accounts payable, accrued expenses, current portion of debt).
- **Non-Current Liabilities**: Long-term obligations (bonds payable, long-term loans, deferred tax).
- **Depreciation**: Allocating the cost of a long-term asset over its useful life. Not a cash outflow -- it's added back in the cash flow statement.
- **Working Capital**: Current Assets - Current Liabilities. Measures short-term financial health.

## Core Concepts

### How Statements Connect
Net income (from income statement) flows into the statement of retained earnings. Ending retained earnings appears on the balance sheet under equity. The cash flow statement reconciles the change in cash on the balance sheet. All four statements are linked and must be consistent.

### Cash Flow from Operations (Indirect Method)
Start with net income. Add back non-cash expenses (depreciation, amortization). Adjust for changes in working capital accounts: increase in receivables = subtract (earned but not collected), increase in payables = add (incurred but not paid), increase in inventory = subtract (cash spent on inventory).

### Reading Financial Statements
Look for trends over multiple periods. Compare to industry peers. Red flags: declining revenue with increasing receivables, growing inventory, negative operating cash flow despite positive net income.

## Examples

### Simple Income Statement
Revenue: $500,000. COGS: $300,000. Gross Profit: $200,000. Operating Expenses: $120,000. Operating Income: $80,000. Interest: $10,000. Tax (25%): $17,500. Net Income: $52,500.

### Cash Flow Reconciliation
Net Income: $52,500. Add depreciation: $20,000. Increase in receivables: -$15,000. Increase in payables: +$8,000. Cash from operations: $65,500. Investing: bought equipment -$50,000. Financing: repaid loan -$20,000, dividends -$10,000. Net cash change: -$14,500.

## Relationships
- Net income -> Retained earnings -> Balance sheet equity
- Revenue - Expenses = Net Income (income statement)
- Assets = Liabilities + Equity (balance sheet)
- Cash from ops + investing + financing = Change in cash
- Depreciation is a non-cash expense that reduces net income but doesn't affect cash

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Profitable companies always have cash"**: A company can be profitable (positive net income) but cash-poor if receivables are growing or capital expenditures are high.
- **"Depreciation is a cash expense"**: Depreciation allocates a past cash purchase over time. It reduces reported income but is NOT a current cash outflow.
- **"The balance sheet shows what a company is worth"**: The balance sheet shows historical cost, not market value. Assets may be worth more (or less) than their book value.

## Practice Angles
- Prepare: an income statement from a list of revenues and expenses
- Prepare: a cash flow statement using the indirect method
- Trace: how a single transaction flows through all four financial statements
- Calculate: working capital and explain its significance
- Analyze: why a company with positive net income might have negative cash flow
