# Auditing Concepts and Standards

## Overview
Auditing is the systematic examination of financial statements and related disclosures to provide assurance that they are presented fairly in accordance with the applicable financial reporting framework (GAAP). An independent auditor evaluates the financial statements and issues an opinion on whether they are free from material misstatement. Auditing serves the public interest by enhancing the reliability of financial information used by investors, creditors, regulators, and other stakeholders.

The audit profession is governed by Generally Accepted Auditing Standards (GAAS) issued by the AICPA for private companies and PCAOB standards for public companies. Key concepts include audit risk, materiality, evidence, internal controls, and professional ethics.

## Key Terms
- **Audit**: An independent examination of financial statements to form an opinion on whether they present fairly the financial position and results of operations in accordance with GAAP.
- **Assurance**: The level of confidence that the financial statements are free from material misstatement. An audit provides reasonable (not absolute) assurance.
- **Auditor Independence**: The auditor must be free from financial or personal relationships with the client that could impair objectivity. Independence is both factual (in fact) and perceptual (in appearance).
- **Professional Skepticism**: An attitude of questioning mind and critical assessment of evidence. Auditors must not assume management is dishonest or honest -- they must evaluate evidence objectively.
- **Materiality**: An amount is material if its misstatement could influence the decisions of a reasonable user. The auditor sets a materiality threshold to determine the scope and nature of testing.
- **Audit Risk**: The risk that the auditor issues an incorrect opinion -- specifically, that the auditor gives an unmodified (clean) opinion when the financial statements contain a material misstatement.
- **Audit Risk Model**: Audit Risk = Inherent Risk x Control Risk x Detection Risk.
- **Inherent Risk (IR)**: The susceptibility of an assertion to a material misstatement, assuming no related internal controls. Higher for complex transactions, estimates, and industries prone to fraud.
- **Control Risk (CR)**: The risk that a material misstatement will not be prevented or detected by the entity's internal controls. Depends on the effectiveness of the control environment.
- **Detection Risk (DR)**: The risk that the auditor's procedures will fail to detect a material misstatement. The auditor controls detection risk by adjusting the nature, timing, and extent of procedures.
- **GAAS (Generally Accepted Auditing Standards)**: The professional standards governing audit quality, established by the AICPA (for non-public companies). Three categories: General Standards, Standards of Field Work, Standards of Reporting.
- **PCAOB (Public Company Accounting Oversight Board)**: The regulatory body that sets auditing standards for audits of public companies (created by the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002).
- **Internal Controls**: Processes designed by management to provide reasonable assurance about the achievement of objectives in: (1) reliability of financial reporting, (2) effectiveness and efficiency of operations, (3) compliance with laws and regulations.
- **COSO Framework**: The Committee of Sponsoring Organizations framework for internal control. Five components: Control Environment, Risk Assessment, Control Activities, Information & Communication, Monitoring Activities.
- **Audit Evidence**: All information used by the auditor to support the audit opinion. Includes documents, records, confirmations, observations, inquiries, and analytical procedures.
- **Sufficient**: The quantity of evidence (enough to support the conclusion).
- **Appropriate**: The quality of evidence (relevant and reliable).
- **Substantive Procedures**: Audit procedures designed to detect material misstatements at the assertion level. Includes tests of details (inspect documents, confirm balances) and substantive analytical procedures.
- **Tests of Controls**: Audit procedures to evaluate the operating effectiveness of controls in preventing or detecting misstatements. If controls are effective, the auditor can reduce substantive testing.
- **Assertions**: Claims made by management about the financial statements. Categories: Existence/Occurrence, Completeness, Rights and Obligations, Valuation/Allocation, Presentation and Disclosure.
- **Audit Opinion Types**:
  - **Unmodified (Clean)**: Financial statements are fairly presented in all material respects.
  - **Qualified**: Except for a specific matter, financial statements are fairly presented.
  - **Adverse**: Financial statements are NOT fairly presented.
  - **Disclaimer**: The auditor cannot form an opinion (scope limitation).
- **Going Concern**: The auditor evaluates whether there is substantial doubt about the entity's ability to continue as a going concern for 12 months. If so, additional disclosure (and possibly a modified opinion) is required.
- **Fraud Triangle**: Three conditions that enable fraud: (1) Pressure/Incentive (motivation to commit fraud), (2) Opportunity (weak controls), (3) Rationalization (justification). Auditors assess fraud risk as part of every audit.
- **Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX, 2002)**: Landmark legislation requiring: CEO/CFO certification of financial statements, auditor independence rules, audit committee requirements, internal control assessment, and the creation of the PCAOB.

## Core Concepts

### The Audit Risk Model
**AR = IR x CR x DR**

The auditor's goal is to reduce audit risk to an acceptably low level. IR and CR are assessed but cannot be changed by the auditor. The auditor controls DR through the nature, timing, and extent of procedures.

If IR and CR are high (risky assertion, weak controls), DR must be set low -> more extensive testing.
If IR and CR are low (straightforward assertion, strong controls), DR can be higher -> less testing.

### Audit Process Overview
1. **Engagement acceptance**: Evaluate client risk, independence, and competence
2. **Planning**: Understand the entity, assess risks, set materiality, design audit procedures
3. **Internal control evaluation**: Understand and test controls (required for public companies under SOX)
4. **Substantive testing**: Test account balances and transactions for misstatements
5. **Completion**: Evaluate findings, subsequent events, management representations
6. **Reporting**: Issue the audit opinion

### Internal Control Components (COSO)
1. **Control Environment**: Tone at the top, ethical values, management philosophy, governance
2. **Risk Assessment**: Entity's process for identifying and analyzing business risks
3. **Control Activities**: Policies and procedures (approvals, reconciliations, segregation of duties)
4. **Information & Communication**: Systems for capturing and sharing relevant information
5. **Monitoring**: Ongoing and periodic evaluation of control effectiveness

### Management Assertions
The auditor tests whether each financial statement amount satisfies these claims:
- **Existence/Occurrence**: Assets and liabilities exist; transactions occurred
- **Completeness**: All transactions are recorded; nothing is omitted
- **Rights and Obligations**: The entity has rights to assets and obligations for liabilities
- **Valuation/Allocation**: Amounts are recorded at appropriate values
- **Presentation and Disclosure**: Items are properly classified and disclosed

### Types of Audit Evidence (ranked by reliability)
1. Physical examination (most reliable -- counting cash, inspecting inventory)
2. External confirmation (bank confirmations, customer balance confirmations)
3. Documentation/inspection (examining invoices, contracts)
4. Analytical procedures (comparing ratios, trends to expectations)
5. Inquiry of management/staff (least reliable -- must be corroborated)

## Examples

### Example 1: Audit Risk Assessment
An auditor assesses inherent risk at 80% and control risk at 50% for inventory. Acceptable audit risk is 5%.
DR = AR / (IR x CR) = 0.05 / (0.80 x 0.50) = 0.05 / 0.40 = 12.5%
The auditor must design procedures that achieve a 12.5% or lower detection risk -- meaning very thorough testing of inventory.

### Example 2: Materiality
A company has total assets of $10M and net income of $500K. The auditor might set materiality at 5% of pretax income = $25,000. Misstatements below $25K are unlikely to influence user decisions. Misstatements above $25K require investigation and possible correction.

### Example 3: Fraud Risk Indicators
Red flags: CEO's compensation tied to stock price (pressure), weak segregation of duties in cash handling (opportunity), and management stating "it's just a small adjustment" (rationalization). The auditor would increase substantive testing of areas where these risks are highest.

## Common Misconceptions
- **An audit guarantees the financial statements are correct**: An audit provides reasonable, not absolute, assurance. Some misstatements may exist below the materiality threshold.
- **Auditors are responsible for detecting all fraud**: Auditors have a responsibility to plan and perform the audit to obtain reasonable assurance that material fraud is detected. Cleverly concealed fraud may still escape detection.
- **The auditor prepares the financial statements**: Management prepares the financial statements. The auditor examines them and issues an opinion.
- **An unmodified opinion means the company is in good financial health**: It only means the financial statements are fairly presented under GAAP. A company can receive a clean audit opinion and still be in financial distress.
- **Internal controls prevent all errors and fraud**: Internal controls provide reasonable, not absolute, assurance. Controls can be overridden by management (especially senior executives) and can fail when people collude.
