# Tax Accounting Basics

## Overview
Tax accounting determines the amount of income tax owed to the government. The U.S. federal income tax system is progressive -- higher income earners pay a higher percentage. Individual taxable income is calculated by starting with gross income, subtracting adjustments to reach adjusted gross income (AGI), then subtracting deductions (standard or itemized) to arrive at taxable income. Tax is computed on taxable income using the appropriate rate brackets, then reduced by any credits.

Understanding the distinction between deductions (reduce taxable income) and credits (directly reduce tax owed) is fundamental. Tax planning involves timing income and deductions to minimize tax liability legally.

## Key Terms
- **Gross Income**: All income from whatever source derived, unless specifically excluded. Includes wages, salary, tips, interest, dividends, business income, capital gains, rental income, alimony (pre-2019 agreements), and retirement distributions.
- **Exclusions**: Income items specifically excluded from gross income by law. Examples: gifts, inheritances, life insurance proceeds, municipal bond interest, certain employer-provided benefits.
- **Adjusted Gross Income (AGI)**: Gross Income minus "above-the-line" deductions (adjustments). AGI is a key threshold used to determine eligibility for various deductions and credits.
- **Above-the-Line Deductions (Adjustments)**: Deductions taken from gross income to arrive at AGI. Available to all taxpayers regardless of whether they itemize. Examples: student loan interest, educator expenses, IRA contributions, self-employment tax deduction.
- **Standard Deduction**: A fixed dollar amount that reduces taxable income, available to all taxpayers who do not itemize. Amounts are adjusted annually for inflation.
- **Itemized Deductions**: Specific expenses that can be deducted instead of the standard deduction. Include state/local taxes (SALT, capped at $10,000), mortgage interest, charitable contributions, and medical expenses (above 7.5% of AGI).
- **Taxable Income**: AGI minus the greater of the standard deduction or itemized deductions. This is the amount on which tax is calculated.
- **Tax Bracket**: The range of income to which a specific tax rate applies. The U.S. has graduated brackets (e.g., 10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, 37%).
- **Marginal Tax Rate**: The tax rate applied to the last dollar of taxable income. The rate on the next dollar earned.
- **Effective Tax Rate**: Total tax / Total taxable income. The average rate paid across all brackets.
- **Tax Credit**: A dollar-for-dollar reduction in tax owed. More valuable than a deduction of the same amount. Examples: Child Tax Credit, Earned Income Credit, education credits.
- **Refundable Credit**: A credit that can result in a refund even if the taxpayer owes no tax (e.g., Earned Income Credit).
- **Non-Refundable Credit**: A credit that can reduce tax to zero but no further (e.g., Lifetime Learning Credit).
- **Filing Status**: Determines tax brackets and standard deduction amount. Options: Single, Married Filing Jointly, Married Filing Separately, Head of Household, Qualifying Surviving Spouse.
- **Capital Gain**: The profit from selling a capital asset (stocks, bonds, real estate) for more than its basis (cost).
- **Short-Term Capital Gain**: Gain on an asset held one year or less. Taxed at ordinary income rates.
- **Long-Term Capital Gain**: Gain on an asset held more than one year. Taxed at preferential rates (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income).
- **Tax Basis**: The original cost of an asset used to calculate gain or loss on sale. Basis may be adjusted for depreciation, improvements, etc.
- **Withholding**: Amounts deducted from paychecks by employers and remitted to the IRS as prepayment of income tax.
- **Estimated Tax Payments**: Quarterly tax payments made by self-employed individuals and others whose income is not subject to withholding.

## Core Concepts

### Individual Tax Formula
```
Gross Income
- Adjustments (above-the-line deductions)
= Adjusted Gross Income (AGI)
- Greater of: Standard Deduction or Itemized Deductions
= Taxable Income
x Tax Rate(s) from brackets
= Tax Liability (before credits)
- Tax Credits
= Tax Owed (or Refund Due)
- Payments/Withholding
= Amount Due / Refund
```

### Progressive Tax Calculation
Example: Single filer, taxable income = $60,000 (using simplified brackets):
- First $11,000 at 10% = $1,100
- $11,001 to $44,725 at 12% = $4,047
- $44,726 to $60,000 at 22% = $3,361
Total tax = $8,508
Marginal rate = 22% (the rate on the last dollar)
Effective rate = $8,508 / $60,000 = 14.2%

### Deduction vs. Credit
A $1,000 deduction at the 22% bracket saves $220 in tax ($1,000 x 22%).
A $1,000 credit saves $1,000 in tax (dollar for dollar).
Credits are always more valuable than deductions of the same amount.

### Capital Gains Taxation
Short-term gains (held <= 1 year): taxed as ordinary income
Long-term gains (held > 1 year): taxed at preferential rates (0%, 15%, or 20%)

Example: Buy stock for $10,000, sell for $15,000 after 2 years.
Long-term capital gain = $5,000. If in the 15% LTCG bracket: Tax = $750.
If the same gain were short-term: Tax = $5,000 x 22% = $1,100 (at 22% bracket).

### Corporate Taxation (ACG 351)
Corporations are taxed on their taxable income (revenue - deductions). Current federal corporate tax rate: flat 21%.
Corporate taxable income = Gross income - Business deductions (COGS, salaries, rent, depreciation, interest, etc.)
Corporations face double taxation: profits taxed at the corporate level, and dividends taxed again at the shareholder level.

### Book-Tax Differences
Financial accounting (GAAP) and tax accounting often differ:
- **Temporary differences**: Items recognized in different periods (e.g., depreciation methods differ). Create deferred tax assets or liabilities.
- **Permanent differences**: Items included in one system but never in the other (e.g., municipal bond interest excluded from taxable income forever; fines not deductible).

## Examples

### Example 1: Individual Tax Calculation
Salary $75,000; Interest income $2,000; Student loan interest paid $1,500; Standard deduction $14,600; Child tax credit $2,000.
Gross Income = $77,000
AGI = $77,000 - $1,500 = $75,500
Taxable Income = $75,500 - $14,600 = $60,900
Tax (computed on brackets) = approximately $8,706
Tax after credit = $8,706 - $2,000 = $6,706

### Example 2: Deduction Decision
Taxpayer has $12,000 in potential itemized deductions. Standard deduction = $14,600.
Decision: Take the standard deduction ($14,600 > $12,000). Itemizing would result in a higher taxable income.

## Common Misconceptions
- **Moving into a higher tax bracket means all income is taxed at the higher rate**: Only the income WITHIN that bracket is taxed at the higher rate. Lower brackets still apply to income below the threshold. This is the progressive system.
- **Deductions and credits are the same**: Deductions reduce taxable income; credits reduce tax owed dollar for dollar. Credits are far more valuable.
- **Self-employed people don't pay Social Security/Medicare**: They pay BOTH the employer and employee portions (self-employment tax), totaling 15.3% on net earnings.
- **Capital losses can fully offset ordinary income**: Capital losses first offset capital gains. Only $3,000 of net capital losses can offset ordinary income per year (excess carries forward).
- **Tax-exempt income is never reported**: Some tax-exempt income (like municipal bond interest) must still be reported on the tax return even though it is not taxable.
