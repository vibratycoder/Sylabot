# GAAP Principles and the Conceptual Framework

## Overview
Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) are the standardized rules and guidelines that govern financial accounting and reporting in the United States. The Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB) is the primary body that establishes GAAP. The conceptual framework provides the theoretical foundation for accounting standards: it defines the objectives of financial reporting, the qualitative characteristics of useful information, and the fundamental recognition and measurement principles.

Understanding GAAP is essential because it determines WHEN revenues and expenses are recognized, HOW assets and liabilities are measured, and WHAT must be disclosed. Deviations from GAAP can lead to misstated financial statements, regulatory penalties, and loss of investor confidence.

## Key Terms
- **GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles)**: The standard framework of guidelines, conventions, and rules that accountants follow in recording and summarizing transactions and in preparing financial statements.
- **FASB (Financial Accounting Standards Board)**: The independent organization that establishes GAAP for public and private companies in the U.S.
- **SEC (Securities and Exchange Commission)**: The government agency with legal authority over financial reporting for publicly traded companies. Delegates standard-setting to FASB but retains oversight.
- **IFRS (International Financial Reporting Standards)**: Accounting standards used in most countries outside the U.S., set by the International Accounting Standards Board (IASB). More principles-based than GAAP.
- **Conceptual Framework**: The theoretical foundation for accounting standards. Defines objectives, qualitative characteristics, elements, and recognition/measurement criteria.
- **Accrual Basis Accounting**: Revenue is recognized when earned and expenses when incurred, regardless of when cash changes hands. Required by GAAP. Provides a more accurate picture of economic reality.
- **Cash Basis Accounting**: Revenue and expenses recognized only when cash is received or paid. Simpler but does not match revenues to the period in which they are earned. NOT GAAP-compliant for most businesses.
- **Revenue Recognition Principle (ASC 606)**: Revenue is recognized when a performance obligation is satisfied -- that is, when control of a good or service transfers to the customer. Five-step process: (1) identify the contract, (2) identify performance obligations, (3) determine transaction price, (4) allocate price to obligations, (5) recognize revenue when each obligation is satisfied.
- **Matching Principle (Expense Recognition)**: Expenses should be recognized in the same period as the revenues they help generate. Ensures income statement reflects the true cost of earning revenue.
- **Historical Cost Principle**: Assets are recorded at their original acquisition cost. Provides objectivity and verifiability but may not reflect current value.
- **Fair Value**: The price that would be received to sell an asset in an orderly transaction between market participants. Used for some financial instruments and in impairment testing.
- **Full Disclosure Principle**: Financial statements should include all information that could affect a user's understanding and decisions. Implemented through notes and supplementary disclosures.
- **Going Concern Assumption**: Financial statements are prepared under the assumption that the company will continue operating indefinitely. If liquidation is likely, different accounting rules apply.
- **Economic Entity Assumption**: The activities of the entity are kept separate from the activities of its owners and other entities.
- **Monetary Unit Assumption**: Financial transactions are recorded in a stable monetary unit (the dollar). Ignores the effects of inflation.
- **Periodicity Assumption**: The life of a business can be divided into artificial time periods (months, quarters, years) for reporting purposes.
- **Materiality**: Information is material if omitting or misstating it could influence the decisions of users. Immaterial items need not follow strict GAAP treatment.
- **Conservatism (Prudence)**: When uncertainty exists, choose the accounting method that is least likely to overstate assets or income. "Anticipate losses, do not anticipate gains."
- **Consistency Principle**: A company should use the same accounting methods from period to period to allow meaningful comparisons. Changes are permitted but must be disclosed.
- **Comparability**: Financial information should be presented in a way that allows users to identify similarities and differences between entities.
- **Relevance**: Information is relevant if it has predictive value, confirmatory value, or both -- it can influence decisions.
- **Faithful Representation**: Information must be complete, neutral, and free from error to faithfully represent economic reality.
- **Verifiability**: Independent parties using the same data and methods would reach the same conclusion.

## Core Concepts

### Hierarchy of GAAP
1. **FASB Accounting Standards Codification (ASC)**: The single authoritative source of GAAP
2. **SEC rules and interpretive releases**: For publicly traded companies
3. **FASB Concepts Statements**: Guide standard-setting but are not themselves GAAP

### Accrual vs. Cash Basis: Why It Matters
Cash basis: A law firm receives a $10,000 retainer in December for work to be done in January. Under cash basis, $10,000 revenue in December. Under accrual basis, $0 revenue in December (unearned revenue liability), $10,000 revenue in January when work is performed.

Accrual accounting better matches economic reality: revenue is reported when earned, expenses when incurred, regardless of cash timing.

### Revenue Recognition (ASC 606) Five Steps
1. **Identify the contract**: Agreement between parties with commercial substance
2. **Identify performance obligations**: Distinct goods/services promised
3. **Determine the transaction price**: The amount expected to be received
4. **Allocate the price**: To each performance obligation based on standalone selling prices
5. **Recognize revenue**: When (or as) each performance obligation is satisfied

Example: A software company sells a $1,200 package: $900 for software (delivered immediately) and $300 for 12 months of support.
At delivery: Recognize $900 revenue (software obligation satisfied)
Monthly: Recognize $25/month revenue for support ($300/12) over 12 months

### Conservatism in Practice
- Write down inventory to market value when it falls below cost (lower of cost or market)
- Record estimated bad debt expense even though specific accounts have not yet defaulted
- Do NOT write up asset values when market value exceeds cost (no gains until realized)
- Accrue estimated lawsuit losses when probable and estimable

## Examples

### Example 1: Accrual vs Cash
A company provides $5,000 of services in March but collects cash in April.
Accrual: Revenue in March (when earned). Cash basis: Revenue in April (when collected).
The company also received a $3,000 electricity bill in March but will pay in April.
Accrual: Expense in March (when incurred). Cash basis: Expense in April (when paid).

### Example 2: Matching Principle
A company pays $12,000 for a one-year insurance policy on July 1.
On July 1: Record Prepaid Insurance (asset) $12,000.
Each month (July-June): Recognize $1,000 Insurance Expense. At Dec 31 (6 months): $6,000 expense, $6,000 prepaid asset remaining.
Matching requires spreading the cost over the periods that benefit from coverage.

### Example 3: Materiality
A company with $500M in revenue discovers a $200 error in office supplies expense. Is it material? No -- $200 out of $500M would not influence any reasonable user's decision. The error need not be corrected. But a $5M error in revenue would likely be material and must be corrected.

## Common Misconceptions
- **GAAP is a single document**: GAAP is a codification of hundreds of standards, interpretations, and guidelines organized into topics (the ASC).
- **Conservative accounting is always best**: Excessive conservatism can understate performance and mislead users. The goal is faithful representation, not pessimism.
- **Cash basis is easier so it must be acceptable**: Cash basis distorts performance by mismatching revenues and expenses across periods. GAAP requires accrual for all but the smallest businesses.
- **GAAP and IFRS are the same**: They share many principles but differ significantly on topics like inventory (LIFO allowed under GAAP, not IFRS), leases, and revenue recognition details.
- **Companies can choose whatever accounting methods they want**: Companies must follow GAAP. They have some choices within GAAP (e.g., depreciation method, inventory method) but must apply them consistently and disclose their choices.
