# Adjusting Entries

## Overview
Adjusting entries are journal entries made at the end of an accounting period to ensure that revenues and expenses are recorded in the correct period under accrual accounting. Without adjusting entries, the financial statements would be inaccurate -- some revenues and expenses would appear in the wrong period. There are four types of adjusting entries: prepaid expenses (deferrals), unearned revenues (deferrals), accrued revenues, and accrued expenses. Depreciation is a special case of prepaid expense deferral.

Adjusting entries are made after recording routine transactions but before preparing financial statements. They never involve cash -- cash was either already received/paid or will be in a future period.

## Key Terms
- **Adjusting Entries**: Journal entries made at the end of the period to update account balances and ensure revenue and expense recognition comply with GAAP. They always affect at least one income statement account and one balance sheet account.
- **Deferrals**: Transactions where cash has already been exchanged, but the revenue or expense must be spread across periods.
- **Prepaid Expenses (Deferred Expenses)**: Assets that have been paid for but not yet used/consumed. The adjusting entry transfers the used portion from the asset to expense.
- **Unearned Revenue (Deferred Revenue)**: Liabilities for cash received before services are performed. The adjusting entry transfers the earned portion from the liability to revenue.
- **Accruals**: Transactions where revenue has been earned or expenses incurred but no cash has yet changed hands.
- **Accrued Revenues**: Revenues that have been earned but not yet recorded or collected. The adjusting entry records the revenue and creates a receivable.
- **Accrued Expenses**: Expenses that have been incurred but not yet recorded or paid. The adjusting entry records the expense and creates a payable.
- **Depreciation Expense**: The adjusting entry to allocate the cost of a long-term asset to expense over its useful life. Dr. Depreciation Expense / Cr. Accumulated Depreciation.
- **Adjusted Trial Balance**: A trial balance prepared after all adjusting entries have been posted. It is the basis for preparing financial statements.

## Core Concepts

### Four Types of Adjusting Entries

**1. Prepaid Expenses (Deferrals -- previously paid)**
Cash was paid BEFORE the expense is recognized.
Adjusting entry: Dr. Expense / Cr. Asset (prepaid)

Example: On Jan 1, paid $12,000 for a 12-month insurance policy.
Initial entry: Dr. Prepaid Insurance $12,000 / Cr. Cash $12,000
Monthly adjusting entry: Dr. Insurance Expense $1,000 / Cr. Prepaid Insurance $1,000
After 3 months: Prepaid Insurance balance = $9,000; Insurance Expense = $3,000

**2. Unearned Revenue (Deferrals -- previously received)**
Cash was received BEFORE the revenue is earned.
Adjusting entry: Dr. Liability (unearned revenue) / Cr. Revenue

Example: On Nov 1, received $6,000 for 6 months of services (Nov-Apr).
Initial entry: Dr. Cash $6,000 / Cr. Unearned Revenue $6,000
Dec 31 adjusting entry (2 months earned): Dr. Unearned Revenue $2,000 / Cr. Service Revenue $2,000
After adjustment: Unearned Revenue = $4,000 (liability for remaining 4 months); Revenue = $2,000

**3. Accrued Revenues (not yet received)**
Revenue has been earned but NOT yet recorded or collected.
Adjusting entry: Dr. Receivable (asset) / Cr. Revenue

Example: On Dec 31, $500 of interest has been earned on a note receivable but will not be collected until Jan 15.
Adjusting entry: Dr. Interest Receivable $500 / Cr. Interest Revenue $500

**4. Accrued Expenses (not yet paid)**
Expense has been incurred but NOT yet recorded or paid.
Adjusting entry: Dr. Expense / Cr. Payable (liability)

Example: Employees have earned $3,000 in wages for Dec 29-31, but payday is Jan 5.
Adjusting entry: Dr. Wages Expense $3,000 / Cr. Wages Payable $3,000

### Depreciation as an Adjusting Entry
Each period, allocate a portion of the asset's cost to expense:
Dr. Depreciation Expense / Cr. Accumulated Depreciation

Accumulated Depreciation is a contra-asset (reduces the book value of the related asset on the balance sheet but keeps the original cost visible).

### Key Rule: Adjusting Entries Never Involve Cash
If an adjusting entry involves the Cash account, something is wrong. Cash transactions should have already been recorded. Adjusting entries correct the allocation of amounts between periods.

### Process
1. Analyze each account to determine if an adjustment is needed
2. Calculate the correct amount for the adjustment
3. Record the adjusting journal entry
4. Post to the ledger
5. Prepare the adjusted trial balance

## Examples

### Example 1: Supplies
Purchased $2,400 of office supplies during the year. A physical count on Dec 31 shows $800 still on hand.
Supplies used = $2,400 - $800 = $1,600
Adjusting entry: Dr. Supplies Expense $1,600 / Cr. Supplies $1,600

### Example 2: Accrued Interest Expense
A company borrowed $100,000 at 6% annual interest on Oct 1. Interest is payable on April 1.
By Dec 31 (3 months), interest has accrued: $100,000 x 6% x 3/12 = $1,500
Adjusting entry: Dr. Interest Expense $1,500 / Cr. Interest Payable $1,500

### Example 3: Multiple Adjustments Impact
Before adjustments: Net income = $50,000
Adjustments: (1) Unrecorded depreciation $5,000, (2) Earned portion of unearned revenue $3,000, (3) Accrued wages $2,000, (4) Used portion of prepaid rent $4,000
Adjusted net income = $50,000 - $5,000 + $3,000 - $2,000 - $4,000 = $42,000
Adjustments decreased net income by $8,000 because expenses exceeded the newly recognized revenue.

## Relationships
- Adjusting entries enforce the matching and revenue recognition principles (Ch. 6)
- They correct the trial balance (Ch. 2) before financial statements are prepared
- Depreciation adjustments affect the balance sheet (Ch. 4) and income statement (Ch. 3)
- Accrued revenues/expenses create receivables/payables on the balance sheet
- The adjusted trial balance feeds directly into financial statement preparation
- Closing entries (Ch. 8) follow adjusting entries in the accounting cycle

## Common Misconceptions
- **Adjusting entries involve cash**: Never. If cash is involved, it is a routine transaction, not an adjusting entry.
- **Adjusting entries are optional**: They are required. Without them, financial statements do not comply with GAAP and will mistate revenues, expenses, assets, and liabilities.
- **Unearned revenue is revenue**: Until the performance obligation is fulfilled, it is a liability. The adjusting entry converts the earned portion to revenue.
- **Prepaid expenses are expenses**: Until consumed, they are assets. The adjusting entry transfers the used portion to expense.
- **Depreciation means the asset lost value**: Depreciation is a cost allocation method, not a measure of value decline. The asset may be worth more or less than its book value.

## Practice Angles
- Identify which type of adjusting entry is needed for a given scenario
- Calculate the correct adjustment amount (especially for partial periods)
- Record all four types of adjusting entries
- Prepare an adjusted trial balance
- Determine the impact of adjusting entries on net income
- Explain what would be wrong with financial statements if a specific adjustment were omitted
