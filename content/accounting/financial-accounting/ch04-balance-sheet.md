# The Balance Sheet

## Overview
The balance sheet (also called the statement of financial position) presents a snapshot of a company's financial position at a specific point in time. It lists what the company owns (assets), what it owes (liabilities), and the residual interest of owners (stockholders' equity). The accounting equation -- Assets = Liabilities + Stockholders' Equity -- is literally the structure of this statement.

A classified balance sheet organizes items by liquidity (how quickly they can be converted to cash), separating current from non-current items. This classification helps users assess the firm's short-term liquidity and long-term solvency.

## Key Terms
- **Classified Balance Sheet**: A balance sheet that separates assets and liabilities into current and non-current categories.
- **Current Assets**: Assets expected to be converted to cash, sold, or consumed within one year or the operating cycle, whichever is longer. Listed in order of liquidity.
- **Cash and Cash Equivalents**: Currency, bank deposits, and short-term investments maturing within 90 days (Treasury bills, money market funds).
- **Accounts Receivable**: Amounts owed to the company by customers for goods or services sold on credit. Reported net of the allowance for doubtful accounts.
- **Allowance for Doubtful Accounts**: A contra-asset that estimates the portion of accounts receivable that will not be collected. Net AR = Gross AR - Allowance.
- **Inventory**: Goods held for sale in the normal course of business. For merchandisers: finished goods. For manufacturers: raw materials + work-in-process + finished goods.
- **Prepaid Expenses**: Amounts paid in advance for future benefits (prepaid rent, prepaid insurance). An asset because the benefit has not yet been used.
- **Property, Plant, and Equipment (PP&E)**: Long-lived tangible assets used in operations: land, buildings, machinery, vehicles, furniture. Reported at cost minus accumulated depreciation.
- **Accumulated Depreciation**: A contra-asset showing the total depreciation charged against an asset since purchase. Net book value = Cost - Accumulated Depreciation.
- **Intangible Assets**: Long-lived assets without physical substance: patents, copyrights, trademarks, goodwill, franchise rights.
- **Goodwill**: The excess of the purchase price over the fair value of identifiable net assets acquired in a business combination. Only recognized through acquisition, not internally generated.
- **Accounts Payable**: Amounts owed to suppliers for goods or services purchased on credit.
- **Accrued Liabilities**: Expenses that have been incurred but not yet paid (wages payable, interest payable, taxes payable).
- **Unearned Revenue (Deferred Revenue)**: Cash received before services are performed or goods delivered. A liability because the company owes a future performance obligation.
- **Notes Payable**: Written promises to pay a specified amount on a specific date. Can be short-term or long-term.
- **Bonds Payable**: Long-term debt securities issued to raise large amounts of capital.
- **Common Stock**: The par value of shares issued. Represents the legal capital of the corporation.
- **Additional Paid-In Capital (APIC)**: The amount received from stockholders in excess of par value. Also called capital surplus or share premium.
- **Treasury Stock**: The company's own stock that has been repurchased. A contra-equity account that reduces total stockholders' equity.
- **Book Value**: Total stockholders' equity, or per-share: (Total Equity - Preferred Equity) / Common Shares Outstanding.

## Core Concepts

### Balance Sheet Structure
```
ASSETS
  Current Assets
    Cash                             $25,000
    Accounts Receivable (net)         40,000
    Inventory                         35,000
    Prepaid Insurance                  5,000
    Total Current Assets                        $105,000
  Non-Current Assets
    Equipment                $80,000
    Less: Accum. Depr.      (20,000)  60,000
    Patent                            10,000
    Total Non-Current Assets                      70,000
  TOTAL ASSETS                                  $175,000

LIABILITIES
  Current Liabilities
    Accounts Payable                  $22,000
    Wages Payable                       8,000
    Unearned Revenue                    5,000
    Current Portion of LT Debt        10,000
    Total Current Liabilities                    $45,000
  Non-Current Liabilities
    Bonds Payable                      50,000
    Total Non-Current Liabilities                 50,000
  TOTAL LIABILITIES                              $95,000

STOCKHOLDERS' EQUITY
    Common Stock ($1 par, 10,000 shares)         $10,000
    Additional Paid-In Capital                    30,000
    Retained Earnings                             40,000
    TOTAL STOCKHOLDERS' EQUITY                   $80,000

TOTAL LIABILITIES + EQUITY                     $175,000
```

### Working Capital
Net Working Capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities = $105,000 - $45,000 = $60,000
This measures the firm's short-term liquidity cushion.

### Limitations of the Balance Sheet
1. **Historical cost**: Most assets are reported at historical cost, not current market value
2. **Omitted assets**: Internally developed brands, workforce talent, and customer relationships are not on the balance sheet
3. **Estimates**: Allowance for doubtful accounts, depreciation, and impairment involve management judgment
4. **Point in time**: Represents only one specific date; may not reflect typical conditions

## Examples

### Example 1: Classifying Items
Classify each: (a) Patent -- Non-current asset (intangible), (b) Wages owed to employees -- Current liability (accrued liability), (c) Inventory -- Current asset, (d) 20-year mortgage -- Split: current portion in current liabilities; remainder in non-current liabilities, (e) Unearned subscription revenue -- Current liability if earned within one year.

### Example 2: Book Value Calculation
Total assets $500,000; Total liabilities $300,000; Preferred stock $50,000; 15,000 common shares outstanding.
Total equity = $200,000
Common equity = $200,000 - $50,000 = $150,000
Book value per share = $150,000 / 15,000 = $10.00

## Relationships
- The balance sheet is the accounting equation visualized as a financial statement
- Retained earnings links the income statement (Ch. 3) to the balance sheet: Ending RE = Beginning RE + Net Income - Dividends
- Cash on the balance sheet must equal the ending balance on the cash flow statement (Ch. 5)
- Current assets and liabilities drive liquidity ratios (FIN 201 Ch. 3)
- PP&E connects to depreciation (Ch. 10)
- Inventory connects to COGS and inventory methods (Ch. 9)
- Adjusting entries (Ch. 7) correct balance sheet accounts before reporting

## Common Misconceptions
- **Assets represent the value of the company**: Book value rarely equals market value. Many valuable items (brand reputation, intellectual property developed internally) are not on the balance sheet.
- **Total assets measures company size**: It depends on the industry. An asset-light tech firm may be worth more than an asset-heavy manufacturer.
- **Stockholders' equity is what you'd get if the company were sold**: Equity is a residual accounting measure, not a market valuation. Liquidation values often differ significantly from book values.
- **Unearned revenue is revenue**: It is a liability. The company has an obligation to deliver goods or services. It only becomes revenue when the obligation is fulfilled.
- **A company with more assets than liabilities is always healthy**: It depends on the quality and liquidity of those assets. A company with most of its assets in obsolete inventory is not healthy.
