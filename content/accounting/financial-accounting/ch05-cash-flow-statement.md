# The Statement of Cash Flows

## Overview
The statement of cash flows explains how a company's cash balance changed during a period by categorizing all cash inflows and outflows into three activities: operating (day-to-day business), investing (buying/selling long-term assets), and financing (borrowing, repaying debt, issuing/repurchasing stock, paying dividends). While the income statement shows profitability on an accrual basis, the cash flow statement reveals actual cash generation -- a critical distinction because profitable companies can and do run out of cash.

The indirect method (most common) starts with net income and adjusts for non-cash items and changes in working capital to arrive at cash from operations. The direct method lists specific cash receipts and payments.

## Key Terms
- **Operating Activities**: Cash flows from the company's primary business operations. Includes cash collected from customers, cash paid to suppliers, cash paid for wages, interest, and taxes.
- **Investing Activities**: Cash flows from buying and selling long-term assets. Includes purchases of PP&E, sales of PP&E, purchases/sales of investments, lending money.
- **Financing Activities**: Cash flows from transactions with owners and creditors. Includes issuing stock, repurchasing stock, borrowing, repaying debt, and paying dividends.
- **Indirect Method**: Starts with net income, then adjusts for (1) non-cash expenses, (2) gains/losses, and (3) changes in current assets and liabilities to compute cash from operations.
- **Direct Method**: Lists specific operating cash receipts (from customers) and payments (to suppliers, employees, etc.). Produces the same cash from operations total as the indirect method.
- **Non-Cash Items**: Expenses that reduce net income but do not require cash (depreciation, amortization). Must be added back under the indirect method.
- **Free Cash Flow (FCF)**: Cash from operations - Capital expenditures. Measures cash available after maintaining/expanding the asset base. Available for debt repayment, dividends, or share repurchases.
- **Capital Expenditures (CapEx)**: Cash spent on purchasing or improving long-term assets. Reported in investing activities.

## Core Concepts

### Indirect Method Format
```
Cash Flows from Operating Activities
  Net Income                                    $61,500
  Adjustments for non-cash items:
    + Depreciation expense                       15,000
    + Loss on sale of equipment                   2,000
    - Gain on sale of investments                (3,000)
  Changes in working capital:
    - Increase in accounts receivable            (5,000)
    - Increase in inventory                      (8,000)
    + Increase in accounts payable                4,000
    + Increase in wages payable                   2,000
    - Decrease in unearned revenue               (1,000)
  Net Cash from Operating Activities                       $67,500

Cash Flows from Investing Activities
    Purchase of equipment                       (40,000)
    Sale of investments                          10,000
  Net Cash from Investing Activities                      ($30,000)

Cash Flows from Financing Activities
    Proceeds from issuing bonds                  50,000
    Repayment of notes payable                  (20,000)
    Dividends paid                              (15,000)
  Net Cash from Financing Activities                       $15,000

Net Increase in Cash                                       $52,500
Beginning Cash Balance                                      25,000
Ending Cash Balance                                        $77,500
```

### Adjustment Rules (Indirect Method)
**Add back to net income:**
- Depreciation and amortization (non-cash expense)
- Losses on sale of assets (non-cash component; actual cash in investing section)
- Decreases in current assets (freed up cash)
- Increases in current liabilities (deferred cash payments)

**Subtract from net income:**
- Gains on sale of assets (non-cash component)
- Increases in current assets (used cash)
- Decreases in current liabilities (used cash to pay)

Memory aid for working capital changes: Current assets move OPPOSITE to cash (increase in AR = less cash collected). Current liabilities move SAME direction as cash (increase in AP = less cash paid).

### Why Depreciation Is Added Back
Depreciation reduces net income but no cash leaves the company. Adding it back does NOT mean depreciation generates cash -- it simply reverses a non-cash charge to get closer to actual cash flow. The real cash outflow occurred when the asset was purchased (reported in investing activities).

### Free Cash Flow
**FCF = Cash from Operations - Capital Expenditures**

Example: Operating cash flow = $67,500, CapEx = $40,000
FCF = $67,500 - $40,000 = $27,500

FCF measures the cash available to pay dividends, repurchase shares, reduce debt, or pursue new investments. It is one of the most important metrics for valuation.

## Examples

### Example 1: Working Capital Changes
Beginning AR = $30,000, Ending AR = $35,000. Change = +$5,000 (increase).
An increase in AR means the company recorded revenue but did not collect all the cash. Subtract $5,000 from net income.

Beginning AP = $20,000, Ending AP = $24,000. Change = +$4,000 (increase).
An increase in AP means the company incurred expenses but has not yet paid. Add $4,000 to net income.

### Example 2: Profitable but Cash-Poor
Net Income: $100,000
Depreciation: $10,000
Increase in AR: ($80,000) -- customers owe but have not paid
Increase in Inventory: ($50,000) -- stocked up heavily
Cash from Operations = 100K + 10K - 80K - 50K = ($20,000) NEGATIVE
Despite $100K profit, the company used $20K of cash from operations. This happens with rapid growth.

## Relationships
- The cash flow statement reconciles the income statement (accrual) to actual cash changes
- Net income (Ch. 3) is the starting point for the indirect method
- Balance sheet changes (Ch. 4) drive the working capital adjustments
- Free cash flow is used in firm valuation models (FIN 201 Ch. 6)
- CapEx connects to depreciation (Ch. 10) and capital budgeting (FIN 201 Ch. 7)

## Common Misconceptions
- **Positive net income means positive cash flow**: A profitable company can have negative operating cash flow if receivables and inventory are growing faster than sales.
- **Depreciation generates cash**: It does not. Adding it back merely reverses a non-cash charge. The cash impact occurred when the asset was purchased.
- **Interest paid is a financing activity**: Under U.S. GAAP, interest paid is an OPERATING activity (though it is a financing activity under IFRS).
- **Dividends received are a financing activity**: Under GAAP, dividends received are an OPERATING activity.
- **All three sections should be positive for a healthy company**: Young, growing companies often have negative investing cash flows (buying assets) and positive financing (raising capital). This is normal.

## Practice Angles
- Prepare a cash flow statement using the indirect method
- Determine whether each item belongs in operating, investing, or financing
- Calculate the adjustments for changes in working capital accounts
- Explain why net income differs from cash from operations
- Calculate free cash flow and interpret its meaning
- Identify warning signs (e.g., net income positive but operating cash flow negative)
