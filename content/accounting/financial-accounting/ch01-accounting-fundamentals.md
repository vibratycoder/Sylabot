# Accounting Fundamentals

## Overview
Accounting is the systematic process of recording, classifying, summarizing, and reporting financial transactions to provide useful information for decision-making. The entire system is built on the accounting equation: Assets = Liabilities + Stockholders' Equity. Every transaction must maintain this equation in balance.

The double-entry bookkeeping system requires that every transaction affect at least two accounts and that total debits always equal total credits. Understanding what "debit" and "credit" mean for different account types is the foundation of all accounting work.

## Key Terms
- **Accounting**: The information system that identifies, records, and communicates the economic events of an organization to interested users.
- **Accounting Equation**: Assets = Liabilities + Stockholders' Equity. The fundamental equation underlying all of accounting. It must always balance.
- **Assets**: Economic resources owned or controlled by an entity that are expected to provide future economic benefits. Examples: cash, accounts receivable, inventory, equipment, buildings.
- **Liabilities**: Obligations owed to outsiders (creditors) that will require the future payment of assets or performance of services. Examples: accounts payable, notes payable, wages payable, bonds payable.
- **Stockholders' Equity (Owners' Equity)**: The residual interest in the assets of an entity after deducting liabilities. Equity = Assets - Liabilities. Includes contributed capital (stock) and retained earnings.
- **Revenue**: Increases in assets or decreases in liabilities from delivering goods or services -- the entity's main business activities. Revenue increases equity.
- **Expenses**: Decreases in assets or increases in liabilities from delivering goods or services. Expenses decrease equity (through retained earnings).
- **Dividends**: Distributions of earnings to stockholders. Dividends decrease retained earnings (equity) but are NOT expenses.
- **Retained Earnings**: The cumulative net income of a company minus cumulative dividends paid. Part of stockholders' equity.
- **Double-Entry Bookkeeping**: Every transaction is recorded with at least one debit and one credit, and total debits must equal total credits. This ensures the accounting equation stays balanced.
- **Debit (Dr.)**: An entry on the LEFT side of an account. Increases assets, expenses, and dividends. Decreases liabilities, equity, and revenue.
- **Credit (Cr.)**: An entry on the RIGHT side of an account. Increases liabilities, equity, and revenue. Decreases assets, expenses, and dividends.
- **Normal Balance**: The side (debit or credit) on which increases to an account are recorded. Assets, expenses, dividends have normal debit balances. Liabilities, equity, revenue have normal credit balances.
- **Account**: A record of increases and decreases in a specific asset, liability, equity, revenue, or expense item.
- **Chart of Accounts**: A numbered list of all accounts used by a company, organized by category (assets, liabilities, equity, revenue, expenses).
- **T-Account**: A simplified representation of an account shaped like the letter T. Debits on the left, credits on the right.
- **Trial Balance**: A list of all accounts and their balances at a point in time. Total debits must equal total credits. Verifies mathematical accuracy but does not guarantee correctness of entries.

## Core Concepts

### The Expanded Accounting Equation
Assets = Liabilities + Common Stock + Retained Earnings
Since Retained Earnings = Beginning RE + Revenue - Expenses - Dividends:
Assets = Liabilities + Common Stock + Beginning RE + Revenue - Expenses - Dividends

This shows why revenue credits (increases equity) and expenses debits (decreases equity) make sense.

### Debit and Credit Rules

| Account Type | Increase | Decrease | Normal Balance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Assets | Debit | Credit | Debit |
| Liabilities | Credit | Debit | Credit |
| Stockholders' Equity | Credit | Debit | Credit |
| Revenue | Credit | Debit | Credit |
| Expenses | Debit | Credit | Debit |
| Dividends | Debit | Credit | Debit |

Memory aid (the "DEALER" acronym): Dividends, Expenses, Assets are increased by Debits (left side). Liabilities, Equity, Revenue are increased by Credits (right side).

### Transaction Analysis Process
For every transaction:
1. Identify which accounts are affected
2. Determine if each account increases or decreases
3. Determine if the change is a debit or credit
4. Verify that total debits = total credits

Example: Company purchases equipment for $10,000 cash.
- Equipment (asset) increases -> Debit Equipment $10,000
- Cash (asset) decreases -> Credit Cash $10,000
- Debits ($10,000) = Credits ($10,000). Equation stays balanced.

### Common Transaction Types
1. **Issue stock for cash**: Debit Cash, Credit Common Stock
2. **Borrow money**: Debit Cash, Credit Notes Payable
3. **Purchase supplies on account**: Debit Supplies, Credit Accounts Payable
4. **Perform services for cash**: Debit Cash, Credit Service Revenue
5. **Perform services on account**: Debit Accounts Receivable, Credit Service Revenue
6. **Pay salaries**: Debit Salaries Expense, Credit Cash
7. **Pay accounts payable**: Debit Accounts Payable, Credit Cash
8. **Receive cash on account**: Debit Cash, Credit Accounts Receivable
9. **Declare dividends**: Debit Dividends, Credit Dividends Payable
10. **Pay dividends**: Debit Dividends Payable, Credit Cash

### Account Classification
**Current Assets** (convertible to cash within one year): Cash, Accounts Receivable, Inventory, Prepaid Expenses, Short-term Investments.
**Non-Current Assets**: Property/Plant/Equipment, Intangible Assets (patents, goodwill), Long-term Investments.
**Current Liabilities** (due within one year): Accounts Payable, Wages Payable, Unearned Revenue, Short-term Notes Payable, Current Portion of Long-term Debt.
**Non-Current Liabilities**: Long-term Notes Payable, Bonds Payable, Lease Obligations.
**Stockholders' Equity**: Common Stock, Additional Paid-in Capital, Retained Earnings, Treasury Stock (contra).

## Examples

### Example 1: Starting a Business
Jan 1: Sarah invests $50,000 cash to start a company and receives common stock.
Debit Cash $50,000 | Credit Common Stock $50,000
Assets (+$50K cash) = Liabilities ($0) + Equity (+$50K stock). Balanced.

### Example 2: Revenue on Account
Jan 15: Company provides $3,000 of consulting services. Client will pay in 30 days.
Debit Accounts Receivable $3,000 | Credit Service Revenue $3,000
Revenue increases retained earnings (equity). AR is an asset. Equation balanced.

### Example 3: Multiple-Account Transaction
Jan 20: Company purchases a $20,000 delivery truck, paying $5,000 cash and signing a note for $15,000.
Debit Vehicle $20,000 | Credit Cash $5,000, Credit Notes Payable $15,000
Assets increase by net $15,000 (truck +20K, cash -5K). Liabilities increase $15,000. Balanced.

## Relationships
- The accounting equation is the foundation for all financial statements
- Debits/credits flow into journal entries (Ch. 2) and T-accounts (Ch. 2)
- Revenue and expense accounts flow into the income statement (Ch. 3)
- Asset, liability, and equity accounts form the balance sheet (Ch. 4)
- The closing process (Ch. 8) resets revenue/expense accounts to zero and updates retained earnings
- GAAP (Ch. 6) provides the rules governing how transactions are recorded

## Common Misconceptions
- **Debit means bad, credit means good**: In accounting, debit simply means left side and credit means right side. An expense debit is a "cost," but an asset debit is an "increase in resources." Context matters.
- **Cash is always involved in every transaction**: Many transactions do not involve cash -- purchasing on account, recognizing revenue earned on credit, adjusting entries for accruals.
- **Revenue equals cash received**: Revenue is recognized when earned, not necessarily when cash is received (accrual accounting). Cash received in advance is a liability (unearned revenue).
- **Dividends are expenses**: Dividends are NOT expenses. They are distributions of profits to owners and reduce retained earnings directly, not through the income statement.
- **A balanced trial balance means all entries are correct**: The trial balance only checks that debits = credits. Errors like recording to the wrong account, omitting a transaction, or recording the wrong amount on both sides will not be detected.

- Prepare a trial balance from a list of account balances
- Classify accounts as current/non-current, asset/liability/equity
- Trace how transactions flow through the accounting equation
