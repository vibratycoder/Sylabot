# Closing Entries and the Accounting Cycle

## Overview
Closing entries are the final step in the accounting cycle before starting the next period. They transfer the balances of all temporary accounts (revenues, expenses, and dividends) to the permanent account Retained Earnings. This process resets temporary accounts to zero so they can accumulate fresh data for the next period. After closing, only permanent accounts (assets, liabilities, equity) remain with balances.

The complete accounting cycle is the systematic series of steps performed each period to analyze, record, adjust, report, and close the accounts.

## Key Terms
- **Temporary Accounts**: Accounts that are closed at the end of each period. Include all revenues, expenses, gains, losses, and dividends. Their balances accumulate only for the current period.
- **Permanent Accounts**: Accounts that carry their balances forward from one period to the next. Include all assets, liabilities, and equity accounts (except dividends). Also called real accounts.
- **Closing Entries**: Journal entries that (1) close revenue accounts to Income Summary, (2) close expense accounts to Income Summary, (3) close Income Summary to Retained Earnings, and (4) close Dividends to Retained Earnings.
- **Income Summary**: A temporary holding account used during closing. Revenues are transferred in (credit), expenses are transferred in (debit), and the net balance (net income or loss) is transferred to Retained Earnings.
- **Post-Closing Trial Balance**: A trial balance prepared after closing entries. Should contain only permanent accounts, all with their correct beginning-of-next-period balances.
- **Accounting Cycle**: The complete sequence of steps from transaction analysis through closing: (1) Analyze transactions, (2) Journalize, (3) Post to ledger, (4) Prepare unadjusted trial balance, (5) Adjusting entries, (6) Adjusted trial balance, (7) Prepare financial statements, (8) Closing entries, (9) Post-closing trial balance.
- **Reversing Entries**: Optional entries made on the first day of the new period to reverse certain adjusting entries (typically accruals). Simplifies recording subsequent cash transactions.
- **Fiscal Year**: A 12-month accounting period. Does not have to coincide with the calendar year (e.g., many retailers use Feb 1 - Jan 31).
- **Interim Statements**: Financial statements covering a period shorter than a full fiscal year (monthly, quarterly).

## Core Concepts

### The Four Closing Entries

**Step 1: Close Revenue Accounts**
Debit each revenue account for its balance; credit Income Summary.
```
Dr. Service Revenue           80,000
Dr. Interest Revenue           2,000
    Cr. Income Summary                    82,000
```
This zeros out all revenue accounts.

**Step 2: Close Expense Accounts**
Credit each expense account for its balance; debit Income Summary.
```
Dr. Income Summary            60,000
    Cr. Salaries Expense                  30,000
    Cr. Rent Expense                      12,000
    Cr. Depreciation Expense               8,000
    Cr. Utilities Expense                  5,000
    Cr. Supplies Expense                   3,000
    Cr. Insurance Expense                  2,000
```
This zeros out all expense accounts.

**Step 3: Close Income Summary to Retained Earnings**
Income Summary now has a credit balance of $82,000 - $60,000 = $22,000 (net income).
```
Dr. Income Summary            22,000
    Cr. Retained Earnings                 22,000
```
(If there were a net loss, the entry would be reversed: Dr. Retained Earnings / Cr. Income Summary.)

**Step 4: Close Dividends to Retained Earnings**
If dividends of $8,000 were declared:
```
Dr. Retained Earnings          8,000
    Cr. Dividends                          8,000
```

After closing: Retained Earnings increased by $22,000 (net income) and decreased by $8,000 (dividends) = net increase of $14,000.

### The Complete Accounting Cycle
1. **Analyze** transactions from source documents
2. **Journalize** in the general journal
3. **Post** to the general ledger (T-accounts)
4. **Prepare unadjusted trial balance** (debits = credits check)
5. **Record and post adjusting entries** (end of period)
6. **Prepare adjusted trial balance**
7. **Prepare financial statements** (income statement, retained earnings statement, balance sheet, cash flow statement)
8. **Record and post closing entries**
9. **Prepare post-closing trial balance** (only permanent accounts remain)

### Why Closing Is Necessary
Without closing, revenue and expense accounts would accumulate balances across periods, making it impossible to determine current-period performance. Closing ensures each period starts fresh while preserving the cumulative effect in Retained Earnings.

## Examples

### Example 1: Effect on Retained Earnings
Beginning Retained Earnings: $100,000
Net Income: $35,000 (closed from Income Summary)
Dividends: $10,000 (closed directly)
Ending Retained Earnings: $100,000 + $35,000 - $10,000 = $125,000

### Example 2: Post-Closing Trial Balance
After closing, these accounts should have ZERO balances: all revenues, all expenses, dividends, income summary.
Remaining accounts with balances: Cash, AR, Inventory, Equipment, Accumulated Depreciation, AP, Notes Payable, Common Stock, Retained Earnings.

## Relationships
- Closing entries follow adjusting entries (Ch. 7) in the accounting cycle
- Income Summary captures net income, which updates Retained Earnings on the balance sheet (Ch. 4)
- Revenue and expense recognition (Ch. 3, 6) determine what gets closed
- The post-closing trial balance provides beginning balances for the next period
- The entire cycle repeats each fiscal period

## Common Misconceptions
- **Closing entries change net income**: They do not. Net income was determined by the adjusted trial balance. Closing merely transfers it to Retained Earnings.
- **Income Summary is a permanent account**: It is temporary. It exists only during the closing process and has a zero balance after closing.
- **Dividends are closed through Income Summary**: Dividends bypass Income Summary and are closed directly to Retained Earnings because dividends are not an expense.
- **Closing entries affect cash**: Like adjusting entries, closing entries never involve the Cash account. They only move balances between temporary and permanent accounts.
- **You can skip closing entries**: Technically the math would still work, but financial statements for the next period would incorrectly include prior-period revenues and expenses.
