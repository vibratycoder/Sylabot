# The Income Statement

## Overview
The income statement (also called the profit and loss statement or statement of operations) reports a company's financial performance over a specific period by showing revenues earned and expenses incurred. The "bottom line" -- net income -- tells whether the company was profitable during the period. The income statement is the most closely watched financial statement by investors and analysts because it reveals whether the company's core operations are generating profit.

There are two formats: the single-step income statement (one subtraction: total revenues minus total expenses) and the multi-step income statement (multiple subtotals revealing gross profit, operating income, and net income separately). The multi-step format provides more analytical value.

## Key Terms
- **Revenue (Sales Revenue)**: The amount earned from the primary business activity of selling goods or services. Recognized when earned under accrual accounting, not necessarily when cash is received.
- **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)**: The direct cost of inventory sold during the period. For merchandisers: COGS = Beginning Inventory + Purchases - Ending Inventory. For manufacturers: includes raw materials, direct labor, and manufacturing overhead.
- **Gross Profit (Gross Margin)**: Revenue - COGS. The profit from selling goods before operating expenses are deducted.
- **Operating Expenses**: Costs incurred in running the business that are not directly tied to producing goods. Includes selling expenses (advertising, sales commissions) and general & administrative expenses (rent, utilities, salaries, depreciation).
- **Operating Income (EBIT)**: Gross Profit - Operating Expenses. Profit from core business operations before interest and taxes.
- **Non-Operating Items**: Revenues and expenses not related to core operations, such as interest revenue, interest expense, gains/losses on sale of assets.
- **Income Before Taxes**: Operating Income +/- Non-Operating Items.
- **Income Tax Expense**: The tax owed on income before taxes.
- **Net Income**: The final profit after all revenues, expenses, and taxes. Net Income = Total Revenues - Total Expenses.
- **Earnings Per Share (EPS)**: Net Income / Weighted Average Shares Outstanding. Required to be disclosed on the income statement for public companies.
- **Single-Step Income Statement**: Groups all revenues together and all expenses together, then subtracts to get net income in one step.
- **Multi-Step Income Statement**: Shows multiple intermediate subtotals (gross profit, operating income) that provide more detail about different levels of profitability.
- **Comprehensive Income**: Net income plus other comprehensive income (OCI) items such as unrealized gains/losses on certain investments, foreign currency translation adjustments, and pension adjustments. Reported separately from the income statement.

## Core Concepts

### Multi-Step Income Statement Structure
```
Revenue (Net Sales)                              $500,000
- Cost of Goods Sold                             (300,000)
= Gross Profit                                    200,000
- Operating Expenses
    Selling Expenses                   (50,000)
    G&A Expenses                       (60,000)   (110,000)
= Operating Income (EBIT)                          90,000
+/- Non-Operating Items
    Interest Revenue                     2,000
    Interest Expense                   (10,000)     (8,000)
= Income Before Taxes                              82,000
- Income Tax Expense (25%)                        (20,500)
= Net Income                                      $61,500

EPS = $61,500 / 10,000 shares = $6.15
```

### Key Margin Calculations
- **Gross Profit Margin** = Gross Profit / Revenue = $200K / $500K = 40%
- **Operating Profit Margin** = Operating Income / Revenue = $90K / $500K = 18%
- **Net Profit Margin** = Net Income / Revenue = $61.5K / $500K = 12.3%

Each margin tells a different story: gross margin reflects pricing power and production efficiency; operating margin reflects operational efficiency; net margin reflects overall profitability after all costs.

### Revenue Recognition Principle
Revenue is recognized when:
1. The performance obligation is satisfied (goods delivered or service performed)
2. The amount can be reliably measured
3. Collection is reasonably assured

Under ASC 606 (current GAAP): identify the contract, identify performance obligations, determine the transaction price, allocate the price, and recognize revenue when each obligation is satisfied.

### Matching Principle
Expenses should be recognized in the same period as the revenues they help generate. If a company sells a product in March, the cost of that product (COGS) should be recognized in March, even if the company paid for the inventory in January.

## Examples

### Example 1: Preparing an Income Statement
A retail company has: Sales $800,000; COGS $480,000; Selling expenses $80,000; Admin expenses $100,000; Interest expense $15,000; Tax rate 30%.
Gross Profit = 800K - 480K = $320,000
Operating Income = 320K - 80K - 100K = $140,000
Income before tax = 140K - 15K = $125,000
Tax = 125K x 0.30 = $37,500
Net Income = $87,500

### Example 2: Analyzing Margin Trends
Year 1: Revenue $1M, Gross Profit $400K (40%), Net Income $100K (10%)
Year 2: Revenue $1.2M, Gross Profit $420K (35%), Net Income $96K (8%)
Revenue grew 20% but gross margin declined from 40% to 35% -- the company is either cutting prices or facing rising costs. Net income actually fell despite higher revenue, signaling a problem.

## Common Misconceptions
- **Revenue equals cash collected**: Under accrual accounting, revenue is recognized when earned, which may be before or after cash is received.
- **A company with high revenue is automatically profitable**: Revenue means nothing without considering expenses. A company can have $1 billion in revenue and still lose money.
- **COGS includes all costs**: COGS only includes direct costs of goods sold. Operating expenses (rent, marketing, admin salaries) are separate.
- **Net income is cash profit**: Net income includes non-cash items (depreciation, accruals). A profitable company can have negative cash flow.
- **Gross margin is comparable across all industries**: Margins vary enormously by industry. Software companies might have 80% gross margins; grocery stores might have 25%.

- Calculate gross profit margin, operating margin, and net profit margin
- Compare margins across periods to identify improving or declining performance
- Distinguish between operating and non-operating items
- Apply revenue recognition criteria to determine when revenue should be recorded
- Calculate earnings per share
