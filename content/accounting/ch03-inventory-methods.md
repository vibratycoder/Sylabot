# Inventory Methods and Cost of Goods Sold

## Overview
Inventory is one of the most significant assets for merchandising and manufacturing companies. The method used to value inventory directly affects Cost of Goods Sold (COGS), gross profit, net income, and taxes. The three main methods -- FIFO, LIFO, and weighted average -- produce different results when prices are changing. Understanding these differences is critical for financial analysis and tax planning.

## Key Terms
- **Inventory**: Goods held for sale to customers. A current asset on the balance sheet.
- **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)**: The cost of inventory sold during the period. COGS = Beginning Inventory + Purchases - Ending Inventory.
- **FIFO (First-In, First-Out)**: Assumes the oldest inventory is sold first. Ending inventory reflects most recent (higher) costs. Lower COGS, higher net income when prices rise.
- **LIFO (Last-In, First-Out)**: Assumes the newest inventory is sold first. Ending inventory reflects oldest (lower) costs. Higher COGS, lower net income when prices rise (tax advantage).
- **Weighted Average Cost**: Average cost per unit = Total cost of goods available / Total units available. Applied to both COGS and ending inventory.
- **Specific Identification**: Tracks the actual cost of each specific item sold. Used for unique, high-value items (cars, art, jewelry).
- **Perpetual Inventory System**: Continuously updates inventory records with each purchase and sale. Real-time tracking.
- **Periodic Inventory System**: Updates inventory only at period end through physical count.
- **Lower of Cost or Market (LCM)**: Inventory reported at whichever is lower: its cost or current market value. Conservatism principle.
- **Inventory Turnover**: COGS / Average Inventory. How many times inventory is sold and replaced per year.

## Core Concepts

### FIFO vs LIFO in Rising Prices
When prices are rising: FIFO produces lower COGS (old, cheap units sold first) -> higher gross profit -> higher taxes. LIFO produces higher COGS (new, expensive units sold first) -> lower gross profit -> lower taxes. LIFO provides a tax advantage but lower reported income. Most international standards (IFRS) prohibit LIFO.

### Impact on Financial Statements
| Method | COGS | Net Income | Ending Inventory | Taxes |
|--------|------|-----------|-----------------|-------|
| FIFO (rising prices) | Lower | Higher | Higher | Higher |
| LIFO (rising prices) | Higher | Lower | Lower | Lower |
| Weighted Avg | Middle | Middle | Middle | Middle |

### LIFO Reserve
The difference between FIFO and LIFO inventory values. Companies using LIFO must disclose the LIFO reserve so analysts can convert to FIFO for comparisons.

## Examples

### Calculating COGS Under Each Method
Beginning inventory: 100 units @ $10. Purchase 1: 200 units @ $12. Purchase 2: 150 units @ $14. Sold: 300 units.
- **FIFO**: Sell 100 @ $10 + 200 @ $12 = $3,400 COGS. Ending inventory: 150 @ $14 = $2,100.
- **LIFO**: Sell 150 @ $14 + 150 @ $12 = $3,900 COGS. Ending inventory: 100 @ $10 + 50 @ $12 = $1,600.
- **Weighted Avg**: Average = ($1,000 + $2,400 + $2,100) / 450 = $12.22/unit. COGS = 300 x $12.22 = $3,667.

## Relationships
- Rising prices + FIFO -> Higher income, higher taxes
- Rising prices + LIFO -> Lower income, lower taxes (tax advantage)
- Higher inventory turnover -> More efficient inventory management
- LCM rule -> Write down inventory if market value drops below cost (conservatism)

## Common Misconceptions
- **"FIFO/LIFO refers to physical flow of goods"**: These are cost flow assumptions for accounting, not physical inventory movement. A grocery store uses FIFO physically (sell oldest first) but could use LIFO for accounting.
- **"LIFO is always better because of tax savings"**: LIFO reduces taxes but also reduces reported income, which can affect stock price, borrowing capacity, and management bonuses.
- **"Inventory methods don't matter much"**: For companies with large inventories and volatile prices (oil companies, retailers), the method choice can swing net income by millions.

## Practice Angles
- Calculate: COGS and ending inventory under FIFO, LIFO, and weighted average
- Compare: how the same transactions produce different net income under each method
- Explain: why LIFO provides a tax advantage when prices rise
- Analyze: which method should a company choose and why? (consider taxes, reporting, industry)
- Apply: the LCM rule when inventory value declines
