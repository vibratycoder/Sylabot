# Management Fundamentals

## Overview
Management is the process of planning, organizing, leading, and controlling resources to achieve organizational goals efficiently and effectively. Managers operate at different levels (top, middle, first-line) and perform different roles (interpersonal, informational, decisional). Effective management balances efficiency (doing things right) with effectiveness (doing the right things).

## Key Terms
- **Management**: The process of coordinating work activities so they are completed efficiently and effectively with and through other people.
- **Planning**: Setting goals and deciding how to achieve them. Includes strategic, tactical, and operational plans.
- **Organizing**: Arranging resources and tasks to implement the plan. Includes organizational structure, job design, and delegation.
- **Leading**: Motivating, directing, and influencing people to work toward organizational goals. Includes communication, motivation, and leadership styles.
- **Controlling**: Monitoring performance, comparing to goals, and taking corrective action.
- **Efficiency**: Achieving maximum output with minimum input ("doing things right").
- **Effectiveness**: Achieving organizational goals ("doing the right things").
- **Strategic Planning**: Long-term (3-5 years), sets direction for the entire organization. Done by top management.
- **Mintzberg's Managerial Roles**: Interpersonal (figurehead, leader, liaison), Informational (monitor, disseminator, spokesperson), Decisional (entrepreneur, disturbance handler, resource allocator, negotiator).
- **Stakeholders**: All parties affected by the organization: employees, customers, shareholders, community, suppliers, government.
- **Organizational Structure**: How tasks are divided, grouped, and coordinated. Includes hierarchy, span of control, centralization, departmentalization.
- **Span of Control**: The number of employees a manager directly supervises. Wide span = flat structure; narrow span = tall structure.

## Core Concepts

### Four Functions of Management
Planning -> Organizing -> Leading -> Controlling. These are iterative, not strictly sequential. Controlling feeds back into planning (adjust plans based on results).

### Levels of Management
Top management (CEO, VP): strategic decisions, long-term vision. Middle management (department heads): implement strategies, coordinate. First-line management (supervisors): daily operations, direct employee supervision.

### Classical vs Behavioral vs Modern Approaches
Classical (Taylor, Fayol, Weber): scientific management, efficiency, bureaucracy. Behavioral (Mayo, Maslow, McGregor): human relations, motivation, group dynamics. Modern: systems theory (organization as interconnected parts), contingency theory (no one best way -- depends on the situation).

## Examples

### Planning at Amazon
Strategic plan: become "the world's most customer-centric company." Tactical plans: expand Prime, build AWS, develop logistics network. Operational plans: daily warehouse operations, delivery routing, customer service protocols. All levels of planning align with the strategic vision.

### Organizing a Startup vs Corporation
Startup: flat structure, wide span of control, informal communication, everyone wears multiple hats. Corporation: tall hierarchy, narrow spans, formal departments, specialized roles. Each structure fits its context.

## Relationships
- Planning -> Goals and strategies -> Guide organizing, leading, controlling
- Organizing -> Structure and resources -> Enable leading and controlling
- Leading -> Motivation and direction -> Drive performance
- Controlling -> Feedback -> Informs replanning
- Efficiency + Effectiveness -> Organizational success

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Managers and leaders are the same"**: Management focuses on planning, organizing, controlling. Leadership focuses on vision, inspiration, change. Good managers are often good leaders, but the roles are distinct.
- **"There's one best way to manage"**: Contingency theory shows the best approach depends on the situation, industry, culture, and people involved.
- **"Management is just telling people what to do"**: Modern management emphasizes coaching, empowering, and facilitating rather than command-and-control.
