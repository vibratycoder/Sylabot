# GDP and Measuring the Economy

## Overview
Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is the most widely used measure of a nation's economic output. It represents the total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given period. GDP can be measured three ways (expenditure, income, production), and the distinction between nominal and real GDP is critical for understanding true economic growth vs inflation.

## Key Terms
- **Gross Domestic Product (GDP)**: The total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given period (usually a year or quarter).
- **Final Goods**: Goods sold to the end user. Intermediate goods (used in production of other goods) are excluded to avoid double counting.
- **Nominal GDP**: GDP measured at current prices. Increases can reflect price increases (inflation), output increases, or both.
- **Real GDP**: GDP adjusted for inflation using a base year's prices. Measures actual output changes.
- **GDP Deflator**: (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) x 100. Measures the overall price level of all goods in GDP.
- **Expenditure Approach**: GDP = C + I + G + NX (Consumption + Investment + Government spending + Net Exports).
- **Consumption (C)**: Household spending on goods and services. The largest component (~68% of US GDP).
- **Investment (I)**: Business spending on capital goods, inventory changes, and new residential construction. NOT financial investment (stocks/bonds).
- **Government Spending (G)**: Government purchases of goods and services. Excludes transfer payments (Social Security, welfare).
- **Net Exports (NX)**: Exports minus Imports. Negative when a country imports more than it exports (trade deficit).
- **Business Cycle**: The recurring pattern of expansion (growth) and contraction (recession) in economic activity.
- **Recession**: A significant decline in economic activity lasting more than a few months. Commonly defined as two consecutive quarters of declining real GDP.
- **Expansion**: A period of growing real GDP, rising employment, and increasing income.
- **Per Capita GDP**: GDP divided by population. A better measure of average living standards than total GDP.
- **GNP (Gross National Product)**: Total output produced by a country's citizens regardless of location. GDP counts output within borders.

## Core Concepts

### What GDP Includes and Excludes
GDP includes: all market transactions for final goods and services, imputed rent for homeowners, government services valued at cost. GDP excludes: intermediate goods, used goods (already counted when new), transfer payments, household production, underground/illegal economy, leisure, environmental quality.

### Expenditure Approach (C + I + G + NX)
The most common method. Consumption is the largest component. Investment includes business equipment, new construction, and inventory changes (not stocks/bonds). Government spending excludes transfer payments. Net exports are typically negative for the US (trade deficit).

### Nominal vs Real GDP
Nominal GDP can increase just because prices went up, not because more stuff was produced. Real GDP strips out inflation to show actual output growth. If nominal GDP rises 5% but prices rose 3%, real GDP grew only about 2%.

## Examples

### US GDP Components (Approximate)
- Consumption: 68% (services dominate: healthcare, housing, food services)
- Investment: 18% (business equipment, software, residential construction)
- Government: 17% (federal defense, state/local education, infrastructure)
- Net Exports: -3% (trade deficit -- imports exceed exports)

### Why GDP is Imperfect
China's GDP may overstate well-being because it doesn't account for severe air pollution. Bhutan proposed "Gross National Happiness" as an alternative. A country with high GDP but extreme inequality may have many citizens living in poverty despite strong aggregate numbers.

## Relationships
- Real GDP growth -> Economic expansion -> Lower unemployment
- Real GDP decline -> Recession -> Higher unemployment
- Nominal GDP increase -> Could be real growth OR inflation (must decompose)
- GDP per capita -> Better indicator of living standards than total GDP
- Investment spending -> Future productivity growth -> Higher future GDP

## Common Misconceptions
- **"GDP measures well-being"**: GDP measures market output, not happiness, health, leisure, inequality, or environmental quality. It's a useful but incomplete measure.
- **"Investment means buying stocks"**: In GDP accounting, investment means business spending on capital goods and new construction. Financial transactions are not counted.
- **"Government transfer payments are in G"**: Social Security, Medicare, and welfare are transfers, not purchases of goods/services. They're not directly in GDP (though they become C when recipients spend them).
- **"A country with higher GDP is always better off"**: Per capita GDP, distribution, non-market production, and quality of life all matter.
