# International Trade and Finance

## Overview

International trade allows countries to specialize in producing goods where they have a comparative advantage, increasing total world output and benefiting all trading partners. Despite these gains, trade creates winners and losers within each country, leading to political pressure for protectionism (tariffs, quotas, subsidies).

Exchange rates determine the relative price of currencies and affect trade flows. A country's balance of payments records all economic transactions with the rest of the world, divided into the current account (trade in goods/services) and the capital/financial account (investment flows). Exchange rates are determined by supply and demand for currencies in floating-rate systems.

This topic is central to ECO 114 and especially ECO 471 (International Trade) and ECO 367 (Economic Development).

## Key Terms

- **Absolute Advantage**: The ability to produce a good using fewer resources than another country. A country has absolute advantage if it is more productive.
- **Comparative Advantage**: The ability to produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than another country. This is the basis for mutually beneficial trade -- even if one country has absolute advantage in everything, both countries benefit by specializing in their comparative advantage good.
- **Terms of Trade**: The ratio at which two goods are exchanged between countries. Must fall between the two countries' opportunity costs for both to gain from trade.
- **Gains from Trade**: The increase in total output and consumption that results from specialization and trade based on comparative advantage.
- **Tariff**: A tax on imported goods. Raises the domestic price, protects domestic producers, generates government revenue, but reduces consumer welfare and creates deadweight loss.
- **Import Quota**: A numerical limit on the quantity of a good that can be imported. Similar to a tariff but does not generate government revenue.
- **Trade Deficit**: When a country's imports exceed its exports (negative net exports). The US has run a trade deficit since the 1970s.
- **Trade Surplus**: When a country's exports exceed its imports (positive net exports).
- **Balance of Payments**: A record of all economic transactions between a country and the rest of the world. Current account + Capital/financial account = 0 (they must balance).
- **Current Account**: Records trade in goods and services, income from abroad, and transfer payments. A trade deficit means a current account deficit.
- **Capital (Financial) Account**: Records investment flows -- foreign purchases of domestic assets and domestic purchases of foreign assets.
- **Exchange Rate**: The price of one currency in terms of another (e.g., $1 = 0.92 euros).
- **Appreciation**: An increase in the value of a currency relative to another. Makes imports cheaper and exports more expensive.
- **Depreciation**: A decrease in the value of a currency relative to another. Makes imports more expensive and exports cheaper.
- **Floating Exchange Rate**: Exchange rate determined by market supply and demand. Most major currencies use this system.
- **Fixed Exchange Rate**: Exchange rate set by the government. The central bank must buy/sell foreign currency to maintain the peg.
- **Protectionism**: Government policies that restrict international trade to shield domestic industries (tariffs, quotas, subsidies, regulations).
- **Free Trade**: The absence of government barriers to international trade.

## Core Concepts

### Comparative Advantage and Specialization

Even if the US is better at producing BOTH wheat and cars than Mexico (absolute advantage in both), trade is still beneficial. If the US has a lower opportunity cost of producing wheat and Mexico has a lower opportunity cost of producing cars, the US should specialize in wheat and Mexico in cars, then trade. Total world output increases. This is David Ricardo's insight (1817) and the most important idea in international economics.

### Why Countries Restrict Trade Despite Gains

Trade creates overall gains but not for everyone. When the US imports cheap steel from China, American steel consumers benefit (lower prices), but American steelworkers lose jobs. The gains are diffuse (millions of consumers save a little) while the losses are concentrated (thousands of workers lose a lot). The losers organize politically and lobby for tariffs and quotas. This explains why protectionism persists despite economists' near-unanimous support for free trade.

### Exchange Rate Determination

In a floating system, exchange rates are determined by supply and demand for currencies. Demand for dollars comes from: foreign buyers of US exports, foreign investors in US assets, and speculators. Supply of dollars comes from: US buyers of imports, US investors abroad, and speculators. Factors that appreciate the dollar: higher US interest rates (attracting foreign capital), stronger US economic growth, demand for safe assets. Factors that depreciate the dollar: higher US inflation, lower US interest rates, trade deficits.

### The Balance of Payments Identity

A trade deficit (current account deficit) must be financed by a capital account surplus -- foreigners invest more in the US than Americans invest abroad. The US trades goods for financial assets: we buy Chinese goods, and China buys US Treasury bonds. This means a trade deficit is not inherently "bad" -- it reflects that foreigners find the US an attractive place to invest.

## Examples

### Example: US-China Trade

The US has a large trade deficit with China (importing far more than exporting). China specializes in manufactured goods (comparative advantage in labor-intensive production). The US specializes in technology, services, agriculture, and financial services. The trade deficit is financed by Chinese purchases of US Treasury bonds (capital account surplus). Tariffs imposed in 2018-2019 raised prices for US consumers and disrupted supply chains without significantly reducing the deficit.

### Example: Comparative Advantage -- US and Colombia

Suppose the US can produce 10 tons of wheat or 5 cars with one unit of resources. Colombia can produce 4 tons of wheat or 2 cars. The US has absolute advantage in both. But opportunity costs: US: 1 car costs 2 tons wheat; Colombia: 1 car costs 2 tons wheat. In this case, no comparative advantage exists -- opportunity costs are equal, so there is no basis for mutually beneficial trade. Now change Colombia to 4 wheat or 3 cars: Colombia's opportunity cost of a car = 4/3 tons of wheat (lower than the US's 2 tons). Colombia has comparative advantage in cars; the US in wheat. Both gain by trading.

### Example: Brexit and the British Pound

When the UK voted to leave the EU in June 2016, the British pound depreciated sharply (about 10% overnight) because markets anticipated reduced foreign investment and trade barriers. The weaker pound made UK exports cheaper (boosting exporters) but imports more expensive (raising prices for consumers). This illustrates how exchange rates respond to expectations about a country's economic future.

## Relationships

- Comparative Advantage -> Specialization -> Gains from Trade -> Higher total world output
- Tariff -> Higher domestic price -> Domestic producers gain, consumers lose, DWL created
- Trade Deficit -> Current Account Deficit -> Capital Account Surplus (foreigners invest in the country)
- Higher domestic interest rates -> Capital inflows -> Currency appreciation -> Exports fall, imports rise
- Currency depreciation -> Exports become cheaper -> Net exports increase -> AD shifts right
- Protectionism -> Reduces trade -> Reduces gains from specialization -> Lower total welfare
- Exchange rate changes -> Affect NX component of AD -> Shift AD

## Common Misconceptions

- **"Trade deficits mean a country is losing"**: A trade deficit means a country imports more than it exports, but it is financed by capital inflows (foreign investment). The US has run deficits for decades while growing wealthily. A deficit can reflect a strong, attractive economy.
- **"If one country gains from trade, the other loses"**: Wrong. Trade is not zero-sum. Comparative advantage means BOTH countries gain from specialization and trade. Total output increases.
- **"Comparative advantage requires absolute advantage"**: Wrong. The whole point is that even a country with absolute DISadvantage in everything can benefit from trade by specializing in the good where its opportunity cost is lowest.
- **"Tariffs protect jobs at no cost"**: Tariffs protect jobs in the tariff-protected industry but at the cost of higher prices for consumers and potential retaliation. Studies show the cost per job saved by tariffs often exceeds $500,000/year.
- **"A strong dollar is always better"**: A stronger dollar makes imports cheaper (good for consumers) but exports more expensive (bad for exporters). It depends on the context.
