# Macroeconomic Schools of Thought

## Overview

Macroeconomists disagree about how the economy works and what policies are appropriate. The major schools of thought differ on key questions: How quickly do markets self-correct? Is government intervention helpful or harmful? What causes business cycles? The debate shapes real-world policy decisions.

The Phillips curve -- the observed trade-off between inflation and unemployment -- is a central battleground. In the short run, expansionary policy can reduce unemployment but at the cost of higher inflation. In the long run, this trade-off may disappear as expectations adjust. Understanding these different perspectives is essential for evaluating policy proposals.

## Key Terms

- **Classical Economics**: The pre-Keynesian view that markets are self-correcting, wages and prices are flexible, and the economy naturally operates at full employment. Government intervention is unnecessary and potentially harmful. Say's Law: "Supply creates its own demand."
- **Keynesian Economics**: Founded by John Maynard Keynes during the Great Depression. Wages and prices are sticky, especially downward. Markets can stay in recession for extended periods. Active fiscal and monetary policy is needed to manage aggregate demand and stabilize the economy.
- **Monetarism**: Associated with Milton Friedman. Emphasizes the money supply as the key driver of economic fluctuations. Advocates for a steady, predictable growth rate of the money supply rather than active discretionary policy. "Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon."
- **New Classical Economics**: Markets clear continuously because agents have rational expectations. Anticipated policy changes are ineffective (policy ineffectiveness proposition). Only unexpected policy changes affect real output. Advocates minimal government intervention.
- **New Keynesian Economics**: Accepts rational expectations but argues that sticky prices and wages (due to menu costs, contracts, efficiency wages) mean markets do not clear instantly. Provides microeconomic foundations for Keynesian policy conclusions. Most central bank models are New Keynesian.
- **Supply-Side Economics**: Emphasizes policies to increase aggregate supply: tax cuts (especially on capital and high incomes), deregulation, and free trade. Associated with the Laffer Curve and Reaganomics.
- **Austrian Economics**: Associated with Hayek and Mises. Emphasizes the business cycle as caused by artificial credit expansion. Opposes central banking and most government intervention. Markets, left alone, self-correct best.
- **Phillips Curve (Short-Run)**: An inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment. Lower unemployment comes with higher inflation, and vice versa.
- **Phillips Curve (Long-Run)**: Vertical at the natural rate of unemployment (NAIRU). In the long run, there is no trade-off -- any unemployment rate can coexist with any inflation rate once expectations adjust.
- **NAIRU (Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment)**: The unemployment rate at which inflation is stable. Equivalent to the natural rate. Attempting to push unemployment below NAIRU causes accelerating inflation.
- **Rational Expectations**: The theory that people use all available information to form accurate forecasts of future economic conditions. Implies that systematic (anticipated) policy is ineffective because people adjust their behavior in advance.
- **Adaptive Expectations**: The theory that people form expectations based on past experience. They adjust slowly, meaning policy can have real effects in the short run.

## Core Concepts

### The Phillips Curve Trade-Off

In 1958, A.W. Phillips observed an inverse relationship between wage inflation and unemployment in UK data. This became the most famous empirical relationship in macroeconomics. Policymakers believed they could "choose" a point on the Phillips curve -- accept higher inflation for lower unemployment. This worked in the 1960s. But the 1970s stagflation (high inflation AND high unemployment) seemed to destroy the trade-off.

### Friedman's Natural Rate Hypothesis

Milton Friedman (1968) argued the Phillips curve trade-off is only temporary. Expansionary policy can reduce unemployment below the natural rate, but only by generating unexpected inflation. Once workers and firms adjust their expectations (adaptive expectations), they demand higher wages, shifting the short-run Phillips curve upward. Unemployment returns to the natural rate but at higher inflation. In the long run, the Phillips curve is vertical at the natural rate -- there is no permanent trade-off.

### Expectations and Policy Effectiveness

If expectations are adaptive (backward-looking), policy can work in the short run because people are surprised. If expectations are rational (forward-looking), anticipated policy changes are immediately incorporated into behavior and have no real effect. Only surprises move the economy. This is the Lucas Critique: historical relationships (like the Phillips curve) may break down when policy changes because people's behavior changes too.

### Key Policy Disagreements

| Issue | Classical/New Classical | Keynesian/New Keynesian | Monetarist |
|-------|----------------------|------------------------|------------|
| Self-correction speed | Fast | Slow | Medium |
| Government role | Minimal | Active stabilization | Rules, not discretion |
| Fiscal policy | Ineffective (Ricardian equivalence) | Effective, especially in recession | Questionable (crowding out) |
| Monetary policy | Neutral in long run | Effective in short run | Follow money growth rules |
| Cause of recessions | Real shocks, government interference | Insufficient aggregate demand | Unstable money supply |
| Wages and prices | Flexible | Sticky | Mostly flexible |

## Examples

### Example: The Volcker Disinflation (1979-1982)

Fed Chair Paul Volcker raised the federal funds rate to 20% to break 1970s inflation. Unemployment rose to 10.8% -- the highest since the Great Depression. But inflation fell from 13% to 3%. Classical interpretation: once expectations adjusted, the sacrifice was unavoidable. Keynesian interpretation: the cost (severe recession) was too high; a more gradual approach would have been better. Monetarist interpretation: the Fed should have controlled money supply growth steadily from the start, avoiding the boom-bust.

### Example: The Great Depression and Keynes

Classical economics could not explain why the economy remained in depression for a decade. Markets should have self-corrected. Keynes argued that wages and prices were too sticky to adjust, animal spirits destroyed confidence, and the economy was stuck in a liquidity trap. His prescription: massive government spending to replace collapsed private demand. The New Deal and WWII spending ultimately vindicated this view (though debate continues about whether the spending was efficient).

### Example: 2021-2023 Inflation and Phillips Curve

Post-COVID stimulus pushed unemployment to 3.4% (well below most estimates of NAIRU around 4-4.5%). Inflation surged to 9.1%. This appeared consistent with the Phillips curve: pushing unemployment too low generated inflation. As the Fed raised rates and unemployment rose slightly (to ~4%), inflation fell. Both Keynesian (sticky prices matter) and Monetarist (excessive money growth) explanations had some validity.

## Relationships

- Classical -> Flexible prices -> Economy self-corrects quickly -> No need for policy
- Keynesian -> Sticky prices -> Economy can stay below potential -> Active policy needed
- Short-run Phillips Curve -> Trade-off between inflation and unemployment (temporary)
- Long-run Phillips Curve -> No trade-off; vertical at natural rate (NAIRU)
- Rational Expectations -> Anticipated policy is ineffective -> Only surprises matter
- Adaptive Expectations -> Policy works in short run -> Expectations adjust slowly
- Monetarism -> Money supply drives price level -> Rules-based policy preferred
- Supply-side -> Tax cuts increase AS -> Growth with lower inflation (if effective)

## Common Misconceptions

- **"There is one correct school of macroeconomics"**: Each school captures important insights. Modern macroeconomics (New Keynesian framework) synthesizes elements from multiple traditions.
- **"The Phillips curve is dead"**: The short-run Phillips curve remains a useful (if imperfect) framework. The 1970s showed the long-run curve is vertical, but the short-run trade-off still operates.
- **"Rational expectations means people are never wrong"**: People make errors, but their errors are not systematic or predictable. On average, they forecast correctly.
- **"Supply-side economics means all tax cuts pay for themselves"**: This claim (the Laffer Curve at extreme rates) has limited empirical support at normal tax rates. Most tax cuts reduce revenue.
- **"Keynesian economics means unlimited government spending"**: Keynes advocated countercyclical policy -- spend during recessions, run surpluses during expansions. He did not advocate permanent deficits.
