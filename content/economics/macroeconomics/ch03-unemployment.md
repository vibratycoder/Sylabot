# Unemployment

## Overview
Unemployment measures the percentage of the labor force that is jobless and actively seeking work. It is one of the most important macroeconomic indicators. Not all unemployment is the same: frictional unemployment (job searching) and structural unemployment (skills mismatch) exist even in a healthy economy, while cyclical unemployment rises during recessions. The natural rate of unemployment is the baseline level when the economy is at full employment.

## Key Terms
- **Labor Force**: All persons 16+ who are either employed or unemployed (actively looking for work). Excludes students, retirees, stay-at-home parents, and discouraged workers.
- **Unemployment Rate**: (Number unemployed / Labor force) x 100. The most commonly cited measure.
- **Employed**: Working for pay (at least 1 hour/week) or temporarily absent from a job.
- **Unemployed**: Not working but actively seeking work in the past 4 weeks.
- **Frictional Unemployment**: Short-term unemployment from the normal job search process. Recent graduates, people switching careers, workers between jobs. Exists even in a healthy economy.
- **Structural Unemployment**: Unemployment due to a mismatch between workers' skills and available jobs. Caused by technological change, globalization, or decline of industries. Can be long-lasting.
- **Cyclical Unemployment**: Unemployment caused by economic downturns (recessions). GDP falls, demand drops, firms lay off workers. Disappears during expansions.
- **Natural Rate of Unemployment**: The unemployment rate when cyclical unemployment is zero (frictional + structural only). Typically estimated at 4-5% in the US.
- **Full Employment**: The economy is at the natural rate of unemployment. Does NOT mean 0% unemployment.
- **Discouraged Workers**: People who want a job but have stopped looking because they believe no jobs are available. Not counted in the labor force, so they don't appear in the unemployment rate.
- **Underemployment**: Workers employed part-time who want full-time work, or workers in jobs below their skill level.
- **Labor Force Participation Rate**: (Labor force / Working-age population) x 100. Has been declining in the US due to aging population and other factors.

## Core Concepts

### Measuring Unemployment
The Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) conducts the Current Population Survey (CPS) monthly, surveying 60,000 households. People are classified as employed, unemployed, or not in the labor force. The official unemployment rate (U-3) is the most cited, but broader measures (U-6) include discouraged and underemployed workers.

### Types of Unemployment
Frictional: temporary, voluntary, healthy (people finding better matches). Structural: longer-lasting, involuntary, caused by economic shifts. Cyclical: linked to business cycle, the type policymakers most want to reduce. The natural rate = frictional + structural.

### Costs of Unemployment
Economic costs: lost output (GDP below potential), lost tax revenue, increased government spending on benefits. Personal costs: income loss, stress, health problems, skill erosion. Social costs: increased crime, family instability, political unrest.

## Examples

### COVID-19 Unemployment Spike
In April 2020, the US unemployment rate spiked to 14.7% -- the highest since the Great Depression. Most was cyclical (lockdowns reduced economic activity). As the economy reopened, unemployment fell rapidly. Some structural unemployment remained as certain industries (retail, hospitality) permanently shrank.

### Automation and Structural Unemployment
Self-checkout machines replace cashiers. Self-driving trucks may replace truckers. This creates structural unemployment -- the displaced workers' skills don't match available jobs. Retraining programs and education can help, but the transition is painful.

## Relationships
- Recession -> Cyclical unemployment rises -> Unemployment rate rises
- Expansion -> Cyclical unemployment falls -> Rate approaches natural rate
- Technological change -> Structural unemployment (short-run) -> Productivity gains (long-run)
- Discouraged workers leave labor force -> Unemployment rate artificially drops
- Natural rate = Frictional + Structural (no cyclical)

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Full employment means 0% unemployment"**: Full employment means the economy is at the natural rate (~4-5%). Some frictional and structural unemployment always exists.
- **"The unemployment rate captures all joblessness"**: It misses discouraged workers, underemployed workers, and those marginally attached to the labor force. The U-6 rate is a broader measure.
- **"All unemployment is bad"**: Frictional unemployment is actually healthy -- it means people are searching for better job matches rather than taking the first available job.
