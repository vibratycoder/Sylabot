# Money and the Banking System

## Overview
Money is anything widely accepted as payment. The modern banking system creates money through fractional reserve banking -- banks hold only a fraction of deposits as reserves and lend the rest, multiplying the money supply. The Federal Reserve controls the money supply to stabilize the economy. Understanding money creation is essential for understanding monetary policy, inflation, and financial crises.

## Key Terms
- **Money**: Anything widely accepted as a medium of exchange. Must serve three functions: medium of exchange, store of value, unit of account.
- **M1**: The narrowest money supply measure: currency in circulation + demand deposits (checking accounts) + traveler's checks.
- **M2**: M1 + savings deposits + money market funds + small time deposits. A broader measure.
- **Fractional Reserve Banking**: Banks hold only a fraction of deposits as reserves and lend the rest. This process creates new money.
- **Reserve Ratio (rr)**: The fraction of deposits banks hold as reserves. Required reserve ratio is set by the Fed.
- **Money Multiplier**: 1 / rr. The maximum amount of new money created from each dollar of new reserves. If rr = 10%, multiplier = 10.
- **Excess Reserves**: Reserves beyond what is required. Banks can lend excess reserves.
- **Federal Reserve (the Fed)**: The US central bank. Controls money supply and monetary policy.
- **Bank Run**: When depositors rush to withdraw money, fearing the bank will fail. Can cause bank failure even if the bank is solvent.
- **FDIC (Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation)**: Insures deposits up to $250,000, preventing bank runs by guaranteeing depositors won't lose money.
- **Federal Funds Rate**: The interest rate banks charge each other for overnight loans. The Fed's primary policy tool.

## Core Concepts

### Money Creation Process
1. Person deposits $1,000 in Bank A. 2. Bank A keeps 10% ($100) as reserves, lends $900 to a borrower. 3. Borrower deposits $900 in Bank B. 4. Bank B keeps $90, lends $810. 5. Process continues. Total money created = $1,000 x (1/0.10) = $10,000. Each round of lending creates new deposits (money).

### The Fed's Structure
12 regional Federal Reserve Banks, overseen by the Board of Governors (7 members, 14-year terms). The Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) sets monetary policy. The Fed's dual mandate: maximum employment and stable prices.

### Why Banks Fail
Banks borrow short (deposits can be withdrawn anytime) and lend long (mortgages, business loans). If too many depositors withdraw at once (bank run), the bank doesn't have enough liquid assets. FDIC insurance prevents most runs in modern banking.

## Examples

### 2008 Banking Crisis
Banks held mortgage-backed securities that lost value when housing prices collapsed. Banks became insolvent (liabilities > assets). Lehman Brothers failed, triggering panic. The Fed and Treasury intervened with bailouts (TARP) and emergency lending to prevent systemic collapse.

### Money Creation Example
If the reserve ratio is 20%, every $1,000 deposited can support $5,000 in total money (multiplier = 1/0.20 = 5). The original deposit creates $4,000 in new money through the lending process.

## Relationships
- Lower reserve ratio -> Higher money multiplier -> More money creation
- Bank run -> Bank failure (without FDIC insurance)
- Fed increases reserves -> Banks lend more -> Money supply increases
- More money (given stable V and Q) -> Higher prices (Quantity Theory)

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Banks lend out depositors' money"**: Banks create new money through lending. The deposit still exists in the depositor's account while the loan creates new spending power.
- **"The money multiplier works perfectly"**: In reality, banks may hold excess reserves (especially during crises) and borrowers may hold cash, reducing the actual multiplier.
- **"The Fed prints money"**: The Fed creates bank reserves electronically. Physical currency is produced by the Treasury's Bureau of Engraving and Printing.
