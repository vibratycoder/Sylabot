# Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply

## Overview
The AD-AS model is the central framework of macroeconomics. Aggregate demand (AD) represents total spending in the economy at each price level. Short-run aggregate supply (SRAS) shows total output at each price level given current input prices. Long-run aggregate supply (LRAS) is vertical at potential GDP. The intersection determines the price level and real GDP. Shifts in AD or AS cause recessions, inflation, or both (stagflation).

## Key Terms
- **Aggregate Demand (AD)**: Total spending on goods and services at each price level. AD = C + I + G + NX. Slopes downward.
- **Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)**: Total output firms supply at each price level, given current input prices. Slopes upward (higher prices -> higher profits -> more output).
- **Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS)**: Vertical line at potential GDP. In the long run, output depends on resources and technology, not the price level.
- **Potential GDP (Y*)**: The level of real GDP the economy produces when at full employment (natural rate of unemployment). Also called full-employment output.
- **Recessionary Gap**: Real GDP is below potential GDP. Unemployment exceeds the natural rate. AD is insufficient.
- **Inflationary Gap**: Real GDP is above potential GDP. Unemployment below natural rate. Upward pressure on prices.
- **Stagflation**: Combination of stagnation (low growth, high unemployment) and inflation. Caused by a negative supply shock (SRAS shifts left).
- **Demand Shock**: An event that shifts AD (consumer confidence, government spending, monetary policy).
- **Supply Shock**: An event that shifts SRAS (oil prices, natural disasters, input cost changes).
- **Self-Correction**: The theory that the economy will return to potential GDP on its own in the long run as wages and prices adjust.
- **Multiplier Effect**: An initial change in spending causes a larger change in GDP because one person's spending becomes another's income.
- **Sticky Wages/Prices**: Wages and prices that are slow to adjust in the short run due to contracts, menu costs, money illusion, and efficiency wages. This is WHY the SRAS slopes upward rather than being vertical.
- **Output Gap**: The difference between actual GDP and potential GDP. Positive gap = inflationary; negative gap = recessionary.

## Core Concepts

### Why AD Slopes Downward
Three effects of a lower price level: (1) Wealth effect -- lower prices increase real value of savings, boosting consumption. (2) Interest rate effect -- lower prices reduce money demand, lowering interest rates, boosting investment. (3) Exchange rate effect -- lower prices make exports cheaper, increasing net exports.

### AD Shifters
Anything that changes C, I, G, or NX at every price level: consumer confidence, wealth changes, tax policy, interest rates, government spending, exchange rates, foreign income.

### SRAS Shifters
Input prices (wages, oil, materials), productivity changes, business taxes, government regulation, supply shocks (weather, pandemics).

### Analyzing Shocks
Demand shock (increase): AD shifts right -> P rises, Y rises -> inflationary gap. Supply shock (decrease): SRAS shifts left -> P rises, Y falls -> stagflation. Policy response differs: demand shocks can be countered with monetary/fiscal policy; supply shocks present a dilemma (fighting inflation worsens unemployment and vice versa).

## Examples

### 2008 Financial Crisis (Negative Demand Shock)
Housing market collapse -> bank failures -> credit freeze -> consumer/business confidence plummets -> AD shifts left dramatically. Result: deep recession, unemployment rises to 10%, prices stable or falling. Policy response: massive fiscal stimulus (ARRA) and Fed rate cuts to zero.

### 1970s Oil Crisis (Negative Supply Shock)
OPEC oil embargo -> oil prices quadruple -> SRAS shifts left. Result: stagflation (high inflation + high unemployment simultaneously). Policy dilemma: fighting inflation with tight money worsens unemployment; stimulating employment worsens inflation.

### Post-COVID Recovery (Dual Shock)
COVID-19 simultaneously shifted AD left (lockdowns reduced spending) and SRAS left (supply chain disruptions). Massive fiscal stimulus and Fed rate cuts shifted AD right rapidly, but SRAS recovered slowly. The result: strong demand hitting constrained supply caused inflation to surge to 9.1% by mid-2022. The Fed then aggressively raised rates (shifting AD left) to cool inflation.

### Long-Run Self-Correction Example
After a recession (recessionary gap), high unemployment puts downward pressure on wages over time. Lower wages reduce production costs, shifting SRAS rightward. This continues until output returns to potential GDP at a lower price level. The process can take 2-5+ years, which is why Keynes argued for active policy intervention rather than waiting.

## Relationships
- AD shifts right -> Higher P, higher Y (inflationary gap)
- AD shifts left -> Lower P, lower Y (recessionary gap)
- SRAS shifts left -> Higher P, lower Y (stagflation)
- SRAS shifts right -> Lower P, higher Y (best case)
- Long-run self-correction: wages adjust -> SRAS shifts -> Economy returns to LRAS

## Common Misconceptions
- **"The AD-AS model is just supply and demand"**: AD-AS deals with the entire economy's price level and total output, not a single market's price and quantity. Different mechanisms drive the slopes.
- **"Government should always stimulate during a recession"**: Depends on the cause. Demand-driven recessions respond well to stimulus. Supply shocks are harder -- stimulus may worsen inflation.
- **"The economy self-corrects quickly"**: Self-correction can take years. Keynesians argue this is too long and government intervention is needed. Classicals argue intervention causes more problems.
