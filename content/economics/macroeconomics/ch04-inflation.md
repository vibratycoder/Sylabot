# Inflation

## Overview
Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level. It reduces the purchasing power of money, distorts economic decisions, and redistributes wealth between borrowers and lenders. Moderate inflation (2-3%) is considered normal and healthy. High or unpredictable inflation is damaging. The Consumer Price Index (CPI) is the primary measure. Inflation can be caused by excess demand (demand-pull) or rising production costs (cost-push).

## Key Terms
- **Inflation**: A sustained increase in the general price level of goods and services over time.
- **Deflation**: A sustained decrease in the general price level. Can be worse than inflation (deferred spending, debt burden increases).
- **Consumer Price Index (CPI)**: A measure of the average change in prices paid by urban consumers for a basket of goods and services. The most commonly used inflation measure.
- **Inflation Rate**: The percentage change in the CPI from one period to the next. Inflation rate = ((CPI_new - CPI_old) / CPI_old) x 100.
- **Hyperinflation**: Extremely rapid inflation (50%+ per month). Destroys the currency's value. Historical examples: Germany (1923), Zimbabwe (2008), Venezuela (2018+).
- **Demand-Pull Inflation**: Inflation caused by aggregate demand growing faster than aggregate supply ("too much money chasing too few goods").
- **Cost-Push Inflation**: Inflation caused by rising production costs (oil prices, wages) that shift aggregate supply left.
- **Purchasing Power**: The amount of goods and services a unit of currency can buy. Inflation reduces purchasing power.
- **Nominal Value**: The face value of money, unadjusted for inflation (nominal wage, nominal interest rate).
- **Real Value**: The value adjusted for inflation, reflecting actual purchasing power (real wage, real interest rate).
- **Fisher Effect**: Nominal interest rate ≈ Real interest rate + Expected inflation rate. Lenders demand higher nominal rates when they expect inflation.
- **Menu Costs**: The costs of changing prices (reprinting menus, updating systems). A cost of inflation.
- **Shoe-Leather Costs**: The costs of reducing money holdings during inflation (more frequent bank trips). A cost of high inflation.

## Core Concepts

### Measuring Inflation with CPI
The BLS tracks prices of ~80,000 items in a fixed "market basket" representing typical urban consumer spending. CPI biases: substitution bias (consumers switch to cheaper alternatives), quality improvement bias (better products at same price aren't fully captured), new goods bias.

### Who Wins and Loses from Inflation
- **Losers**: Savers (purchasing power of savings erodes), lenders (repaid in less valuable dollars), workers with fixed wages, people on fixed incomes
- **Winners**: Borrowers (repay loans with cheaper dollars), owners of real assets (property, commodities appreciate), flexible-wage earners

### The Quantity Theory of Money
MV = PQ (Money supply x Velocity = Price level x Real output). If velocity and output are stable, increases in money supply lead to proportional increases in prices. This is the classical explanation for inflation and why excessive money printing causes hyperinflation.

## Examples

### Venezuela's Hyperinflation
Venezuela's government printed money to cover massive budget deficits. Inflation exceeded 1,000,000% in 2018. Prices doubled every few days. The currency became worthless -- people weighed stacks of bills rather than counting them. The economy collapsed as no one could plan or save.

### 2021-2023 US Inflation
Post-COVID stimulus (demand-pull: trillions in government spending) combined with supply chain disruptions (cost-push: shipping delays, chip shortages) drove US inflation to 9.1% in June 2022 -- the highest in 40 years. The Federal Reserve raised interest rates aggressively to bring it down.

## Relationships
- Money supply grows faster than output -> Demand-pull inflation
- Oil price spike -> Cost-push inflation -> Stagflation risk
- Expected inflation -> Higher nominal interest rates (Fisher Effect)
- Unexpected inflation -> Transfers wealth from lenders to borrowers
- Central bank raises rates -> Reduces inflation (but may increase unemployment)

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Inflation means everything gets more expensive"**: Some prices may fall even during inflation. CPI measures the average change across a basket.
- **"Deflation is always good because prices fall"**: Deflation discourages spending (why buy now if it's cheaper tomorrow?), increases real debt burden, and can trigger deflationary spirals.
- **"Printing money is free money"**: Printing money without corresponding output growth causes inflation, which is effectively a hidden tax on everyone holding that currency.
