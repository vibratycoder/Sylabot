# Economic Growth

## Overview
Economic growth -- sustained increases in real GDP per capita -- is the most important determinant of long-run living standards. Small differences in growth rates compound into enormous differences over time. Growth depends on accumulation of physical capital, human capital, technological progress, and the quality of institutions. Understanding why some countries grow fast and others stagnate is one of the central questions of economics.

## Key Terms
- **Economic Growth**: A sustained increase in real GDP per capita over time.
- **Productivity**: Output per worker (or per hour). The primary driver of living standards.
- **Physical Capital**: Tools, machinery, equipment, and infrastructure used in production.
- **Human Capital**: Knowledge, skills, education, and health of the workforce.
- **Technological Progress**: New knowledge, inventions, and innovations that allow more output from the same inputs. The most important long-run growth driver.
- **Rule of 70**: Doubling time ≈ 70 / growth rate (%). At 2% growth, GDP doubles in ~35 years. At 7% growth, ~10 years.
- **Diminishing Returns to Capital**: Each additional unit of capital adds less output than the previous one (holding other inputs constant).
- **Convergence**: The hypothesis that poorer countries grow faster than richer ones, eventually catching up. Conditional convergence depends on institutions and policies.
- **Institutions**: Legal systems, property rights, rule of law, political stability, and governance structures that create incentives for investment and innovation.
- **Total Factor Productivity (TFP)**: The portion of output growth not explained by increases in labor and capital. Often interpreted as technological progress.

## Core Concepts

### Sources of Growth
1. **Physical capital accumulation** -- More machines, factories, roads. Subject to diminishing returns.
2. **Human capital accumulation** -- More education, training, health. Increases productivity of each worker.
3. **Technological progress** -- New ideas, processes, inventions. The only source of sustained long-run growth (no diminishing returns to ideas).
4. **Institutions** -- Property rights, stable government, rule of law, free markets, openness to trade. Create incentives for investment and innovation.

### The Power of Compounding
Small growth rate differences have enormous effects over time. Country A growing at 1% and Country B at 3% start with the same GDP. After 70 years, Country B's GDP is 4x larger. This is why growth policy matters more than almost any other economic issue.

### Why Some Countries Are Poor
The "why are some countries poor?" question has many answers: lack of physical and human capital, poor institutions (corruption, weak property rights), geographic disadvantages, colonial legacy, conflict, and barriers to technology adoption.

## Examples

### South Korea vs North Korea
In 1960, South and North Korea had similar GDP per capita. South Korea invested heavily in education, opened to trade, built strong institutions, and adopted new technology. By 2020, South Korea's GDP per capita was 20-40x North Korea's. Same people, culture, and geography -- different institutions and policies.

### China's Growth Miracle
China grew at ~10%/year from 1980-2010, lifting 800 million people out of poverty. Key factors: market reforms (1978), massive infrastructure investment, export-oriented manufacturing, huge human capital investment, and technology adoption from abroad.

## Relationships
- More physical capital -> Higher productivity -> Growth (with diminishing returns)
- More education -> Higher human capital -> Higher productivity -> Growth
- Better technology -> More output per input -> Sustained growth
- Strong institutions -> Incentives to invest/innovate -> Faster growth
- Rule of 70: doubling time = 70 / growth rate

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Natural resources are the key to growth"**: Japan and South Korea grew rapidly with few resources. Many resource-rich countries (Venezuela, Nigeria) have struggled ("resource curse").
- **"Just adding capital will create sustained growth"**: Diminishing returns to capital mean that capital alone can't sustain growth. Technological progress is essential.
- **"All countries will eventually converge"**: Unconditional convergence hasn't happened. Countries with bad institutions may never catch up. Conditional convergence (among countries with similar institutions) has more support.
