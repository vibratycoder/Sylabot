# Fiscal Policy

## Overview
Fiscal policy is the use of government spending and taxation to influence the economy. During recessions, expansionary fiscal policy (more spending or tax cuts) boosts aggregate demand. During inflation, contractionary fiscal policy (less spending or tax increases) cools the economy. Fiscal policy has limitations: time lags, political constraints, crowding out, and growing national debt.

## Key Terms
- **Fiscal Policy**: Government decisions about spending and taxation to achieve macroeconomic goals.
- **Expansionary Fiscal Policy**: Increasing G or decreasing T to boost AD during a recession.
- **Contractionary Fiscal Policy**: Decreasing G or increasing T to reduce AD during inflation.
- **Spending Multiplier**: The ratio of total GDP change to the initial change in spending. Multiplier = 1 / (1 - MPC). If MPC = 0.8, multiplier = 5.
- **Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC)**: The fraction of additional income that is spent. MPC + MPS = 1.
- **Marginal Propensity to Save (MPS)**: The fraction of additional income that is saved.
- **Tax Multiplier**: The change in GDP from a $1 change in taxes. Tax multiplier = -MPC / (1 - MPC). Smaller than spending multiplier because some tax cut is saved.
- **Crowding Out**: When government borrowing raises interest rates, reducing private investment. Limits the effectiveness of fiscal stimulus.
- **Budget Deficit**: When government spending exceeds tax revenue in a given year.
- **Budget Surplus**: When tax revenue exceeds government spending.
- **National Debt**: The total accumulation of past deficits minus surpluses. The amount the government owes.
- **Automatic Stabilizers**: Government programs that automatically counter the business cycle without new legislation: progressive income taxes (revenue falls in recession), unemployment insurance (spending rises in recession).
- **Discretionary Fiscal Policy**: Deliberate changes in spending or taxes requiring legislative action.

## Core Concepts

### The Multiplier Process
When government spends $100 billion, that becomes income for workers and firms, who spend a portion (MPC), which becomes income for others, and so on. If MPC = 0.8: $100B -> $80B -> $64B -> ... Total GDP increase = $100B x (1/0.2) = $500B. Tax cuts have a smaller multiplier because recipients save some of the tax cut first.

### Crowding Out
Government borrows to fund deficit spending -> demand for loanable funds increases -> interest rates rise -> private investment falls. The GDP boost from government spending is partially offset by reduced private investment. Crowding out is less severe during recessions (when private investment demand is already low).

### The Debt Debate
Keynesian view: deficits are appropriate during recessions (borrow now, pay back during expansions). Classical view: government borrowing crowds out private investment, burden falls on future generations. Reality: moderate debt is manageable if GDP grows; excessive debt (high debt-to-GDP ratio) can become a crisis.

## Examples

### American Recovery and Reinvestment Act (2009)
Congress passed $787 billion in stimulus during the Great Recession: tax cuts, infrastructure spending, aid to states. Supporters: prevented a depression, saved jobs. Critics: added to national debt, some spending was wasteful, crowded out private activity.

### COVID-19 Stimulus (2020-2021)
CARES Act ($2.2T), plus subsequent packages totaling ~$5T. Direct payments, enhanced unemployment, PPP loans. GDP bounced back quickly but critics argue excessive stimulus contributed to 2022 inflation.

## Relationships
- Recession -> Expansionary fiscal policy (increase G or cut T) -> AD shifts right
- Inflation -> Contractionary fiscal policy (cut G or raise T) -> AD shifts left
- Higher MPC -> Larger multiplier -> Stronger fiscal impact
- Government borrowing -> Higher interest rates -> Crowding out of private investment
- Automatic stabilizers -> Moderate business cycle without legislative action

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Government spending always creates growth"**: Crowding out, waste, and misallocation can reduce the net effect. The multiplier is not infinite.
- **"Budget deficits are always bad"**: Deficit spending during severe recessions can prevent economic collapse. The key is the debt-to-GDP ratio and whether the spending generates returns.
- **"Tax cuts pay for themselves"**: Rarely true. Tax cuts may boost growth, but the additional tax revenue from growth almost never fully offsets the initial revenue loss.
