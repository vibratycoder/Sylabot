# Monetary Policy and the Federal Reserve

## Overview
Monetary policy is the Fed's use of tools to control the money supply and interest rates to achieve stable prices and maximum employment. The primary tool is the federal funds rate, controlled through open market operations. Expansionary policy (lower rates) stimulates the economy during recessions; contractionary policy (higher rates) cools inflation. Monetary policy works through the interest rate channel, affecting investment, consumption, and exchange rates.

## Key Terms
- **Monetary Policy**: The Fed's actions to manage the money supply and interest rates to achieve macroeconomic goals.
- **Expansionary Monetary Policy**: Increasing money supply / lowering interest rates to stimulate AD during a recession.
- **Contractionary Monetary Policy**: Decreasing money supply / raising interest rates to reduce AD during inflation.
- **Open Market Operations (OMO)**: The Fed buying or selling government bonds. Buying bonds injects reserves (expansionary); selling bonds withdraws reserves (contractionary). The primary policy tool.
- **Federal Funds Rate**: The overnight lending rate between banks. The FOMC sets a target; the Fed achieves it through OMO.
- **Discount Rate**: The interest rate the Fed charges banks for direct loans. Usually set above the federal funds rate.
- **Reserve Requirements**: The percentage of deposits banks must hold as reserves. Rarely changed as a policy tool.
- **Quantitative Easing (QE)**: Large-scale purchases of long-term bonds and mortgage-backed securities to lower long-term interest rates when the federal funds rate is already near zero.
- **Zero Lower Bound**: When the federal funds rate is at or near 0%, the Fed can't lower it further. QE and forward guidance are alternatives.
- **Transmission Mechanism**: How monetary policy affects the economy: Fed changes reserves -> interest rates change -> investment and consumption change -> AD shifts -> output and prices change.
- **Taylor Rule**: A guideline for setting the federal funds rate based on inflation and the output gap: r = 2% + inflation + 0.5(inflation - 2%) + 0.5(output gap).

## Core Concepts

### How OMO Works
Fed buys bonds -> pays with newly created reserves -> bank reserves increase -> banks have more to lend -> federal funds rate falls -> borrowing becomes cheaper -> investment and consumption rise -> AD increases. Reverse for selling bonds.

### Monetary Policy Transmission
Rate cut -> Lower mortgage rates (housing), lower corporate borrowing costs (investment), lower car loan rates (consumption), weaker dollar (more exports) -> All boost AD -> Higher output and employment.

### Limitations of Monetary Policy
- **Liquidity trap**: At zero lower bound, further rate cuts impossible. Banks may hoard reserves rather than lend (happened in 2008-2015).
- **Time lags**: Policy takes 6-18 months to fully affect the economy.
- **Pushing on a string**: Easy to slow an overheating economy (raise rates) but harder to stimulate a depressed one (can't force banks to lend or businesses to borrow).

## Examples

### Fed Response to 2008 Crisis
Fed cut the federal funds rate from 5.25% to 0-0.25% between 2007-2008. When that wasn't enough, launched QE1, QE2, and QE3 (bought $4.5 trillion in bonds). Kept rates at zero for 7 years (2008-2015). Unprecedented intervention.

### 2022-2023 Rate Hikes
With inflation at 9.1%, the Fed raised rates from 0% to 5.25% in 18 months -- the fastest tightening cycle in 40 years. Mortgage rates doubled, housing market cooled, inflation fell toward target. Debate: did the Fed wait too long to act?

## Relationships
- Fed buys bonds -> Money supply increases -> Interest rates fall -> AD rises
- Fed sells bonds -> Money supply decreases -> Interest rates rise -> AD falls
- Lower rates -> More investment, consumption, housing, weaker dollar
- Higher rates -> Less investment, consumption, housing, stronger dollar
- Inflation above target -> Contractionary policy
- Unemployment above target -> Expansionary policy

## Common Misconceptions
- **"The Fed controls all interest rates"**: The Fed directly controls the federal funds rate. Other rates (mortgage, corporate, savings) are influenced but not directly set.
- **"Printing money always causes inflation"**: Only if it exceeds the economy's capacity. During the 2008 crisis, massive money creation didn't cause inflation because banks hoarded reserves and demand was weak.
- **"Low interest rates are always good"**: Low rates stimulate borrowing but can create asset bubbles (housing, stocks), encourage excessive risk-taking, and leave no room to cut during the next recession.
