# Elasticity

## Overview
Elasticity measures how responsive one variable is to a change in another. Price elasticity of demand tells us how much quantity demanded changes when price changes. This matters enormously for businesses (pricing strategy, revenue) and governments (tax policy). An elastic good sees large quantity changes from small price changes; an inelastic good sees small quantity changes. Elasticity depends on availability of substitutes, necessity vs luxury, time horizon, and the share of income spent on the good.

## Key Terms
- **Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)**: The percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price. PED = %ΔQd / %ΔP. Always negative (law of demand), but typically reported as absolute value.
- **Elastic Demand**: |PED| > 1. Quantity is highly responsive to price. A 1% price increase causes more than 1% drop in quantity.
- **Inelastic Demand**: |PED| < 1. Quantity is not very responsive to price. A 1% price increase causes less than 1% drop in quantity.
- **Unit Elastic**: |PED| = 1. Percentage change in quantity exactly equals percentage change in price.
- **Perfectly Elastic**: PED = infinity. Any price increase causes quantity demanded to drop to zero. Horizontal demand curve.
- **Perfectly Inelastic**: PED = 0. Quantity demanded does not change regardless of price. Vertical demand curve.
- **Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)**: %ΔQs / %ΔP. Measures how responsive producers are to price changes.
- **Income Elasticity of Demand**: %ΔQd / %ΔIncome. Positive for normal goods, negative for inferior goods.
- **Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand**: %ΔQd of Good A / %ΔP of Good B. Positive for substitutes, negative for complements.
- **Total Revenue**: Price x Quantity. The relationship between price changes and total revenue depends on elasticity.
- **Midpoint Method**: Formula for calculating elasticity using average of initial and final values to avoid direction bias: %ΔQ = (Q2-Q1)/((Q2+Q1)/2).

## Core Concepts

### Determinants of Price Elasticity of Demand
1. **Availability of substitutes** -- More substitutes = more elastic (Coke vs water)
2. **Necessity vs luxury** -- Necessities are inelastic (insulin), luxuries are elastic (vacations)
3. **Time horizon** -- Demand becomes more elastic over time (can find alternatives)
4. **Share of income** -- Goods that take a larger share of income are more elastic (cars vs salt)
5. **Definition of the market** -- Narrow markets are more elastic (Coke is elastic, beverages less so, food is inelastic)

### Total Revenue Test
- If demand is elastic: price up -> total revenue down (quantity drops a lot)
- If demand is inelastic: price up -> total revenue up (quantity drops a little)
- If demand is unit elastic: price change -> total revenue unchanged

### Tax Incidence and Elasticity
The burden of a tax falls more heavily on the side of the market that is more inelastic. If demand is more inelastic than supply, consumers bear most of the tax burden. If supply is more inelastic, producers bear most of it.

## Examples

### Gasoline (Inelastic)
Gasoline has few short-run substitutes -- most people need to drive to work regardless of price. PED is approximately 0.2 to 0.5 in the short run. A 10% price increase reduces quantity demanded by only 2-5%. This is why gas taxes generate substantial revenue.

### Restaurant Meals (Elastic)
Eating out is a luxury with many substitutes (cook at home, cheaper restaurants). PED is often greater than 1. A 10% price increase may reduce visits by 15-20%. Restaurants must be careful with pricing.

### Insulin (Perfectly Inelastic)
Diabetics need insulin regardless of price. Demand is nearly perfectly inelastic in the short run -- there is no substitute. This is why insulin pricing is a major public health debate.

## Relationships
- More substitutes -> Higher elasticity
- Necessity -> Lower elasticity (inelastic)
- Elastic demand + price increase -> Total revenue decreases
- Inelastic demand + price increase -> Total revenue increases
- More inelastic side -> Bears more tax burden
- Longer time horizon -> More elastic demand and supply

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Inelastic means quantity doesn't change at all"**: Inelastic means quantity is less responsive, not unresponsive. Only perfectly inelastic (PED = 0) means zero change.
- **"Elasticity is the same as slope"**: Elasticity uses percentage changes; slope uses absolute changes. A straight-line demand curve has varying elasticity along its length.
- **"Raising price always increases revenue"**: Only true for inelastic goods. For elastic goods, raising price decreases revenue because quantity drops proportionally more.
- **"Elasticity is a fixed property of a good"**: Elasticity can change depending on price level, time frame, and market definition.
