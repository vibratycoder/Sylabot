# Consumer and Producer Surplus / Efficiency

## Overview
Markets generate value for both buyers and sellers. Consumer surplus is the difference between what buyers are willing to pay and what they actually pay. Producer surplus is the difference between the price sellers receive and the minimum they'd accept. Total surplus (the sum of both) is maximized at market equilibrium, making free markets efficient under standard assumptions. Government interventions like price controls or taxes create deadweight loss -- a reduction in total surplus that represents lost gains from trade.

## Key Terms
- **Willingness to Pay (WTP)**: The maximum price a consumer will pay for a good. Reflects the value the consumer places on it.
- **Consumer Surplus (CS)**: The area below the demand curve and above the market price. CS = WTP - Price paid. Represents the net benefit to buyers.
- **Producer Surplus (PS)**: The area above the supply curve and below the market price. PS = Price received - Minimum acceptable price. Represents the net benefit to sellers.
- **Total Surplus (TS)**: CS + PS. The total net benefit to society from market transactions.
- **Deadweight Loss (DWL)**: The reduction in total surplus caused by a market distortion (tax, price control, monopoly). Represents transactions that would have been mutually beneficial but don't occur.
- **Allocative Efficiency**: Resources are distributed so that total surplus is maximized. Occurs at market equilibrium where MB = MC.
- **Market Failure**: When the free market fails to achieve allocative efficiency (externalities, public goods, market power, information asymmetry).

## Core Concepts

### Measuring Surplus Graphically
On a supply-demand diagram, consumer surplus is the triangle between the demand curve and the price line (from the y-axis to equilibrium quantity). Producer surplus is the triangle between the price line and the supply curve. At equilibrium, total surplus (both triangles together) is maximized -- no reallocation of resources could make someone better off without making someone else worse off.

### Deadweight Loss from Price Controls
A price ceiling below equilibrium (e.g., rent control) creates a shortage and deadweight loss. Some transactions that would benefit both buyer and seller at the equilibrium price don't happen. A price floor above equilibrium (e.g., minimum wage) creates a surplus and deadweight loss. The DWL triangle represents lost gains from trade.

### Deadweight Loss from Taxes
A tax drives a wedge between the price buyers pay and the price sellers receive. Quantity falls below the efficient level. The tax revenue rectangle is a transfer (not a loss), but the DWL triangle represents trades that no longer occur. The size of DWL depends on elasticities -- more elastic markets have larger deadweight losses from taxes.

## Examples

### Concert Tickets
You'd pay $200 for a ticket but it costs $80. Your consumer surplus is $120. If the venue caps prices at $50 (below equilibrium), more people want tickets than are available -- shortage. Some fans who value tickets at $150 can't get them, while total surplus falls.

### Tax on Sugary Drinks
A $0.50/can tax on soda shifts supply up. Consumers pay more, producers receive less, and fewer cans are sold. The DWL triangle represents the consumers who valued soda above its production cost but below the tax-inclusive price -- trades that no longer happen.

## Relationships
- Market equilibrium -> Maximum total surplus (efficiency)
- Price ceiling below equilibrium -> Shortage + deadweight loss
- Price floor above equilibrium -> Surplus + deadweight loss
- Tax -> Wedge between buyer and seller prices -> DWL
- More elastic market + tax -> Larger deadweight loss
- Consumer surplus + Producer surplus = Total surplus

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Tax revenue is deadweight loss"**: No. Tax revenue is a transfer from market participants to the government. DWL is the separate triangle of lost surplus from reduced transactions.
- **"Price controls help everyone they're designed to help"**: Price ceilings create shortages, so some intended beneficiaries can't get the good at all. Price floors create surpluses, leaving some sellers unable to sell.
- **"Maximizing consumer surplus is the goal"**: Efficiency maximizes total surplus. Policies that increase CS at the expense of PS may or may not increase TS.
