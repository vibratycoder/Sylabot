# Consumer Theory

## Overview

Consumer theory explains how rational consumers make purchasing decisions to maximize their satisfaction (utility) subject to a budget constraint. The theory rests on two pillars: utility analysis (what consumers want) and the budget constraint (what consumers can afford).

The key insight is the equi-marginal principle: a consumer maximizes utility by allocating spending so that the marginal utility per dollar spent is equal across all goods. This explains the downward-sloping demand curve from a theoretical foundation -- as you consume more of a good, diminishing marginal utility means you are willing to pay less for each additional unit.

At the intermediate level (ECO 313), this analysis extends to indifference curves and budget lines, providing a more rigorous graphical framework.

## Key Terms

- **Utility**: A measure of the satisfaction or happiness a consumer receives from consuming a good or service.
- **Total Utility (TU)**: The total satisfaction from consuming a given quantity of a good.
- **Marginal Utility (MU)**: The additional satisfaction gained from consuming one more unit of a good. MU = change in TU / change in quantity.
- **Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility**: As a consumer consumes more of a good (holding consumption of other goods constant), the marginal utility of each additional unit eventually decreases.
- **Utility Maximization**: The consumer's goal -- to achieve the highest total utility possible given their budget constraint.
- **Budget Constraint**: The limit on consumption imposed by income and prices. For two goods: P_x * Q_x + P_y * Q_y <= Income.
- **Equi-Marginal Principle (Utility-Maximizing Rule)**: A consumer maximizes utility when MU_x / P_x = MU_y / P_y for all goods x, y. The marginal utility per dollar is equalized across goods.
- **Consumer Surplus**: The difference between what a consumer is willing to pay for a good and what they actually pay. Graphically, it is the area above the price line and below the demand curve.
- **Indifference Curve**: A curve showing all combinations of two goods that give the consumer the same level of utility. Higher curves represent higher utility.
- **Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS)**: The rate at which a consumer is willing to trade one good for another while maintaining the same utility level. MRS = MU_x / MU_y. It is the slope of the indifference curve.
- **Budget Line**: A line showing all combinations of two goods a consumer can afford given income and prices. Slope = -P_x / P_y.
- **Consumer Equilibrium**: The point where the budget line is tangent to the highest attainable indifference curve. At this point, MRS = P_x / P_y.
- **Income Effect**: The change in quantity demanded resulting from a change in real purchasing power (due to a price change).
- **Substitution Effect**: The change in quantity demanded resulting from a change in relative prices, holding real income constant.

## Core Concepts

### Diminishing Marginal Utility and Demand

The law of diminishing marginal utility provides the theoretical basis for the downward-sloping demand curve. As you consume more pizza slices, each additional slice gives less satisfaction. Therefore, you are only willing to pay a lower price for each additional slice. This generates the inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded.

### The Utility-Maximizing Rule

A rational consumer with a fixed budget allocates spending so that the last dollar spent on each good yields the same marginal utility. If MU_x/P_x > MU_y/P_y, the consumer should buy more of good X and less of good Y (getting more utility per dollar from X). Reallocation continues until the ratios equalize. This is the mathematical condition for utility maximization.

### Indifference Curves and Budget Lines (Intermediate Level)

Indifference curves are convex to the origin (reflecting diminishing MRS) and cannot cross. The consumer chooses the point on the budget line that reaches the highest indifference curve -- this tangency point is the optimal consumption bundle where MRS = price ratio. Changes in income shift the budget line (parallel shift); changes in a price pivot the budget line.

### Income and Substitution Effects

When the price of a good falls, two effects occur simultaneously. The substitution effect: the good is now relatively cheaper, so the consumer substitutes toward it (always increases quantity). The income effect: the consumer's real purchasing power has increased, and for normal goods this further increases quantity demanded (for inferior goods, the income effect partially offsets the substitution effect). For Giffen goods (extremely rare), the income effect dominates and quantity demanded falls when price falls.

## Examples

### Example: Water vs. Diamond Paradox

Water is essential for life but cheap; diamonds are non-essential but expensive. Consumer theory resolves this paradox: what determines price is marginal utility, not total utility. Water has high total utility but low marginal utility (because it is abundant). Diamonds have lower total utility but high marginal utility (because they are scarce). Prices reflect marginal value, not total value.

### Example: All-You-Can-Eat Buffet and Diminishing MU

At a buffet, the first plate of food provides tremendous satisfaction. The second plate is still enjoyable but less so. By the third or fourth plate, marginal utility may approach zero or even become negative (you feel sick). This illustrates diminishing marginal utility and explains why people voluntarily stop eating even when additional food is "free."

### Example: Consumer Surplus at a Concert

You are willing to pay $150 for a front-row concert ticket, but the ticket costs $80. Your consumer surplus is $70 -- the difference between your maximum willingness to pay and the actual price. Aggregated across all ticket buyers, total consumer surplus is the area between the demand curve and the price line. This measures the net benefit to consumers from participating in the market.

## Relationships

- Diminishing Marginal Utility -> Downward-sloping Demand Curve: Consumers pay less for additional units
- Budget Constraint + Preferences -> Optimal Consumption Bundle: Tangency of budget line and indifference curve
- Price Change -> Income Effect + Substitution Effect: Two channels through which price changes affect quantity demanded
- Consumer Surplus -> Demand Curve: Area below demand curve and above price measures consumer benefit
- MRS = Price Ratio -> Consumer Equilibrium: The optimality condition for consumption choices
- Equi-Marginal Principle -> Rational Allocation: Explains how consumers distribute spending across goods

## Common Misconceptions

- **"Utility can be measured precisely in numbers"**: Disputed. Cardinal utility assigns specific numbers; ordinal utility (the modern approach) only requires ranking preferences. We don't need to say "pizza gives me 50 utils" -- only that I prefer pizza to salad.
- **"More is always better"**: Not necessarily. Marginal utility can become negative (eating too much, owning too many of the same item). The relevant range is where MU > 0.
- **"Consumer surplus means the consumer got a deal or discount"**: Not exactly. Consumer surplus measures the difference between willingness to pay and market price. It exists for every buyer who values the good more than the market price, even at full price.
- **"Indifference curves can cross"**: Never. If two indifference curves crossed, it would create a logical contradiction about preferences (the same bundle would be both preferred and equally valued compared to another bundle).
- **"The substitution effect can go in either direction"**: Wrong. The substitution effect always increases quantity demanded when price falls (the good is relatively cheaper). It is the income effect that can go either direction depending on whether the good is normal or inferior.
