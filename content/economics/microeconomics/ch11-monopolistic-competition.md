# Monopolistic Competition

## Overview
Monopolistic competition combines elements of monopoly and competition. Many firms sell differentiated products (different brands, styles, locations), giving each some market power. However, free entry and exit drive long-run economic profit to zero. Firms compete on price, quality, and advertising. This is the most common market structure -- restaurants, clothing stores, apps, and personal services all fit this model.

## Key Terms
- **Monopolistic Competition**: A market with many sellers, differentiated products, free entry/exit, and some market power.
- **Product Differentiation**: Making a product distinct from competitors through quality, features, branding, location, or service.
- **Brand Loyalty**: Consumer preference for a particular brand, giving the firm some pricing power.
- **Excess Capacity**: In long-run equilibrium, monopolistically competitive firms produce below the output at minimum ATC. They could produce more at lower average cost but don't because demand is limited.
- **Markup**: Price exceeds marginal cost (P > MC). The gap reflects the firm's market power from differentiation.
- **Advertising**: Spending to inform consumers or create brand loyalty. Shifts the firm's demand curve right and may make it less elastic.

## Core Concepts

### Short Run vs Long Run
Short run: like a monopolist. Firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve, produces where MR = MC, can earn economic profit. Long run: profit attracts entry of new firms with similar (but differentiated) products. Each existing firm's demand curve shifts left as customers switch. Entry continues until P = ATC and economic profit = 0.

### Long-Run Equilibrium
The demand curve is tangent to the ATC curve. P = ATC (zero economic profit), but P > MC (markup exists). The firm produces below minimum ATC (excess capacity). This means monopolistic competition is less efficient than perfect competition but provides product variety.

### The Role of Advertising
Firms spend on advertising to differentiate their products, build brand loyalty, and shift demand right. Critics argue advertising is wasteful (raises costs passed to consumers). Defenders argue it provides information and signals quality.

## Examples

### Coffee Shops
Many coffee shops in a city (Starbucks, Dunkin', local cafes) sell differentiated products (different ambiance, quality, location). Each has some pricing power but faces competition. If one shop earns high profits, new shops enter nearby, drawing away customers until profits are eliminated.

### Smartphone Apps
The App Store has thousands of apps in each category. Each is somewhat unique (different UI, features), giving some pricing power. But if an app category is profitable, new apps flood in, fragmenting demand until most earn minimal profit.

## Relationships
- Product differentiation -> Downward-sloping demand (some market power)
- Free entry -> Long-run zero economic profit
- P > MC -> Markup (allocative inefficiency)
- Produce below min ATC -> Excess capacity (productive inefficiency)
- Excess capacity = "cost" of product variety

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Zero profit means firms are struggling"**: Like perfect competition, zero economic profit means firms earn a normal return covering all opportunity costs. Accounting profit is positive.
- **"Monopolistic competition is the same as monopoly"**: Key difference: free entry/exit eliminates long-run profit in monopolistic competition. Monopolists maintain profits due to barriers.
- **"Excess capacity is always bad"**: It's the cost of having product variety. Society values having different restaurants, brands, and styles, even if each operates slightly below full efficiency.
