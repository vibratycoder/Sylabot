# Supply and Demand

## Overview

Supply and demand is the central model of market economics. It explains how prices are determined in competitive markets and how those prices allocate scarce resources. The demand curve represents consumers' willingness and ability to buy at various prices; the supply curve represents producers' willingness and ability to sell at various prices.

The intersection of supply and demand determines the equilibrium price and quantity -- the point where the market "clears" with no surplus or shortage. When external factors shift either curve, a new equilibrium emerges. Understanding the difference between a movement along a curve (caused by a price change) and a shift of the curve (caused by non-price factors) is essential.

This model applies to virtually every market: goods, services, labor, financial assets, housing, and more. It is the single most important tool in microeconomics.

## Key Terms

- **Demand**: The relationship between the price of a good and the quantity consumers are willing and able to purchase, ceteris paribus.
- **Quantity Demanded (Qd)**: The specific amount consumers wish to buy at a particular price.
- **Law of Demand**: As the price of a good rises, quantity demanded falls (and vice versa), ceteris paribus. Demand curves slope downward.
- **Demand Schedule**: A table showing the quantity demanded at each price level.
- **Demand Curve**: A graphical representation of the demand schedule; typically slopes downward from left to right.
- **Determinants of Demand (Demand Shifters)**: Factors other than price that change demand -- income, tastes/preferences, prices of related goods (substitutes and complements), number of buyers, expectations of future prices.
- **Normal Good**: A good for which demand increases when income rises (e.g., restaurant meals).
- **Inferior Good**: A good for which demand decreases when income rises (e.g., instant ramen for many consumers).
- **Substitutes**: Goods that can replace each other; an increase in the price of one raises demand for the other (e.g., Pepsi and Coca-Cola).
- **Complements**: Goods used together; an increase in the price of one decreases demand for the other (e.g., printers and ink cartridges).
- **Supply**: The relationship between the price of a good and the quantity producers are willing and able to offer for sale, ceteris paribus.
- **Quantity Supplied (Qs)**: The specific amount producers wish to sell at a particular price.
- **Law of Supply**: As the price of a good rises, quantity supplied rises (and vice versa), ceteris paribus. Supply curves slope upward.
- **Determinants of Supply (Supply Shifters)**: Factors other than price that change supply -- input prices, technology, number of sellers, expectations, taxes/subsidies, natural events.
- **Equilibrium**: The price and quantity where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. No tendency for change.
- **Equilibrium Price (Market-Clearing Price)**: The price at which Qd = Qs.
- **Surplus (Excess Supply)**: When the price is above equilibrium, Qs > Qd. Sellers cannot sell all they wish, putting downward pressure on price.
- **Shortage (Excess Demand)**: When the price is below equilibrium, Qd > Qs. Buyers cannot buy all they wish, putting upward pressure on price.

## Core Concepts

### Movement Along vs. Shift of a Curve

A change in the good's own price causes a movement along the existing curve (change in quantity demanded or quantity supplied). A change in any non-price determinant causes the entire curve to shift (change in demand or change in supply). This distinction is tested constantly. Example: If the price of coffee falls, there is a movement along the demand curve for coffee (increase in quantity demanded). If consumer income rises, the demand curve for coffee shifts rightward (increase in demand).

### Equilibrium and Market Adjustment

Markets naturally move toward equilibrium through the price mechanism. If price is too high (surplus), unsold goods incentivize sellers to lower prices, which increases Qd and decreases Qs until equilibrium is restored. If price is too low (shortage), competition among buyers pushes prices up, which decreases Qd and increases Qs until equilibrium is restored. This self-correcting process is Adam Smith's "invisible hand."

### Shifts in Demand and Supply -- Predicting New Equilibria

When demand increases (shifts right), both equilibrium price and quantity rise. When supply increases (shifts right), equilibrium price falls and quantity rises. When both curves shift simultaneously, the effect on either price or quantity becomes ambiguous depending on the relative magnitudes of the shifts. Students must be able to work through all four cases: demand increase, demand decrease, supply increase, supply decrease -- and the combined shifts.

### The Role of Prices as Signals

Prices convey information. A rising price signals to producers that consumers want more of a good (incentivizing increased production) and signals to consumers that the good is becoming scarcer (incentivizing conservation). This information function of prices is what makes market economies efficient at allocating resources without central planning.

## Examples

### Example: Gasoline Market After a Hurricane

A hurricane damages Gulf Coast oil refineries, reducing the supply of gasoline. The supply curve shifts left. At the original price, there is a shortage -- consumers want more gas than is available. The price rises until a new equilibrium is reached at a higher price and lower quantity. This also explains why "price gouging" laws (price ceilings during emergencies) can worsen shortages by preventing the price mechanism from rationing scarce supplies.

### Example: Smartphones and Complementary Goods

When Apple releases a new iPhone at a lower entry price, demand for the iPhone increases (movement along the demand curve due to price drop). Simultaneously, demand for iPhone cases, screen protectors, and AirPods (complements) shifts rightward because more people now own iPhones. Meanwhile, demand for Samsung phones (a substitute) may shift leftward as consumers switch.

### Example: Avocado Market Boom

Rising health consciousness (change in tastes) shifted the demand for avocados rightward over the 2010s. Simultaneously, droughts in California and Mexico (a supply-side shock) shifted the supply curve leftward. Both effects pushed the price of avocados up dramatically. The effect on quantity was ambiguous -- demand wanted more, supply constraints wanted less.

## Relationships

- Law of Demand + Law of Supply -> Equilibrium: The intersection determines market price and quantity
- Income changes -> Demand shifts: Normal goods (demand increases with income), inferior goods (demand decreases with income)
- Technology improvements -> Supply shifts right: Lower production costs increase supply
- Surplus -> Price falls -> Movement along both curves toward equilibrium
- Shortage -> Price rises -> Movement along both curves toward equilibrium
- Substitute price increase -> Demand for this good increases (shift right)
- Complement price increase -> Demand for this good decreases (shift left)

## Common Misconceptions

- **"A change in price shifts the demand/supply curve"**: Wrong. A price change causes a movement ALONG the curve. Only non-price factors shift the curve itself.
- **"Equilibrium means the market is fair"**: Wrong. Equilibrium is where Qd = Qs. It says nothing about fairness, equity, or whether the price is affordable.
- **"Supply means the same as quantity supplied"**: Wrong. Supply is the entire relationship (the curve). Quantity supplied is a specific point on that curve at a given price.
- **"Demand means want"**: Wrong. Demand requires both willingness AND ability to pay. A desire without purchasing power is not economic demand.
- **"Price floors and ceilings help markets reach equilibrium"**: Wrong. Price controls prevent markets from reaching equilibrium and create surpluses (floors above equilibrium) or shortages (ceilings below equilibrium).
