# Supply and Demand

## Overview
Supply and demand is the core model of how markets work. Buyers (demand) and sellers (supply) interact in markets, and their interactions determine the price and quantity of goods traded. The demand curve shows how much buyers want at each price; the supply curve shows how much sellers offer. Where they intersect is equilibrium -- the price at which quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. Understanding what shifts these curves and how markets reach equilibrium is essential to all of economics.

## Key Terms
- **Demand**: The relationship between a good's price and the quantity consumers are willing and able to buy, ceteris paribus.
- **Law of Demand**: As price rises, quantity demanded falls (and vice versa), ceteris paribus. The demand curve slopes downward.
- **Demand Curve**: A graph showing quantity demanded at each price. Slopes downward from left to right.
- **Quantity Demanded**: The specific amount buyers want to purchase at a particular price. A single point on the demand curve.
- **Supply**: The relationship between a good's price and the quantity producers are willing and able to sell, ceteris paribus.
- **Law of Supply**: As price rises, quantity supplied rises (and vice versa), ceteris paribus. The supply curve slopes upward.
- **Supply Curve**: A graph showing quantity supplied at each price. Slopes upward from left to right.
- **Quantity Supplied**: The specific amount sellers want to produce at a particular price. A single point on the supply curve.
- **Equilibrium**: The point where the supply and demand curves intersect. At this price, quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. No tendency to change.
- **Equilibrium Price**: The price at which the market clears (Qd = Qs). Also called the market-clearing price.
- **Equilibrium Quantity**: The quantity bought and sold at the equilibrium price.
- **Shortage (Excess Demand)**: When price is below equilibrium, quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied. Buyers compete, pushing price up.
- **Surplus (Excess Supply)**: When price is above equilibrium, quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded. Sellers compete, pushing price down.
- **Normal Good**: A good for which demand increases when income rises (e.g., restaurant meals).
- **Inferior Good**: A good for which demand decreases when income rises (e.g., instant ramen for many consumers).
- **Substitute**: A good that can replace another. If the price of Coke rises, demand for Pepsi increases.
- **Complement**: A good consumed with another. If the price of printers rises, demand for ink cartridges falls.
- **Shift vs Movement**: A shift is when the entire curve moves (due to a non-price determinant changing). A movement along the curve is caused by a change in the good's own price.

## Core Concepts

### Determinants of Demand (Demand Shifters)
The demand curve shifts when any non-price factor changes:
1. **Income** -- Normal goods: income up -> demand up. Inferior goods: income up -> demand down.
2. **Prices of related goods** -- Substitutes: price of substitute up -> demand up. Complements: price of complement up -> demand down.
3. **Tastes and preferences** -- Fashion, advertising, trends shift demand.
4. **Expectations** -- If consumers expect prices to rise, demand increases now.
5. **Number of buyers** -- More buyers in the market -> demand increases.

### Determinants of Supply (Supply Shifters)
The supply curve shifts when any non-price factor changes:
1. **Input prices** -- Higher input costs -> supply decreases (shifts left).
2. **Technology** -- Better technology -> supply increases (shifts right).
3. **Expectations** -- If producers expect higher prices, they may withhold supply now.
4. **Number of sellers** -- More firms entering -> supply increases.
5. **Government policy** -- Taxes decrease supply; subsidies increase supply.
6. **Natural events** -- Weather, disasters can decrease supply.

### How Markets Reach Equilibrium
If price is above equilibrium, surplus forms. Sellers can't sell all their goods, so they lower prices. If price is below equilibrium, shortage forms. Buyers can't get all they want, so they bid prices up. The market naturally gravitates toward equilibrium.

### Analyzing Shifts
When a demand or supply shifter changes: (1) Identify which curve shifts, (2) Determine the direction of the shift, (3) Find the new equilibrium. If demand increases (shifts right), equilibrium price and quantity both rise. If supply increases (shifts right), equilibrium price falls and quantity rises.

## Examples

### Gas Prices After a Hurricane
A hurricane damages Gulf Coast oil refineries (supply shifter: natural event). Supply of gasoline decreases (shifts left). At the original price, there's a shortage. Price rises and quantity falls until a new equilibrium is reached.

### Smartphones and Phone Cases
Smartphones and phone cases are complements. If the price of smartphones drops (movement along demand), more smartphones are purchased. This increases the demand for phone cases (shift right of case demand curve), raising both the price and quantity of cases sold.

### Avocado Toast Trend
Social media drives a trend making avocado toast extremely popular (taste/preference shift). Demand for avocados increases (shifts right). Price and quantity of avocados rise. Simultaneously, avocado farmers invest in more production (supply eventually shifts right too), partially offsetting the price increase.

## Relationships
- Price increase -> Quantity demanded decreases (movement along demand curve -- Law of Demand)
- Price increase -> Quantity supplied increases (movement along supply curve -- Law of Supply)
- Demand shift right -> Equilibrium price up, equilibrium quantity up
- Supply shift right -> Equilibrium price down, equilibrium quantity up
- Shortage -> Price rises toward equilibrium
- Surplus -> Price falls toward equilibrium

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Demand means how much people want something"**: Demand is willingness AND ability to pay at each price, not just desire. You might want a Ferrari, but if you can't afford it, it's not part of demand.
- **"A price increase shifts the demand curve left"**: No. A change in the good's own price causes a movement along the demand curve, not a shift. Only non-price factors shift the curve.
- **"Supply means the amount available"**: Supply is the entire relationship between price and quantity offered, not a fixed amount.
- **"Markets always reach equilibrium instantly"**: Adjustment takes time. In the real world, prices may be sticky due to contracts, menu costs, or regulations.
