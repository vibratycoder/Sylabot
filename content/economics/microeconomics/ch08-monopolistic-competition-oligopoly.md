# Monopolistic Competition and Oligopoly

## Overview

Most real-world markets fall between the extremes of perfect competition and monopoly. Monopolistic competition features many firms selling differentiated products with free entry/exit -- think restaurants, clothing brands, or hair salons. Oligopoly features a few dominant firms whose decisions are interdependent -- think airlines, auto manufacturers, or wireless carriers.

Monopolistic competition combines elements of competition (many firms, free entry) with elements of monopoly (downward-sloping demand due to product differentiation). In the long run, entry drives economic profit to zero, but firms do not achieve productive or allocative efficiency.

Oligopoly is characterized by strategic interaction -- each firm must consider rivals' reactions when making decisions. Game theory (especially the prisoner's dilemma) is the primary analytical tool. Oligopolists may collude (forming cartels) or compete aggressively.

These topics connect to Bryant courses ECO 113, ECO 313, ECO 316 (Game Theory), and ECO 363 (Industrial Organization).

## Key Terms

- **Monopolistic Competition**: A market with many sellers, differentiated products, and free entry/exit. Each firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve but earns zero economic profit in the long run.
- **Product Differentiation**: Making a product distinct from competitors' through quality, features, branding, location, or service. This gives each firm limited market power.
- **Excess Capacity**: In monopolistic competition, firms produce less than the output that would minimize ATC. The gap between actual output and minimum-ATC output is excess capacity -- an inefficiency of this market structure.
- **Non-Price Competition**: Competition through advertising, branding, quality improvements, and customer service rather than price reductions.
- **Oligopoly**: A market dominated by a few large firms whose decisions are interdependent. Products may be identical (steel) or differentiated (cars).
- **Strategic Interaction (Mutual Interdependence)**: The defining feature of oligopoly -- each firm's optimal strategy depends on what rivals do.
- **Game Theory**: The study of strategic decision-making. Used to model oligopoly behavior.
- **Nash Equilibrium**: An outcome where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, given the other players' strategies.
- **Prisoner's Dilemma**: A game where the individually rational strategy for each player leads to a collectively suboptimal outcome. In oligopoly: each firm has an incentive to cheat on a collusive agreement, even though both firms would be better off cooperating.
- **Cartel**: A group of firms that explicitly collude to set prices and restrict output (e.g., OPEC). Cartels are illegal in the US under antitrust law.
- **Collusion**: An agreement (explicit or tacit) among firms to restrict competition by fixing prices, dividing markets, or limiting output.
- **Kinked Demand Curve Model**: A model explaining price rigidity in oligopoly. If a firm raises its price, rivals do not follow (firm loses many customers). If it lowers its price, rivals match (firm gains few customers). This creates a "kink" at the current price and a gap in the MR curve.
- **Concentration Ratio**: The percentage of market sales accounted for by the largest firms (e.g., four-firm concentration ratio).
- **Contestable Market**: A market where potential entry disciplines incumbent firms, even if few firms currently operate. Low barriers to entry make the market contestable.

## Core Concepts

### Monopolistic Competition: Short Run vs Long Run

In the short run, a monopolistically competitive firm can earn economic profit (if P > ATC at MR = MC output). This attracts new entrants who offer similar differentiated products, shifting each firm's demand curve leftward (taking away customers). Entry continues until each firm's demand curve is tangent to its ATC curve, yielding zero economic profit. At this point, P = ATC (zero profit) but P > MC (allocative inefficiency) and output < minimum ATC (excess capacity, productive inefficiency).

### Oligopoly and the Prisoner's Dilemma

Consider two firms deciding whether to set high or low prices. If both set high prices, both earn $10M. If both set low prices, both earn $5M. If one sets low while the other sets high, the low-price firm earns $12M and the high-price firm earns $2M. Each firm's dominant strategy is to set the low price (regardless of what the rival does), leading to the Nash equilibrium of ($5M, $5M) -- even though both would prefer ($10M, $10M). This explains why cartels are unstable: each member has an incentive to cheat.

### Game Theory and Strategic Thinking

Game theory analyzes situations where outcomes depend on the strategies of multiple players. Key concepts: dominant strategy (best response regardless of rival's action), Nash equilibrium (mutual best responses), repeated games (the threat of future punishment can sustain cooperation), credible threats (a threat that is in the player's self-interest to carry out). These tools are central to ECO 316 at Bryant.

### Why Cartels are Unstable

OPEC illustrates cartel instability. Members agree to restrict oil output to keep prices high. But each member has an incentive to secretly produce above its quota (capturing high prices without bearing the full output restriction). When enough members cheat, total output rises, price falls, and the cartel's effectiveness collapses. This is the prisoner's dilemma applied to real-world collusion.

## Examples

### Example: Fast Food as Monopolistic Competition

McDonald's, Burger King, Wendy's, Chick-fil-A, and hundreds of local burger joints represent monopolistic competition. Products are similar but differentiated (menu items, branding, quality, location). Each firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve. Free entry means new restaurants open when profits are high and close when losses occur. In equilibrium, each restaurant earns approximately zero economic profit but has some excess capacity (could serve more customers at lower per-unit cost).

### Example: US Wireless Carriers as Oligopoly

The US wireless market is dominated by three carriers (AT&T, Verizon, T-Mobile) controlling about 98% of subscribers. Each firm's pricing and investment decisions directly affect rivals. When T-Mobile introduced unlimited data plans at lower prices, AT&T and Verizon were forced to respond. This strategic interdependence is the hallmark of oligopoly. High infrastructure costs create barriers to entry, keeping the market concentrated.

### Example: OPEC as a Cartel

OPEC member nations agree on production quotas to keep oil prices high. When compliance is strong, oil prices rise. But members like Iran or Nigeria often exceed quotas to earn more revenue, increasing supply and undermining the cartel. Saudi Arabia sometimes acts as the "swing producer," adjusting its own output to enforce discipline. This recurring pattern illustrates the prisoner's dilemma: cooperation is profitable but individually unstable.

## Relationships

- Product Differentiation -> Downward-sloping demand -> Limited market power (monopolistic competition)
- Free Entry -> Zero long-run economic profit (monopolistic competition)
- Excess Capacity -> Productive inefficiency: firms do not produce at minimum ATC
- Strategic Interdependence -> Game Theory needed to analyze oligopoly
- Prisoner's Dilemma -> Cartels are unstable: individual incentive to cheat
- Repeated Games -> Cooperation possible: threat of retaliation sustains collusion
- Concentration Ratio -> Measures market power: higher = more oligopolistic
- Contestable Markets -> Potential entry disciplines pricing even in concentrated markets

## Common Misconceptions

- **"Monopolistic competition is a combination of monopoly and perfect competition"**: Misleading. It is its own market structure with unique features (differentiated products, many firms, free entry, zero long-run profit but with excess capacity).
- **"Oligopolists always collude"**: Wrong. Collusion is illegal in most countries and unstable even when attempted. Oligopolists often compete fiercely (price wars, advertising battles).
- **"The prisoner's dilemma means players can never cooperate"**: In one-shot games, yes -- the dominant strategy is to defect. But in repeated games, cooperation can emerge through strategies like tit-for-tat (cooperate until the rival defects, then punish).
- **"Product differentiation wastes resources"**: Debatable. While advertising costs and excess capacity are inefficiencies, consumers value variety, choice, and innovation that differentiation provides.
- **"Nash equilibrium is the best outcome"**: Not necessarily. It is the outcome where no player can unilaterally improve -- but it can be collectively suboptimal (as in the prisoner's dilemma).
