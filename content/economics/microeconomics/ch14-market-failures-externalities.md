# Market Failures and Externalities

## Overview
Markets are efficient under ideal conditions, but several situations cause market failure -- where the free market produces too much or too little of a good. Externalities (costs or benefits imposed on third parties), public goods (non-excludable, non-rival), common resources (tragedy of the commons), and asymmetric information all cause markets to fail. Government intervention through taxes, subsidies, regulation, or property rights can potentially improve outcomes.

## Key Terms
- **Market Failure**: When the free market fails to allocate resources efficiently, producing too much or too little of a good.
- **Externality**: A cost or benefit that affects someone not involved in the transaction. Can be positive or negative.
- **Negative Externality**: A cost imposed on third parties (pollution, secondhand smoke, noise). Market produces too much.
- **Positive Externality**: A benefit conferred on third parties (education, vaccines, R&D). Market produces too little.
- **Social Cost**: Private cost + external cost. The true cost to society.
- **Pigouvian Tax**: A tax equal to the external cost, designed to internalize a negative externality and restore efficiency.
- **Subsidy**: A payment to encourage production of goods with positive externalities.
- **Coase Theorem**: If property rights are well-defined and transaction costs are low, private bargaining will lead to an efficient outcome regardless of the initial allocation of rights.
- **Public Good**: A good that is non-excludable (can't prevent people from using it) and non-rival (one person's use doesn't reduce availability). Examples: national defense, street lights.
- **Free Rider Problem**: People benefit from a public good without paying. This leads to under-provision by private markets.
- **Common Resource**: A good that is rival but non-excludable (fisheries, public grazing land). Leads to overuse -- the tragedy of the commons.
- **Tragedy of the Commons**: When individuals overuse a shared resource because no one bears the full cost of their use.
- **Asymmetric Information**: When one party to a transaction has more information than the other.
- **Moral Hazard**: When a party takes on more risk because someone else bears the cost (insurance encourages risky behavior).
- **Adverse Selection**: When asymmetric information causes the market to disproportionately attract high-risk participants (sick people buy more insurance).

## Core Concepts

### Negative Externalities
When production creates negative externalities (pollution), the social cost exceeds the private cost. The market produces more than the socially optimal quantity. Solutions: Pigouvian tax (shifts supply up to reflect social cost), cap-and-trade (create a market for pollution permits), regulation (emissions standards), or Coase bargaining.

### Positive Externalities
When consumption creates positive externalities (education, vaccines), the social benefit exceeds the private benefit. The market produces less than the socially optimal quantity. Solutions: subsidies (shift demand up), public provision, or mandates.

### Public Goods and Free Riders
Because public goods are non-excludable, people can enjoy them without paying. Rational self-interest leads to under-provision. Government typically provides public goods funded by taxes (national defense, roads, basic research).

## Examples

### Carbon Emissions and Climate Change
Burning fossil fuels creates a negative externality: CO2 emissions cause climate change affecting everyone. A carbon tax (Pigouvian tax) makes emitters pay the social cost, incentivizing cleaner energy. Cap-and-trade systems (EU ETS) achieve similar results by limiting total emissions and allowing permit trading.

### Vaccination
Getting vaccinated provides a positive externality: it protects not just the individual but also those who can't be vaccinated (herd immunity). Without subsidies or mandates, too few people get vaccinated because they don't account for the benefit to others.

### Overfishing
Ocean fish are a common resource -- rival (each fish caught leaves fewer for others) but non-excludable (hard to prevent fishing in open ocean). Each fisher takes as much as possible, leading to depletion. Solutions: fishing quotas, property rights (individual transferable quotas), international agreements.

## Relationships
- Negative externality -> Social cost > Private cost -> Overproduction
- Positive externality -> Social benefit > Private benefit -> Underproduction
- Pigouvian tax -> Internalizes negative externality -> Efficient output
- Non-excludable + non-rival -> Public good -> Free rider problem -> Government provision
- Rival + non-excludable -> Common resource -> Tragedy of commons -> Regulation needed

## Common Misconceptions
- **"All pollution should be eliminated"**: The optimal level of pollution is not zero. Some pollution is a byproduct of valuable production. The goal is to produce where social marginal cost equals social marginal benefit.
- **"Government always fixes market failures"**: Government failure is also possible. Regulations may be captured by special interests, politicians may be poorly informed, and bureaucracies can be inefficient.
- **"The Coase theorem means we don't need government"**: Coase requires low transaction costs and well-defined property rights -- conditions rarely met for large-scale externalities like climate change.
