# Oligopoly and Game Theory

## Overview
An oligopoly is a market dominated by a few large firms whose actions are interdependent -- each firm's decisions affect and are affected by competitors. This strategic interaction is what makes oligopoly unique and is analyzed using game theory. Firms may collude (act like a monopoly) or compete (driving prices toward competition). The Prisoner's Dilemma shows why collusion is unstable -- each firm has an incentive to cheat. OPEC, airlines, and tech giants are real-world oligopolies.

## Key Terms
- **Oligopoly**: A market structure with a few dominant firms, interdependent decision-making, and barriers to entry.
- **Strategic Interaction**: Each firm's optimal strategy depends on what other firms do.
- **Game Theory**: A framework for analyzing strategic decision-making where outcomes depend on the actions of all players.
- **Prisoner's Dilemma**: A game where two players each have a dominant strategy that leads to a worse outcome for both than if they cooperated. Illustrates why cartels break down.
- **Nash Equilibrium**: An outcome where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy. Both players are best-responding to each other.
- **Dominant Strategy**: A strategy that is best for a player regardless of what the other player does.
- **Collusion**: An agreement among firms to restrict output and raise prices (act like a monopoly). Illegal in most countries.
- **Cartel**: A formal agreement among firms to collude. OPEC is the most famous example.
- **Duopoly**: An oligopoly with exactly two firms.
- **Kinked Demand Curve**: A model explaining price rigidity in oligopolies. Firms believe rivals will match price cuts but not price increases, creating a kink.
- **Concentration Ratio**: The percentage of market output produced by the largest firms (e.g., 4-firm concentration ratio).

## Core Concepts

### The Prisoner's Dilemma Applied to Firms
Two firms (A and B) each choose to cooperate (restrict output) or defect (produce more). If both cooperate, they earn high profits (like a shared monopoly). If one defects while the other cooperates, the defector gets even higher profits while the cooperator loses. If both defect, both earn low profits (competitive outcome). The dominant strategy is to defect -- so both end up worse than if they'd cooperated. This explains why cartels are unstable.

### Nash Equilibrium
In a Nash equilibrium, each player's strategy is the best response to the other's. In the Prisoner's Dilemma, the Nash equilibrium is (Defect, Defect) even though (Cooperate, Cooperate) would be better for both. Finding Nash equilibria: for each cell, check if either player would want to switch; if neither would, it's a Nash equilibrium.

### Cartels and Cheating
Cartels agree to restrict output to keep prices high. But each member has an incentive to cheat -- secretly produce more to capture extra revenue at the high price. If everyone cheats, the cartel collapses and prices drop to competitive levels. This is why cartels are inherently unstable (OPEC struggles with cheating members).

## Examples

### OPEC
OPEC (Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries) is a cartel of oil-producing nations. They agree on production quotas to keep oil prices high. But individual members (like Saudi Arabia vs Venezuela) often cheat by exceeding quotas. When cheating is widespread, oil prices fall.

### Coca-Cola vs Pepsi
The soft drink industry is a duopoly. Each company's advertising, pricing, and product launches directly affect the other. They compete fiercely on branding and marketing but avoid destructive price wars (which would hurt both). Their behavior is best analyzed using game theory.

## Relationships
- Few firms + barriers -> Oligopoly
- Strategic interaction -> Game theory analysis required
- Prisoner's Dilemma -> Collusion is unstable (incentive to cheat)
- Dominant strategy for each firm to defect -> Nash equilibrium at lower profits
- Repeated games -> Cooperation more sustainable (tit-for-tat strategies)
- Cartels -> Higher prices -> Incentive to cheat -> Cartel instability

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Oligopolies always collude"**: Collusion is difficult to maintain (incentive to cheat), illegal in many countries, and requires monitoring. Many oligopolies compete intensely.
- **"Nash equilibrium is the best outcome"**: The Nash equilibrium is stable, not optimal. In the Prisoner's Dilemma, the Nash equilibrium is worse for both players than mutual cooperation.
- **"Game theory only applies to two players"**: Game theory applies to any number of strategic players, though analysis gets more complex.
