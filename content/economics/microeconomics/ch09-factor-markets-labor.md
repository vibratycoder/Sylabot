# Factor Markets and Labor Economics

## Overview

Factor markets (also called resource or input markets) are markets where firms purchase the factors of production -- labor, capital, land, and entrepreneurship. The labor market is the most important factor market and receives the most attention in economics courses.

In factor markets, the roles are reversed from product markets: firms are the buyers (demanding labor) and households are the sellers (supplying labor). The demand for labor is a derived demand -- firms want workers not for their own sake but because workers produce goods that generate revenue. A firm hires workers up to the point where the marginal revenue product (MRP) of the last worker equals the wage (MRC).

This topic connects to Bryant courses ECO 113, ECO 313, and especially ECO 463 (Labor Economics).

## Key Terms

- **Factor Markets (Resource Markets)**: Markets where factors of production (labor, capital, land, entrepreneurship) are bought and sold.
- **Derived Demand**: The demand for a factor of production is derived from (depends on) the demand for the product it produces. If demand for cars increases, demand for autoworkers increases.
- **Marginal Revenue Product (MRP)**: The additional revenue generated by hiring one more unit of a factor. MRP = Marginal Product x Marginal Revenue. For competitive firms, MRP = MP x Price.
- **Marginal Resource Cost (MRC)**: The additional cost of hiring one more unit of a factor. In a competitive labor market, MRC = the wage rate.
- **Profit-Maximizing Hiring Rule**: Hire workers until MRP = MRC (= wage in competitive labor markets). If MRP > wage, hire more; if MRP < wage, hire fewer.
- **Labor Demand Curve**: The MRP curve IS the firm's labor demand curve. It slopes downward due to diminishing marginal product.
- **Labor Supply Curve**: Shows the quantity of labor workers are willing to supply at each wage. For the market, it slopes upward (higher wages attract more workers). For an individual, it may bend backward at very high wages (income effect dominates substitution effect).
- **Human Capital**: The skills, education, training, and experience that make workers more productive and command higher wages.
- **Compensating Differentials**: Wage differences that compensate for non-monetary characteristics of jobs. Dangerous, unpleasant, or unstable jobs pay more to attract workers (oil rig workers, garbage collectors).
- **Monopsony**: A labor market with a single buyer of labor (one employer). The monopsonist pays wages below the competitive level and hires fewer workers.
- **Minimum Wage**: A government-imposed price floor in the labor market. If set above the equilibrium wage, it creates a surplus of labor (unemployment). The magnitude of the unemployment effect depends on the elasticity of labor demand and supply.
- **Wage Differentials**: Differences in wages across occupations, industries, or individuals. Explained by human capital, compensating differentials, discrimination, unions, and efficiency wages.
- **Labor Union**: An organization of workers that bargains collectively with employers for higher wages, better benefits, and improved working conditions. Unions can raise wages above the competitive level but may reduce employment.

## Core Concepts

### The MRP = Wage Hiring Rule

A competitive firm hires workers as long as each worker generates more revenue (MRP) than they cost (wage). The 5th worker might have MRP = $25/hour while the wage is $20/hour -- hire them (adds $5 to profit). The 10th worker might have MRP = $18/hour -- do not hire (costs more than they produce). The profit-maximizing number of workers is where MRP = wage. This is the fundamental rule of factor demand.

### Why Labor Demand Slopes Downward

Labor demand slopes downward for two reasons: (1) Diminishing marginal product -- each additional worker produces less additional output (short-run phenomenon). (2) Output effect -- to sell additional output, the firm must lower its product price (if it has market power), reducing MRP further. The MRP curve (= labor demand) shifts when product demand changes, product prices change, or the productivity of labor changes (technology, capital).

### Wage Determination in Different Market Structures

In a competitive labor market, the wage is determined by the intersection of market labor supply and market labor demand. Individual firms are wage takers. In a monopsony, the single employer faces an upward-sloping labor supply curve and sets MRP = MRC (but MRC > wage because hiring one more worker raises the wage for all workers). The monopsony wage is below the competitive wage, and employment is below the competitive level.

### Minimum Wage Debate

In the standard competitive model, a minimum wage above equilibrium creates unemployment (Qd < Qs for labor). However, if the labor market has monopsony power, a moderate minimum wage can actually INCREASE employment (by moving the market from the monopsony outcome toward the competitive outcome). This is why economists debate the employment effects of minimum wage increases -- the real world is not a simple competitive model.

## Examples

### Example: Tech Industry Wages and Human Capital

Software engineers earn high wages because of high human capital (years of training, specialized skills) and high MRP (their code generates massive revenue for tech companies). The demand for software engineers is derived from the demand for software products and services. When AI and cloud computing boomed, derived demand for engineers surged, pushing wages to $150K-300K+ at major firms.

### Example: Amazon Warehouse Monopsony

In small towns where Amazon builds a fulfillment center, it may be the dominant employer -- approaching monopsony. If Amazon is the primary source of warehouse jobs, it has wage-setting power. Workers have few outside options, so Amazon can pay below what a competitive market would dictate. When Amazon voluntarily raised its minimum to $15/hour in 2018, it simultaneously reduced some benefits, illustrating the complex dynamics of monopsony-like labor markets.

## Relationships

- Product Demand -> Derived Demand for Labor: Higher product demand shifts labor demand right
- Diminishing Marginal Product -> Downward-sloping MRP -> Downward-sloping labor demand
- Human Capital Investment -> Higher MP -> Higher MRP -> Higher wages
- Monopsony -> Wage below competitive level -> Employment below competitive level
- Minimum Wage above equilibrium -> Unemployment in competitive markets, potentially higher employment in monopsony
- Technology -> Can increase or decrease labor demand depending on whether it complements or substitutes for labor
- Labor Unions -> Higher wages -> Potentially lower employment (depends on elasticity)

## Common Misconceptions

- **"Firms pay workers what they're worth"**: In competitive markets, firms pay workers their MRP. But in monopsony, workers are paid less than MRP. And MRP itself depends on product demand, not just worker skill.
- **"Higher minimum wage always causes unemployment"**: Only in a competitive labor market. In monopsony conditions, a moderate minimum wage increase can raise both wages and employment.
- **"Education always increases wages proportionally"**: Not guaranteed. Human capital increases earning potential, but the return depends on the field, market demand for those skills, and the supply of similarly educated workers.
- **"Derived demand means labor demand follows product demand exactly"**: The relationship is mediated by marginal product and technology. Automation can reduce labor demand even as product demand rises.
- **"Unions are always good (or always bad) for workers"**: Unions raise wages for members but may reduce employment in the unionized sector. Workers displaced from union jobs increase labor supply in the non-union sector, potentially depressing wages there.
