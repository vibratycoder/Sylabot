# Labor Markets and Factor Markets

## Overview
Factor markets are where firms buy inputs (labor, capital, land). The labor market is the most important factor market. Firms demand labor based on the value workers produce (marginal revenue product). Workers supply labor based on the wage relative to the value of leisure. Wages are determined by supply and demand in the labor market and vary based on human capital, working conditions, discrimination, and market structure.

## Key Terms
- **Derived Demand**: Demand for labor (or any input) is derived from the demand for the product it produces. If demand for cars rises, demand for autoworkers rises.
- **Marginal Revenue Product (MRP)**: The additional revenue from hiring one more worker. MRP = MP x MR (or MP x P in perfect competition).
- **Profit-Maximizing Hiring Rule**: Hire workers until MRP = Wage. If MRP > W, hire more; if MRP < W, hire fewer.
- **Human Capital**: Knowledge, skills, and experience that make workers more productive. Education and training increase human capital and wages.
- **Compensating Differential**: Wage differences that compensate for non-monetary characteristics of jobs. Dangerous, unpleasant, or remote jobs pay more.
- **Monopsony**: A labor market with a single employer. The employer has market power to pay below the competitive wage.
- **Labor Union**: An organization of workers that bargains collectively with employers for wages, benefits, and working conditions.
- **Wage Discrimination**: Paying different wages to equally productive workers based on race, gender, age, or other characteristics.

## Core Concepts

### Labor Demand
A profit-maximizing firm hires until MRP = W. As more workers are hired, diminishing marginal returns cause MRP to fall. The firm's labor demand curve is the downward-sloping MRP curve. Market demand for labor is the horizontal sum of all firms' MRP curves.

### Labor Supply
Individual workers face a tradeoff between income and leisure. Higher wages have two effects: substitution effect (work more because the opportunity cost of leisure rises) and income effect (work less because you're richer). Market labor supply generally slopes upward.

### Why Wages Differ
1. Human capital differences (education, experience, skills)
2. Compensating differentials (risky jobs pay more)
3. Market power (unions raise wages; monopsony lowers wages)
4. Discrimination (wage gaps not explained by productivity)
5. Efficiency wages (firms pay above-market wages to reduce turnover)

## Examples

### College Wage Premium
Workers with a bachelor's degree earn roughly 65% more than those with only a high school diploma. This reflects the human capital investment: college builds skills that increase productivity (MRP), justifying higher wages.

### Why Garbage Collectors Earn More Than Daycare Workers
Despite requiring less education, garbage collectors often earn more due to compensating differentials -- the work is physically demanding, dirty, and involves health risks. Daycare workers, despite valuable work, face excess labor supply (many people enjoy working with children).

## Relationships
- Product demand increases -> Derived demand for labor increases -> Wages rise
- More human capital -> Higher MRP -> Higher wages
- Monopsony -> Wage below competitive level -> May justify minimum wage
- Union bargaining -> Wages above competitive level -> Possible employment reduction
- Discrimination -> Wage gap not explained by productivity differences

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Workers are paid what they deserve"**: Wages reflect marginal revenue product in competitive markets, but market power, discrimination, and institutional factors cause deviations.
- **"Education always pays off"**: On average yes, but it depends on the field, quality of education, and individual ability. The return varies by major and career.
- **"Minimum wage always reduces employment"**: In competitive markets, yes. In monopsony markets, a moderate minimum wage can increase both wages and employment.
