# Elasticity

## Overview

Elasticity measures the responsiveness of one variable to changes in another. While supply and demand tell us the direction of change, elasticity tells us the magnitude. The most important type is price elasticity of demand -- how much quantity demanded changes when price changes.

Elasticity determines critical business and policy outcomes: whether a price increase raises or lowers total revenue, who bears the burden of a tax, and how sensitive consumers are to price changes. Goods with many substitutes, that are luxuries, that represent a large share of the budget, or that consumers have time to adjust to tend to have more elastic demand.

Understanding elasticity connects supply-and-demand theory to practical decision-making for firms, governments, and consumers.

## Key Terms

- **Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)**: The percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price. Measures consumer responsiveness to price changes. Always negative (law of demand) but often expressed as an absolute value.
- **Elastic Demand**: |PED| > 1. Quantity demanded is highly responsive to price changes. A 1% price increase causes more than a 1% decrease in quantity demanded.
- **Inelastic Demand**: |PED| < 1. Quantity demanded is not very responsive to price changes. A 1% price increase causes less than a 1% decrease in quantity demanded.
- **Unit Elastic**: |PED| = 1. The percentage change in quantity demanded exactly equals the percentage change in price.
- **Perfectly Elastic**: |PED| = infinity. Horizontal demand curve. Any price increase causes quantity demanded to fall to zero (e.g., a perfectly competitive firm's product).
- **Perfectly Inelastic**: |PED| = 0. Vertical demand curve. Quantity demanded does not change regardless of price (e.g., life-saving medication with no substitute).
- **Midpoint (Arc Elasticity) Method**: Calculates PED using the average of the two prices and quantities as the base, avoiding the problem of different results depending on direction: PED = [(Q2-Q1)/((Q2+Q1)/2)] / [(P2-P1)/((P2+P1)/2)].
- **Total Revenue Test**: If demand is elastic, a price decrease increases total revenue (TR = P x Q). If demand is inelastic, a price decrease decreases total revenue. If unit elastic, TR is unchanged.
- **Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)**: The percentage change in quantity supplied divided by the percentage change in price. Measures producer responsiveness.
- **Income Elasticity of Demand**: The percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in income. Positive for normal goods, negative for inferior goods. Greater than 1 for luxury goods.
- **Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand**: The percentage change in quantity demanded of good A divided by the percentage change in price of good B. Positive for substitutes, negative for complements, zero for unrelated goods.
- **Tax Incidence**: The division of the tax burden between buyers and sellers. The side with the more inelastic curve bears a larger share of the tax.

## Core Concepts

### Determinants of Price Elasticity of Demand

Four main factors: (1) Availability of close substitutes -- more substitutes means more elastic (butter vs. margarine). (2) Necessity vs. luxury -- necessities are inelastic (insulin), luxuries are elastic (vacation cruises). (3) Proportion of income -- goods that take a larger share of the budget are more elastic (cars vs. salt). (4) Time horizon -- demand becomes more elastic over time as consumers find alternatives (gasoline in the short run vs. long run).

### Total Revenue and Pricing Strategy

The total revenue test is crucial for businesses. A firm selling a product with elastic demand should lower its price to increase total revenue (the gain in quantity sold more than offsets the lower price). A firm selling a product with inelastic demand should raise its price (the loss in quantity sold is small relative to the higher price per unit). This explains why pharmaceutical companies can charge high prices for drugs with no substitutes, and why competitive retailers must keep prices low.

### Tax Incidence and Elasticity

When a government imposes a per-unit tax, the burden does not fall entirely on whoever legally pays the tax. Instead, the burden is shared based on relative elasticities. If demand is more inelastic than supply, buyers bear most of the tax burden (the price they pay rises by nearly the full tax amount). If supply is more inelastic than demand, sellers bear most of the burden. In the extreme, perfectly inelastic demand means buyers pay the entire tax; perfectly elastic demand means sellers pay the entire tax.

### Cross-Price and Income Elasticity Applications

Cross-price elasticity reveals market relationships. If cross-price elasticity between goods A and B is +2.5, they are strong substitutes; if -0.8, they are complements. Income elasticity classifies goods: luxury goods have income elasticity > 1 (demand grows faster than income), necessities have income elasticity between 0 and 1 (demand grows slower than income), and inferior goods have negative income elasticity (demand falls as income rises).

## Examples

### Example: Gasoline Price Elasticity

Studies estimate the short-run PED for gasoline at about -0.2 to -0.3 (very inelastic). People need to drive to work and have limited immediate alternatives. Over the long run, PED increases to around -0.6 to -0.8 as consumers buy fuel-efficient cars, carpool, move closer to work, or switch to public transit. This explains why gas stations can raise prices and still see only small decreases in sales in the short run.

### Example: Netflix and the Total Revenue Test

When Netflix raised its monthly subscription price from $9.99 to $13.99, some subscribers canceled. But because the demand for streaming (with limited close substitutes at the time) was relatively inelastic, the revenue gained from the higher price per subscriber exceeded the revenue lost from cancellations. Total revenue increased. This strategy works only because demand was inelastic at the original price point.

### Example: Cigarette Taxes and Tax Incidence

Governments impose heavy excise taxes on cigarettes. Because cigarette demand is highly inelastic (addiction reduces consumer responsiveness), most of the tax burden falls on consumers in the form of higher prices. A $1 tax per pack might raise the consumer price by $0.90 and reduce what sellers receive by only $0.10. This is why cigarette taxes are effective revenue generators but less effective at reducing consumption.

## Relationships

- Price Elasticity of Demand -> Total Revenue: Determines whether price increases raise or lower TR
- Availability of Substitutes -> Higher Elasticity: More alternatives means consumers switch more easily
- Time Horizon -> Elasticity increases: Consumers adjust behavior over time
- Tax Incidence -> Relative Elasticity: More inelastic side bears more of the tax
- Income Elasticity -> Good Classification: Normal (>0), Inferior (<0), Luxury (>1), Necessity (0-1)
- Cross-Price Elasticity -> Market Relationship: Positive = substitutes, Negative = complements
- Elasticity -> Supply and Demand model: Quantifies the magnitude of responses predicted by the model

## Common Misconceptions

- **"Elastic means demand changes, inelastic means it doesn't"**: Wrong. Inelastic demand still changes -- it just changes proportionally LESS than the price change. Only perfectly inelastic demand shows zero response.
- **"Elasticity is the same as slope"**: Wrong. Elasticity measures percentage changes, slope measures absolute changes. A linear demand curve has a constant slope but varying elasticity along its length (elastic at high prices, inelastic at low prices).
- **"A firm should always lower prices to sell more"**: Wrong. If demand is inelastic, lowering the price reduces total revenue. The optimal pricing strategy depends on elasticity.
- **"The seller always pays a sales tax"**: Wrong. Tax incidence depends on relative elasticities, not on who legally writes the check. The economic burden can fall mostly on buyers.
- **"Luxury goods always have elastic demand"**: Generally true but context-dependent. A luxury good with no close substitutes (e.g., a specific designer brand for a loyal customer) might have inelastic demand for that customer.

## Practice Angles

- Calculate: given price and quantity data, compute PED using the midpoint method
- Compare and contrast: elastic vs inelastic demand and their implications for total revenue
- Application: should a concert venue raise or lower ticket prices? Analyze using the total revenue test
- Cause and effect: how does a per-unit tax affect equilibrium price and quantity when demand is inelastic?
- Classify: given income elasticity values, classify goods as normal, inferior, luxury, or necessity
- Interpret: given a cross-price elasticity of +1.8, what is the relationship between the two goods?
- Graph interpretation: identify elastic vs inelastic regions on a linear demand curve
