# Government Policies -- Price Controls, Taxes, and Subsidies

## Overview
Governments intervene in markets to address perceived problems: affordability, fairness, revenue generation, or correcting market failures. The main tools are price controls (ceilings and floors), taxes, and subsidies. Each tool has predictable effects on prices, quantities, and surplus that can be analyzed using supply and demand. While well-intentioned, many interventions create unintended consequences including shortages, surpluses, deadweight loss, and black markets.

## Key Terms
- **Price Ceiling**: A legal maximum price. Only binding (effective) if set below equilibrium. Creates shortages.
- **Price Floor**: A legal minimum price. Only binding if set above equilibrium. Creates surpluses.
- **Rent Control**: A price ceiling on apartment rents. Reduces quantity of housing supplied in the long run.
- **Minimum Wage**: A price floor on labor. Can cause unemployment if set above the market wage.
- **Tax Incidence**: How the burden of a tax is divided between buyers and sellers. Depends on relative elasticities, not on who legally pays the tax.
- **Excise Tax**: A per-unit tax on a specific good (e.g., $1 per gallon of gas).
- **Subsidy**: A per-unit payment from the government to buyers or sellers, effectively a negative tax. Increases quantity and lowers price to consumers.
- **Quota**: A limit on the quantity of a good that can be produced or imported.
- **Black Market**: An illegal market that emerges when price controls create persistent shortages.

## Core Concepts

### Price Ceiling Effects
When a ceiling is binding (below equilibrium): Qd > Qs, creating a shortage. Consumers who get the good at the lower price benefit, but many can't get it at all. Quality may decline. Black markets develop. Long-run effects are worse as supply contracts further.

### Price Floor Effects
When a floor is binding (above equilibrium): Qs > Qd, creating a surplus. Producers who sell at the higher price benefit, but many can't sell at all. Government may need to purchase surplus (agricultural price supports). Minimum wage debate: helps workers who keep jobs, hurts those who lose them.

### Tax Incidence
The economic burden of a tax depends on elasticities, not on whether the tax is legally imposed on buyers or sellers. The more inelastic side bears more of the tax. If demand is perfectly inelastic (e.g., insulin), consumers bear the entire tax. If supply is perfectly inelastic (e.g., land), producers bear the entire tax.

### Subsidies
A subsidy is the mirror image of a tax. It increases quantity, lowers price to buyers, raises price to sellers, and costs the government money. Creates a DWL if the market was already at a competitive equilibrium (overproduction). Can be efficient if correcting a positive externality.

## Examples

### Rent Control in New York
NYC has had rent control/stabilization since WWII. Short-run: tenants in controlled apartments pay below-market rent. Long-run: landlords reduce maintenance, convert to condos, fewer new apartments built. Housing shortage worsens. Wait lists grow. Those with connections get apartments.

### Minimum Wage Debate
Federal minimum wage is $7.25/hr. Economists debate effects: if most workers already earn above it (non-binding), the effect is small. In areas where it binds, it raises wages for employed workers but may reduce employment, especially for teenagers and low-skill workers. Monopsony models suggest minimum wage can sometimes increase employment.

## Relationships
- Price ceiling below equilibrium -> Shortage -> Potential black market
- Price floor above equilibrium -> Surplus -> Government may buy excess
- Tax on inelastic good -> Revenue high, DWL small, consumers bear most burden
- Tax on elastic good -> Revenue lower, DWL large, producers bear more burden
- Subsidy -> Increased quantity, lower buyer price, higher seller price, government cost

## Common Misconceptions
- **"If the government taxes sellers, only sellers pay"**: Wrong. The economic incidence depends on elasticities, not the legal incidence. The tax burden is shared.
- **"Price ceilings always help consumers"**: They help some consumers (those who get the good) but hurt others (those who face shortages, lower quality, or black market prices).
- **"Minimum wage always causes unemployment"**: In competitive labor markets, yes. But in monopsony markets (few employers), a moderate minimum wage can increase both wages and employment.
