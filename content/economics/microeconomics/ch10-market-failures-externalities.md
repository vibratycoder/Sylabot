# Market Failures, Externalities, and Public Goods

## Overview

Markets are efficient under the conditions of perfect competition, but real-world markets often fail to allocate resources efficiently. Market failures occur when the free market produces either too much or too little of a good relative to the socially optimal quantity. The main sources of market failure are externalities, public goods, common resources, and imperfect information.

Externalities occur when a transaction imposes costs or benefits on third parties not involved in the transaction. Negative externalities (pollution) cause markets to overproduce; positive externalities (education, vaccines) cause markets to underproduce. Public goods (national defense, lighthouses) are non-excludable and non-rival, making private provision inadequate due to the free-rider problem.

Government can potentially correct market failures through taxes, subsidies, regulation, property rights, or direct provision. However, government intervention itself can be imperfect ("government failure"), so the analysis requires comparing the imperfect market outcome to the imperfect government intervention.

This topic is central to ECO 113 and especially ECO 461 (Environmental Economics) and ECO 462 (Public Finance).

## Key Terms

- **Market Failure**: A situation where the free market does not allocate resources efficiently, producing either too much or too little of a good.
- **Externality**: A cost or benefit that affects a party not directly involved in a transaction. Not reflected in the market price.
- **Negative Externality**: An external cost imposed on third parties (e.g., pollution from a factory harms nearby residents). Causes overproduction relative to the social optimum.
- **Positive Externality**: An external benefit conferred on third parties (e.g., a neighbor's beautiful garden increases your property value). Causes underproduction relative to the social optimum.
- **Social Cost**: Private cost + external cost. The full cost to society of producing a good.
- **Social Benefit**: Private benefit + external benefit. The full benefit to society of consuming a good.
- **Pigouvian Tax**: A tax equal to the external cost per unit, designed to internalize a negative externality. Shifts the private cost curve up to the social cost curve, reducing output to the socially optimal level.
- **Pigouvian Subsidy**: A subsidy equal to the external benefit per unit, designed to internalize a positive externality. Shifts the private benefit curve up to the social benefit curve, increasing output to the socially optimal level.
- **Coase Theorem**: If property rights are well-defined and transaction costs are low, private parties can bargain to an efficient outcome regardless of the initial assignment of property rights. The role of government is limited to defining and enforcing property rights.
- **Public Good**: A good that is non-excludable (cannot prevent non-payers from consuming) and non-rival (one person's consumption does not reduce availability to others). Examples: national defense, public radio, street lighting.
- **Free-Rider Problem**: When individuals benefit from a public good without paying for it, leading to underprovision by the private market.
- **Common Resources (Common-Pool Resources)**: Goods that are rival (one person's use reduces availability) but non-excludable (hard to prevent access). Examples: ocean fish stocks, public grazing land, clean air.
- **Tragedy of the Commons**: The overuse and depletion of common resources because each individual has an incentive to consume as much as possible while the cost of depletion is shared by all.
- **Cap and Trade**: A market-based policy for negative externalities. The government sets a cap on total emissions and issues tradable permits. Firms that can reduce emissions cheaply sell permits to firms that cannot, achieving the pollution reduction at the lowest total cost.
- **Carbon Tax**: A Pigouvian tax on carbon emissions designed to internalize the external cost of climate change.

## Core Concepts

### Negative Externalities and Overproduction

When a factory pollutes a river, the social cost of production exceeds the private cost (the factory does not pay for the environmental damage). The supply curve based on private costs lies below the social cost curve. The market equilibrium produces MORE than the socially optimal quantity (where social marginal cost = social marginal benefit). The gap between market output and socially optimal output creates a deadweight loss. Solutions: Pigouvian tax, regulation (emission standards), cap and trade, or Coasean bargaining.

### Positive Externalities and Underproduction

Education generates positive externalities: educated individuals earn more (private benefit) AND contribute to a more productive, innovative, and civically engaged society (external benefit). The social benefit exceeds the private benefit, so the socially optimal quantity of education exceeds the market quantity. Left to the free market, people would consume too little education. Solutions: subsidies (scholarships, public schools), government provision, or mandates (compulsory education laws).

### Public Goods and the Free-Rider Problem

National defense protects everyone in the country regardless of whether they pay taxes. If provision were voluntary, most people would free-ride -- enjoy the protection without paying. The result: the private market would provide far too little national defense. This is why governments tax citizens to fund public goods. The same logic applies to public parks, street lighting, and basic scientific research.

### The Coase Theorem and Its Limitations

Coase showed that if transaction costs are zero and property rights are clear, the parties affected by an externality will bargain to the efficient outcome without government intervention. If a factory's pollution damages a fishery worth $100K, and the factory values the pollution at $60K, the fishery could pay the factory $70K to stop polluting -- both sides benefit. However, the Coase theorem often fails in practice because transaction costs are high, property rights are unclear, and many parties are involved (millions of people affected by air pollution cannot practically negotiate with every polluter).

## Examples

### Example: Carbon Emissions and Climate Change

Burning fossil fuels generates CO2 emissions -- a negative externality because the cost of climate change (rising seas, extreme weather, agricultural disruption) is borne by the entire planet, not just the fuel burner. The social cost of carbon is estimated at $50-200+ per ton of CO2. A carbon tax set at this level would internalize the externality, raising the price of fossil fuels and incentivizing cleaner alternatives. The EU Emissions Trading System (cap and trade) is the largest real-world implementation.

### Example: Vaccination as Positive Externality

Getting vaccinated provides private benefits (you do not get sick) and external benefits (you cannot spread the disease to others -- herd immunity). Because individuals do not account for the benefit to others, the market would underprovide vaccinations. This justifies government subsidies (free or low-cost vaccines), public health campaigns, and sometimes mandates (school vaccination requirements).

### Example: Overfishing and the Tragedy of the Commons

The Atlantic cod fishery collapsed in the 1990s because each fishing boat had an incentive to catch as many fish as possible, while the cost of depleted stocks was shared by all fishers. No individual fisher could solve the problem alone. Solutions include government-imposed catch limits, individual transferable quotas (ITQs), and exclusive fishing zones. Iceland's ITQ system successfully rebuilt its fish stocks by giving fishers a property right in a share of the total catch.

## Relationships

- Negative Externality -> Social Cost > Private Cost -> Overproduction -> DWL
- Positive Externality -> Social Benefit > Private Benefit -> Underproduction -> DWL
- Pigouvian Tax -> Internalizes negative externality -> Moves output to social optimum
- Pigouvian Subsidy -> Internalizes positive externality -> Moves output to social optimum
- Public Goods -> Free-rider problem -> Market underprovision -> Government provision needed
- Common Resources -> Tragedy of the commons -> Overuse -> Property rights or regulation needed
- Coase Theorem -> Private bargaining can solve externalities IF transaction costs are low
- Cap and Trade -> Uses market mechanism to achieve pollution target at lowest cost
- Market Failure -> Justification for government intervention (but government failure also possible)

## Common Misconceptions

- **"All externalities are negative"**: Wrong. Positive externalities (education, R&D, vaccines, pollination by beekeepers) are equally important and lead to underproduction.
- **"Government should eliminate all pollution"**: Wrong. The optimal level of pollution is NOT zero. Some pollution is efficient if the social benefit of the activity (driving, manufacturing) exceeds the social cost. The goal is to reduce pollution to the socially optimal level, not eliminate it.
- **"The Coase theorem means government is unnecessary"**: In theory, private bargaining can work. In practice, high transaction costs, unclear property rights, and many affected parties make Coasean solutions impractical for most major externalities.
- **"Public goods cannot be provided by the private sector"**: Some public goods are partially provided privately (Wikipedia, open-source software, private lighthouses historically). But they are typically underprovided without government support.
- **"Cap and trade is the same as a carbon tax"**: Both internalize externalities, but they differ. A carbon tax fixes the price (tax per ton) and lets the market determine quantity. Cap and trade fixes the quantity (total emissions cap) and lets the market determine the price. Under certainty they are equivalent, but they respond differently to shocks.
