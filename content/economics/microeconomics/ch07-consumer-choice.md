# Consumer Choice Theory

## Overview
Consumer choice theory explains how rational consumers allocate their limited income across goods to maximize satisfaction (utility). The key insight is that consumers should allocate spending so that the last dollar spent on each good yields equal marginal utility. This framework explains why demand curves slope downward (diminishing marginal utility) and how consumers respond to price and income changes.

## Key Terms
- **Utility**: A measure of satisfaction or happiness from consuming a good. Measured in hypothetical "utils."
- **Total Utility**: The total satisfaction from consuming a given quantity of a good.
- **Marginal Utility (MU)**: The additional satisfaction from consuming one more unit. MU = ΔTU / ΔQ.
- **Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility**: As consumption increases, marginal utility decreases. The first slice of pizza gives more satisfaction than the fifth.
- **Budget Constraint**: The set of all combinations of goods a consumer can afford given their income and prices. Income = P_a × Q_a + P_b × Q_b.
- **Consumer Equilibrium**: The point where the consumer maximizes utility. Occurs where MU_a/P_a = MU_b/P_b for all goods (equal marginal utility per dollar).
- **Indifference Curve**: A curve showing all combinations of two goods that give equal utility. Higher curves = more utility.
- **Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS)**: The rate at which a consumer is willing to trade one good for another while maintaining the same utility. MRS = MU_x / MU_y. Equals the slope of the indifference curve.
- **Income Effect**: The change in consumption due to a change in purchasing power when price changes.
- **Substitution Effect**: The change in consumption due to a good becoming relatively cheaper or more expensive.

## Core Concepts

### Utility Maximization Rule
A consumer maximizes utility when: MU_a/P_a = MU_b/P_b = ... = MU_n/P_n. If the marginal utility per dollar of Good A exceeds that of Good B, the consumer should buy more A and less B until they equalize. This rule explains how consumers rationally allocate their budgets.

### Why Demand Curves Slope Downward
Diminishing marginal utility explains the law of demand. As price falls, the MU/P ratio for that good increases relative to other goods. Consumers buy more to re-equalize MU per dollar across all goods. Additionally, a lower price increases purchasing power (income effect) and makes the good cheaper relative to substitutes (substitution effect).

### Indifference Curve Analysis
At the optimal bundle, the budget constraint is tangent to the highest attainable indifference curve. At this point, the slope of the indifference curve (MRS) equals the slope of the budget constraint (price ratio): MRS = P_x / P_y. This is equivalent to the MU per dollar rule.

## Examples

### Diamond-Water Paradox
Water is essential but cheap; diamonds are non-essential but expensive. Why? Total utility of water is huge, but marginal utility is low (water is abundant). Diamonds are scarce, so marginal utility is high. Price reflects marginal utility, not total utility.

### All-You-Can-Eat Buffet
At a buffet, the price of additional food is zero. You eat until MU = 0 (not until MU/P equals other goods, since P = 0). This explains why people often overeat at buffets -- they keep eating until the next bite gives zero additional satisfaction.

## Relationships
- Diminishing marginal utility -> Downward-sloping demand curve
- Price decrease -> Substitution effect (buy more) + Income effect (can afford more)
- Budget constraint tangent to indifference curve -> Optimal consumption bundle
- MU_a/P_a = MU_b/P_b -> Consumer equilibrium (utility maximized)

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Utility can be measured exactly"**: Utility is ordinal (we can rank preferences) not cardinal (we can't assign exact numbers). The MU/P rule works with relative comparisons.
- **"Consumers always make perfectly rational choices"**: Behavioral economics shows systematic biases (anchoring, framing, loss aversion). Standard theory is a useful approximation.
- **"More expensive goods always give more utility"**: Price reflects marginal utility at the market level, not individual satisfaction. Water gives enormous total utility despite a low price.
