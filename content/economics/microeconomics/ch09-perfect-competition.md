# Perfect Competition

## Overview
Perfect competition is a market structure where many small firms sell identical products, no firm can influence the market price, and firms can freely enter and exit. Each firm is a price taker -- it accepts the market price as given. Firms maximize profit by producing where marginal revenue equals marginal cost (MR = MC). In the short run, firms may earn profits or losses. In the long run, entry and exit drive economic profit to zero, and firms produce at the minimum of average total cost -- the most efficient outcome.

## Key Terms
- **Perfect Competition**: A market with many buyers and sellers, identical products, perfect information, and free entry/exit.
- **Price Taker**: A firm that cannot influence the market price. It can sell any quantity at the market price but nothing above it.
- **Marginal Revenue (MR)**: The additional revenue from selling one more unit. In perfect competition, MR = Price (since the firm can sell unlimited units at the market price).
- **Profit Maximization Rule**: Produce where MR = MC. If MR > MC, produce more. If MR < MC, produce less.
- **Economic Profit**: Total revenue minus total cost (including opportunity costs). Different from accounting profit.
- **Break-Even Point**: Where P = ATC. Firm earns zero economic profit (but still earns normal accounting profit).
- **Shutdown Point**: Where P = AVC. Below this, firm loses more by operating than by shutting down temporarily.
- **Short-Run Supply Curve**: The portion of the MC curve above the AVC (shutdown point).
- **Long-Run Equilibrium**: P = MC = minimum ATC. Zero economic profit. No incentive to enter or exit.

## Core Concepts

### Short-Run Profit/Loss Analysis
- If P > ATC: firm earns economic profit (profit = (P - ATC) x Q)
- If AVC < P < ATC: firm operates at a loss but covers variable costs. Better than shutting down (would still pay FC).
- If P < AVC: firm shuts down. Revenue doesn't even cover variable costs.

### Long-Run Adjustment
If existing firms earn economic profit, new firms enter the market. Market supply shifts right, price falls. Entry continues until P = minimum ATC and economic profit = 0. If firms suffer losses, some exit. Supply shifts left, price rises until losses are eliminated. The long-run equilibrium is efficient: P = MC = min ATC.

### Efficiency of Perfect Competition
Allocative efficiency: P = MC (consumers' marginal value equals marginal cost). Productive efficiency: firms produce at min ATC (lowest possible cost). This is why perfect competition is the benchmark for evaluating other market structures.

## Examples

### Agricultural Markets
Wheat farming approximates perfect competition: many farmers, identical product, no single farm affects the price. Farmers are price takers. When wheat prices rise (demand increases), farms earn short-run profits. New farms enter; supply increases; price returns to minimum ATC.

### Shutdown Decision
A restaurant has fixed costs of $5,000/month (lease) and variable costs of $3,000/month. If revenue is $4,000/month, it should stay open in the short run: revenue ($4,000) > variable costs ($3,000). The $1,000 excess covers part of fixed costs. Shutting down would mean losing the full $5,000.

## Relationships
- MR = MC -> Profit-maximizing output
- P > ATC -> Economic profit -> Entry in long run
- P < ATC -> Economic loss -> Exit in long run
- Entry -> Supply increases -> Price falls -> Profit eliminated
- Long-run equilibrium -> P = MC = min ATC -> Zero economic profit

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Zero economic profit means the firm is failing"**: Zero economic profit means the firm covers all costs including the opportunity cost of the owner's time and capital. The firm earns a normal return -- it's doing as well as its next-best alternative.
- **"Firms should shut down whenever they lose money"**: In the short run, a firm should operate as long as P >= AVC, because operating covers variable costs and contributes to fixed costs. Shutting down means losing all fixed costs.
- **"Perfect competition exists in the real world"**: Few markets are perfectly competitive, but agricultural commodities, foreign exchange, and some online markets come close. It's a useful theoretical benchmark.
