# Government Intervention and Regulation

## Overview

Government intervenes in markets through price controls (ceilings and floors), taxes, subsidies, quotas, tariffs, and regulation. While intervention can correct market failures, it can also create new inefficiencies. Welfare analysis -- measuring changes in consumer surplus, producer surplus, and deadweight loss -- is the primary tool for evaluating whether government action improves or worsens outcomes.

Price ceilings (rent control) set a maximum price below equilibrium, creating shortages. Price floors (minimum wage, agricultural price supports) set a minimum price above equilibrium, creating surpluses. Taxes create a wedge between the price buyers pay and the price sellers receive, reducing quantity traded and creating deadweight loss. The division of the tax burden depends on relative elasticities, not on who legally pays the tax.

This topic connects to ECO 113, ECO 364 (Government and Business), and ECO 462 (Public Finance).

## Key Terms

- **Price Ceiling**: A legal maximum price. Effective only if set below the equilibrium price. Creates a shortage (Qd > Qs).
- **Price Floor**: A legal minimum price. Effective only if set above the equilibrium price. Creates a surplus (Qs > Qd).
- **Rent Control**: A price ceiling on rental housing. In the short run, helps current tenants. In the long run, reduces housing supply, deteriorates quality, and creates long waiting lists.
- **Consumer Surplus (CS)**: The area below the demand curve and above the price. Measures the net benefit to consumers.
- **Producer Surplus (PS)**: The area above the supply curve and below the price. Measures the net benefit to producers.
- **Total Surplus (TS)**: CS + PS. Maximized at the competitive equilibrium. Any deviation from equilibrium reduces total surplus.
- **Deadweight Loss (DWL)**: The reduction in total surplus from a market distortion (taxes, price controls, monopoly). Represents lost transactions that would have been mutually beneficial.
- **Tax Incidence**: Who bears the economic burden of a tax. Determined by relative elasticities: the more inelastic side bears more of the burden.
- **Tax Wedge**: The difference between the price buyers pay and the price sellers receive after a tax is imposed.
- **Subsidy**: A payment from the government to producers or consumers. Opposite of a tax. Lowers the price for buyers, raises the price for sellers, increases quantity -- but creates its own deadweight loss if the good does not have positive externalities.
- **Tariff**: A tax on imports. Raises the domestic price, increases domestic production, decreases imports, generates government revenue, but creates deadweight loss.
- **Import Quota**: A limit on the quantity of imports. Similar effects to a tariff but does not generate government revenue (the revenue goes to foreign producers as quota rents).
- **Government Failure**: When government intervention produces a worse outcome than the market failure it was designed to correct.
- **Regulatory Capture**: When a regulatory agency serves the interests of the industry it is supposed to regulate, rather than the public interest.

## Core Concepts

### Welfare Analysis of Price Ceilings

A price ceiling below equilibrium: Price is held below where Qs = Qd. At the controlled price, Qd > Qs, creating a shortage. Consumer surplus changes ambiguously: some consumers gain (those who get the good at the lower price) but others lose (those who cannot find the good). Producer surplus falls. Total surplus falls by the deadweight loss triangle. Real-world example: rent control creates housing shortages, long wait lists, black markets, and reduced maintenance incentives.

### Welfare Analysis of Taxes

A per-unit tax: The supply curve shifts up by the tax amount (or equivalently, creates a wedge). The new equilibrium has a higher price for buyers and a lower price for sellers. Quantity falls. Consumer surplus shrinks, producer surplus shrinks, government collects tax revenue (= tax x new quantity), and a deadweight loss triangle appears. The DWL represents transactions that no longer occur because the tax made them unprofitable. Larger taxes create disproportionately larger DWL (DWL increases with the square of the tax rate).

### Tax Incidence and Elasticity

The economic burden of a tax does not depend on who legally pays it. If a tax is imposed on sellers, they pass some of it to buyers through higher prices. The more inelastic side of the market bears the larger burden. If demand is perfectly inelastic, buyers bear the entire tax. If supply is perfectly inelastic, sellers bear the entire tax. This principle applies equally to payroll taxes: economists argue that both the "employer share" and "employee share" of Social Security taxes are ultimately borne by workers through lower pre-tax wages.

### Trade Policy: Tariffs and Quotas

A tariff on imported steel raises the domestic price. Domestic producers benefit (higher PS), domestic consumers lose (lower CS), and the government earns tariff revenue. The net effect is a deadweight loss from two sources: production inefficiency (domestic firms produce steel at a higher cost than foreign firms) and consumption inefficiency (consumers buy less steel than they would at the world price). Quotas have similar effects but the "revenue" goes to foreign producers.

## Examples

### Example: New York City Rent Control

NYC has maintained some form of rent control since the 1940s. Economists almost unanimously agree it creates shortages: landlords have less incentive to build new apartments or maintain existing ones, while below-market rents encourage tenants to stay even if they no longer need the space. The result: long waiting lists, deteriorating housing quality, apartments passed between family members, and a parallel uncontrolled market with much higher rents.

### Example: Sugar Tariffs in the US

The US imposes tariffs and quotas on sugar imports, keeping the domestic sugar price roughly double the world price. This benefits US sugar producers (about 4,500 farms) at the expense of millions of consumers who pay higher prices for sugar and sugar-containing products. The concentrated benefits to producers (who lobby actively) and diffuse costs to consumers (a few dollars per person per year) explain why the policy persists politically despite reducing total welfare.

### Example: Cigarette Tax Incidence

A $2-per-pack cigarette tax is imposed on sellers. Because demand for cigarettes is highly inelastic (addiction), consumers bear most of the burden -- the retail price rises by about $1.80, and sellers absorb only about $0.20. This demonstrates that the economic incidence of a tax depends on elasticities, not legal incidence.

## Relationships

- Price Ceiling below equilibrium -> Shortage -> Deadweight Loss
- Price Floor above equilibrium -> Surplus -> Deadweight Loss
- Tax -> Wedge between buyer and seller prices -> Reduced quantity -> DWL
- More inelastic side -> Bears more of the tax burden
- DWL increases with the square of the tax rate: Larger distortions are disproportionately costly
- Tariff = Tax on imports -> Higher domestic price, less trade, DWL
- Subsidy = Negative tax -> Lower buyer price, higher seller price, increased quantity
- Market Failure may justify intervention, but Government Failure is also possible

## Common Misconceptions

- **"Rent control makes housing more affordable for everyone"**: Wrong. It benefits current tenants at the controlled price but reduces the total housing supply, hurts future tenants who cannot find apartments, and deteriorates housing quality.
- **"A tax on sellers is paid by sellers, a tax on buyers is paid by buyers"**: Wrong. The economic incidence is the same regardless of legal incidence. The market adjusts prices so that the burden is shared based on relative elasticities.
- **"Tariffs protect American jobs at no cost"**: Wrong. Tariffs protect some jobs (in the protected industry) but at the cost of higher prices for all consumers and potential retaliation by trading partners. Studies consistently find the cost per job saved by tariffs far exceeds the wages of those jobs.
- **"Deadweight loss just transfers money from one group to another"**: Wrong. DWL is a net loss to society -- it represents transactions that do not happen. It is not transferred; it is destroyed.
- **"Price floors and ceilings are always binding"**: Only if set above (floor) or below (ceiling) the equilibrium price. A floor set below equilibrium or a ceiling set above equilibrium has no effect.
