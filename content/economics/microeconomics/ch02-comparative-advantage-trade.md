# Comparative Advantage and Trade

## Overview
Trade makes both parties better off even when one party is better at producing everything. This counterintuitive insight is one of the most powerful ideas in economics. The key is comparative advantage: the ability to produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than someone else. When individuals, firms, or countries specialize in what they do relatively best and then trade, total output increases and both sides gain. This principle explains why nations trade, why workers specialize, and why self-sufficiency is costly.

## Key Terms
- **Absolute Advantage**: The ability to produce a good using fewer resources (or more output per unit of input) than another producer. A worker who can make 10 shirts/hour has an absolute advantage over one who makes 6 shirts/hour.
- **Comparative Advantage**: The ability to produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than another producer. Even if you're worse at making everything, you have a comparative advantage in the good where your disadvantage is smallest.
- **Specialization**: Concentrating production on the good in which you have a comparative advantage, then trading for other goods.
- **Gains from Trade**: The increase in total output (and consumption) that results from specialization and exchange. Both trading partners benefit.
- **Terms of Trade**: The rate at which one good is exchanged for another. For trade to benefit both sides, the terms of trade must fall between the two producers' opportunity costs.
- **Autarky**: Self-sufficiency -- producing everything you consume without trade. Autarky is inefficient compared to specialization and trade.
- **Import**: A good or service purchased from another country.
- **Export**: A good or service sold to another country.

## Core Concepts

### Absolute vs Comparative Advantage
Absolute advantage is about productivity (who makes more per hour). Comparative advantage is about opportunity cost (who gives up less). A doctor might be a faster typist than her secretary (absolute advantage in both doctoring and typing), but her opportunity cost of typing is much higher (she gives up $300/hour of medical work). The secretary has the comparative advantage in typing because her opportunity cost is lower.

### Why Trade Benefits Both Parties
Without trade, each producer is limited to consuming what they produce. With trade based on comparative advantage, total production increases. Each party specializes in their comparative advantage good, produces more of it, and trades for the other good. The result: both consume more than they could alone. This is true even if one party has an absolute advantage in everything.

### Calculating Comparative Advantage
Given a table of production capabilities, calculate opportunity costs for each good for each producer. The producer with the lower opportunity cost for a good has the comparative advantage in that good. Note: if you have the comparative advantage in one good, the other producer necessarily has the comparative advantage in the other good.

## Examples

### Two Countries: US and Mexico
Suppose the US can produce 100 cars or 200 tons of wheat, and Mexico can produce 20 cars or 60 tons of wheat. The US has absolute advantage in both. But the US opportunity cost of 1 car = 2 tons wheat, while Mexico's opportunity cost of 1 car = 3 tons wheat. The US has comparative advantage in cars (lower opportunity cost). Mexico has comparative advantage in wheat (1 ton wheat costs 1/3 car vs 1/2 car in US). Both gain if the US specializes more in cars and Mexico in wheat.

### Athletes and Lawn Mowing
LeBron James could probably mow his lawn faster than any landscaper (absolute advantage). But his opportunity cost of mowing -- the income from basketball practice or endorsements he forgoes -- is enormous. The landscaper has the comparative advantage in lawn mowing because their opportunity cost is much lower.

## Relationships
- Opportunity Cost -> Comparative Advantage: Comparative advantage is determined by comparing opportunity costs
- Comparative Advantage -> Specialization: Producers should specialize in their comparative advantage good
- Specialization -> Gains from Trade: Specialization enables gains from trade
- PPF -> Trade: Trade allows consumption beyond the PPF

## Common Misconceptions
- **"The country that's better at everything doesn't need to trade"**: Wrong. Even with absolute advantage in all goods, both countries gain from trade based on comparative advantage.
- **"Trade is zero-sum -- one side wins, the other loses"**: Wrong. Trade based on comparative advantage is positive-sum; both sides consume more than under autarky.
- **"Comparative advantage means you're good at something"**: Not necessarily. It means your opportunity cost is lower. You might be bad at both goods but still have a comparative advantage in one.
- **"A country can have comparative advantage in everything"**: Impossible. If you have comparative advantage in one good, the other party must have it in the other good.
