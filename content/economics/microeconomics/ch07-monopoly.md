# Monopoly

## Overview

A monopoly is a market structure with a single seller producing a product with no close substitutes, protected by high barriers to entry. Unlike a competitive firm (price taker), a monopolist is a price maker -- it chooses the price-quantity combination on the market demand curve that maximizes profit.

The monopolist still follows the MR = MC rule, but because it faces a downward-sloping demand curve, marginal revenue is less than price (to sell one more unit, the monopolist must lower the price on ALL units). This leads to a quantity lower than and a price higher than the competitive outcome, creating a deadweight loss -- a net reduction in total social welfare.

Monopoly is analyzed in ECO 113, ECO 313, ECO 363 (Industrial Organization), and ECO 364 (Government and Business).

## Key Terms

- **Monopoly**: A market with a single seller, no close substitutes, and high barriers to entry.
- **Barriers to Entry**: Obstacles that prevent new firms from entering the market. Types: legal barriers (patents, licenses, government franchises), economies of scale (natural monopoly), control of key resources, brand loyalty/network effects.
- **Price Maker**: A firm with the power to set its own price (within the constraint of the demand curve).
- **Marginal Revenue < Price**: For a monopolist, MR < P because selling one more unit requires lowering the price on all previous units. The MR curve lies below the demand curve.
- **Deadweight Loss (DWL)**: The reduction in total surplus (consumer + producer) due to the monopolist producing less than the socially optimal quantity. Represents transactions that would benefit both buyers and sellers but do not occur.
- **Natural Monopoly**: A monopoly that arises because a single firm can supply the entire market at a lower ATC than two or more firms could. Occurs when economies of scale are large relative to market demand (e.g., utilities, water systems).
- **Price Discrimination**: Charging different prices to different consumers (or groups) for the same product based on willingness to pay. First-degree: each consumer pays their maximum willingness to pay. Second-degree: pricing varies by quantity (bulk discounts). Third-degree: different prices for different groups (student discounts, senior discounts).
- **Rent Seeking**: The use of resources to obtain or maintain monopoly power (lobbying, legal battles) rather than to produce goods. This is socially wasteful.
- **Monopoly Profit**: (P - ATC) x Q at the profit-maximizing output. Can persist in the long run because barriers to entry prevent competition.
- **Antitrust Law**: Government regulation designed to prevent monopoly abuse and promote competition. Key laws: Sherman Act (1890), Clayton Act (1914), Federal Trade Commission Act (1914).

## Core Concepts

### Monopoly Profit Maximization

The monopolist produces where MR = MC, then charges the price consumers are willing to pay for that quantity (read from the demand curve). Because MR < P (the MR curve is steeper and below the demand curve), the monopoly price is higher and quantity is lower than in perfect competition. The monopolist earns economic profit = (P - ATC) x Q, shown as a rectangle on the graph. Unlike perfect competition, this profit can persist in the long run because barriers to entry prevent new firms from entering.

### Deadweight Loss and Inefficiency

The monopolist's output restriction creates a deadweight loss triangle between the demand curve and the MC curve, from the monopoly quantity to the competitive quantity. At the monopoly output, consumers value additional units more than they cost to produce (P > MC), but the monopolist does not produce them because doing so would require lowering the price on all units. This is allocative inefficiency: P > MC.

### Price Discrimination

If a monopolist can identify consumers' willingness to pay and prevent resale, it can charge different prices to different consumers. Perfect (first-degree) price discrimination captures ALL consumer surplus, converting it to producer surplus, and -- paradoxically -- eliminates deadweight loss because the monopolist now produces the competitive quantity. Real-world examples: airline pricing, college tuition (financial aid), coupons (self-selecting price discrimination), surge pricing.

### Natural Monopoly and Regulation

When average total cost is still declining over the relevant range of market demand, a single firm can produce more cheaply than multiple firms. Breaking up a natural monopoly would raise costs. Instead, governments typically regulate natural monopolies by setting price = ATC (average-cost pricing, which allows normal profit and is sustainable) or price = MC (marginal-cost pricing, which is allocatively efficient but requires a government subsidy because P < ATC at that output).

## Examples

### Example: De Beers and Diamond Monopoly

De Beers historically controlled roughly 80-85% of the global diamond market. By restricting the supply of diamonds and controlling distribution channels, De Beers maintained artificially high prices. Their "A Diamond is Forever" campaign also shaped demand. This illustrates how control of a key resource plus marketing power creates monopoly pricing. Antitrust pressure and new mines in Russia, Canada, and Australia have since reduced their market share.

### Example: Pharmaceutical Patents

Drug patents grant a 20-year monopoly to incentivize R&D investment. During the patent period, the company sets the monopoly price (often very high for life-saving drugs with inelastic demand). When the patent expires, generic competitors enter, prices plummet, and the market moves toward competitive pricing. This illustrates the trade-off between innovation incentives (monopoly profits as reward) and consumer welfare (higher prices during patent period).

### Example: Airline Price Discrimination

Airlines charge vastly different prices for the same flight: business travelers who book last-minute pay $800; leisure travelers who book 3 weeks ahead pay $200. This is third-degree price discrimination based on price elasticity. Business travelers have inelastic demand (they must travel), so airlines charge them more. Leisure travelers have elastic demand (they can choose not to fly), so airlines charge them less to fill seats.

## Relationships

- Barriers to Entry -> Monopoly Power -> Long-run economic profits persist
- MR < P -> Monopolist produces less than competitive output -> Higher price
- Monopoly output < Competitive output -> Deadweight loss -> Allocative inefficiency
- Natural Monopoly -> Regulation needed: Average-cost pricing or marginal-cost pricing
- Price Discrimination -> Different prices for different elasticities -> Can reduce or eliminate DWL
- Antitrust Laws -> Limit monopoly power -> Move market toward competitive outcomes
- Monopoly -> ECO 363/364 Industrial Organization: Extended analysis of market power and regulation

## Common Misconceptions

- **"Monopolists can charge any price they want"**: Wrong. They are constrained by the demand curve. If they set the price too high, quantity demanded falls dramatically. They maximize profit, not price.
- **"Monopolists always earn profits"**: Not guaranteed. If ATC > P at the profit-maximizing quantity, the monopolist loses money. A monopoly in a market with very low demand can still fail.
- **"Price discrimination is always harmful"**: Not necessarily. It can increase total output (closer to the competitive level), reduce deadweight loss, and allow some consumers to purchase at lower prices who otherwise couldn't afford the product.
- **"Natural monopolies should be broken up"**: Wrong. Breaking up a natural monopoly would INCREASE costs because no single smaller firm could achieve the same economies of scale. Regulation is the better approach.
- **"Monopoly is illegal"**: Having a monopoly is not illegal. Abusing monopoly power (predatory pricing, exclusive dealing, tying) is what antitrust laws target.
