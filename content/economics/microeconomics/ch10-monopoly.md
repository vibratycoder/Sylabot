# Monopoly

## Overview
A monopoly is a market with a single seller and no close substitutes. Unlike a perfectly competitive firm, a monopolist is a price maker -- it chooses the price-quantity combination on the demand curve that maximizes profit. Monopolists produce less and charge more than competitive markets, creating deadweight loss. Barriers to entry (patents, economies of scale, government licenses) prevent competitors from entering. Government can regulate monopolies through antitrust law, price regulation, or public ownership.

## Key Terms
- **Monopoly**: A market with one seller, no close substitutes, and high barriers to entry.
- **Price Maker**: A firm with the power to set its own price (by choosing its quantity).
- **Barriers to Entry**: Obstacles that prevent new firms from entering a market: patents, economies of scale, resource control, government licenses.
- **Natural Monopoly**: A monopoly that arises because one firm can supply the entire market at lower cost than two or more firms (due to economies of scale). Examples: utilities, water, sewage.
- **Marginal Revenue (Monopoly)**: MR < P for a monopolist because it must lower the price on ALL units to sell one more. MR curve lies below the demand curve.
- **Deadweight Loss**: The loss in total surplus because the monopolist produces less than the competitive quantity.
- **Price Discrimination**: Charging different prices to different consumers for the same good based on willingness to pay. First-degree (perfect), second-degree (quantity discounts), third-degree (group pricing).
- **Rent Seeking**: Spending resources to acquire or maintain monopoly power (lobbying, legal battles) rather than productive activity.
- **Antitrust Laws**: Laws preventing monopolistic practices. Sherman Act (1890), Clayton Act (1914), FTC Act.

## Core Concepts

### Monopoly Profit Maximization
The monopolist produces where MR = MC (same rule as all firms). But because MR < P for a monopolist, the profit-maximizing price is above MC. The monopolist reads up from the MR = MC quantity to the demand curve to find the price. Profit = (P - ATC) x Q.

### Monopoly vs Competition
Compared to a competitive market: monopolist charges higher price, produces lower quantity, earns economic profit in both short and long run (barriers prevent entry), and creates deadweight loss. Consumer surplus is reduced; some is transferred to the monopolist as profit, some is lost as DWL.

### Price Discrimination
If a monopolist can identify consumers' willingness to pay and prevent resale, it can charge each consumer a different price. Perfect price discrimination captures all consumer surplus but eliminates deadweight loss (all efficient trades occur). Third-degree discrimination (student discounts, senior pricing) is most common.

## Examples

### Pharmaceutical Patents
Drug patents grant a 20-year monopoly. A company developing a new drug charges high prices during the patent period (MR = MC, but P >> MC). When the patent expires, generic competitors enter, price drops to near MC, and consumer surplus increases dramatically.

### Utility Companies
Electric utilities are natural monopolies -- the fixed cost of the power grid is enormous, making it cheaper for one company to serve the market. Governments regulate utilities, often setting price = ATC (average cost pricing) to prevent monopoly pricing while allowing the firm to cover costs.

## Relationships
- Single seller + barriers -> Monopoly power
- MR < P -> Monopolist restricts output to raise price
- Monopoly output < Competitive output -> Deadweight loss
- Barriers to entry -> Long-run economic profit persists
- Price discrimination -> Captures consumer surplus, may reduce DWL

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Monopolists can charge any price they want"**: They're constrained by the demand curve. If they set price too high, quantity demanded drops significantly. They choose the profit-maximizing point on the demand curve.
- **"Monopolies are always bad"**: Natural monopolies may be more efficient than competition (duplicating infrastructure is wasteful). Patents incentivize innovation. Regulation can mitigate the worst effects.
- **"Price discrimination is always unfair"**: Third-degree discrimination (student/senior discounts) can increase total quantity sold and make goods accessible to price-sensitive consumers.
