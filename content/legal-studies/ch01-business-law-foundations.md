# Business Law Foundations

## Overview
Business law governs the rules and regulations that apply to commercial transactions and business organizations. It draws from multiple sources: the Constitution, statutes, administrative regulations, and common law (court decisions). Business professionals must understand contract law, tort law, property rights, and the legal framework within which businesses operate.

## Key Terms
- **Common Law**: Law developed through court decisions and precedent (case law). Judges follow prior decisions (stare decisis).
- **Statutory Law**: Law enacted by legislatures (Congress, state legislatures). Includes the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC).
- **Administrative Law**: Rules and regulations created by government agencies (SEC, EPA, FTC, IRS).
- **Constitutional Law**: The US Constitution is the supreme law. Establishes government structure, individual rights, and commerce regulation.
- **Jurisdiction**: A court's authority to hear a case. Based on subject matter and geography.
- **Contract**: A legally enforceable agreement between two or more parties. Elements: offer, acceptance, consideration, capacity, legality.
- **Tort**: A civil wrong (not a crime) that causes harm. Three types: intentional, negligence, strict liability.
- **Negligence**: Failure to exercise reasonable care, causing harm. Elements: duty, breach, causation, damages.
- **Strict Liability**: Liability without fault. Applied to abnormally dangerous activities and defective products.
- **Stare Decisis**: "Let the decision stand" -- courts follow precedent from prior cases.
- **Plaintiff**: The party bringing a lawsuit.
- **Defendant**: The party being sued.
- **Breach of Contract**: Failure to perform a contractual obligation. Remedies: damages, specific performance, rescission.

## Core Concepts

### Sources of Law Hierarchy
1. US Constitution (supreme), 2. Federal statutes, 3. State constitutions, 4. State statutes, 5. Administrative regulations, 6. Common law (court decisions). Higher sources override lower ones in conflict.

### Contract Formation
A valid contract requires: (1) Offer -- clear terms communicated to offeree, (2) Acceptance -- unequivocal agreement to the offer, (3) Consideration -- something of value exchanged, (4) Capacity -- parties must be competent (age, mental state), (5) Legality -- purpose must be lawful.

### Negligence Analysis
Four elements must be proven: (1) Duty -- defendant owed a duty of care, (2) Breach -- defendant failed to meet that duty, (3) Causation -- breach caused the injury (actual and proximate cause), (4) Damages -- plaintiff suffered measurable harm.

## Examples

### Breach of Contract
A supplier agrees to deliver 1,000 units by March 1. They deliver 800 units on March 15. This is a breach. The buyer can sue for compensatory damages (cost of obtaining the remaining 200 units from another supplier, plus any losses from the delay).

### Negligence Case
A store fails to clean a spilled drink (breach of duty to maintain safe premises). A customer slips and breaks her arm (causation and damages). The store is liable for medical bills, lost wages, and pain and suffering.

## Relationships
- Contract breach -> Legal remedies (damages, specific performance)
- Negligence: duty + breach + causation + damages = liability
- Constitutional law -> Limits government power, protects individual rights
- Statutory law -> Created by legislatures, more specific than constitutional law
- Common law -> Evolves through court decisions and precedent

## Common Misconceptions
- **"A verbal agreement isn't a real contract"**: Most oral contracts are legally enforceable. Some must be in writing (Statute of Frauds: real estate, >$500, >1 year).
- **"You can sue for anything"**: You need legal standing (actual harm), a recognized legal theory, and the ability to prove your case.
- **"Negligence and intentional harm are the same"**: Negligence is careless behavior; intentional torts involve deliberate harmful acts. Different legal standards and damages apply.

## Practice Angles
