# Financial Statements and Ratio Analysis

## Overview
Financial statements are the primary way companies communicate their financial health. The three main statements -- income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement -- provide different perspectives on a firm's performance. Financial ratios distill these statements into comparable metrics for liquidity, profitability, efficiency, and leverage. Every business and finance professional must be able to read and analyze financial statements.

## Key Terms
- **Income Statement**: Reports revenues, expenses, and net income over a period. Revenue - COGS = Gross Profit. Gross Profit - Operating Expenses = Operating Income (EBIT). EBIT - Interest - Taxes = Net Income.
- **Balance Sheet**: A snapshot of what the firm owns (assets), owes (liabilities), and equity at a point in time. Assets = Liabilities + Equity.
- **Cash Flow Statement**: Reports cash inflows and outflows from operations, investing, and financing activities. Reconciles net income to actual cash changes.
- **Current Ratio**: Current Assets / Current Liabilities. Measures short-term liquidity. Above 1.0 means the firm can cover short-term obligations.
- **Quick Ratio**: (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities. Stricter liquidity test excluding hard-to-sell inventory.
- **Debt-to-Equity Ratio**: Total Debt / Total Equity. Measures financial leverage.
- **Return on Assets (ROA)**: Net Income / Total Assets. How efficiently the firm uses assets to generate profit.
- **Return on Equity (ROE)**: Net Income / Shareholders' Equity. Return earned for equity investors. The DuPont formula decomposes ROE = Profit Margin x Asset Turnover x Equity Multiplier.
- **Profit Margin**: Net Income / Revenue. What percentage of each dollar of revenue becomes profit.
- **Asset Turnover**: Revenue / Total Assets. How efficiently assets generate revenue.
- **Earnings Per Share (EPS)**: Net Income / Shares Outstanding. Key metric for stock valuation.
- **Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E)**: Stock Price / EPS. How much investors pay per dollar of earnings.

## Core Concepts

### Reading an Income Statement
Revenue (or Sales) is the top line. Subtract COGS for gross profit. Subtract operating expenses (SG&A, depreciation) for operating income (EBIT). Subtract interest and taxes for net income (bottom line). Trends matter more than single-year numbers.

### Reading a Balance Sheet
Assets: Current assets (cash, receivables, inventory) + Long-term assets (property, equipment, intangibles). Liabilities: Current liabilities (accounts payable, short-term debt) + Long-term liabilities (bonds, long-term loans). Equity: Common stock + Retained earnings.

### DuPont Analysis
ROE = (Net Income/Revenue) x (Revenue/Assets) x (Assets/Equity) = Profit Margin x Turnover x Leverage. This decomposition reveals whether high ROE comes from profitability, efficiency, or debt (which adds risk).

## Examples

### Comparing Two Companies
Company A: ROE = 20% (margin 5%, turnover 2, leverage 2). Company B: ROE = 20% (margin 10%, turnover 1, leverage 2). Same ROE but A relies on high efficiency while B relies on high margins. Different business models, different risks.

### Red Flags in Financial Statements
Revenue growing but cash flow from operations declining -> may be recognizing revenue aggressively. Inventory growing faster than revenue -> products may not be selling. Accounts receivable growing faster than revenue -> customers may not be paying.

## Relationships
- Revenue - Expenses = Net Income (Income Statement)
- Assets = Liabilities + Equity (Balance Sheet)
- ROE = Profit Margin x Asset Turnover x Equity Multiplier (DuPont)
- Higher leverage -> Higher ROE but higher risk
- Current ratio > 1 -> Can meet short-term obligations

## Common Misconceptions
- **"Net income equals cash"**: Net income includes non-cash items (depreciation, accruals). The cash flow statement shows actual cash.
- **"Higher ROE is always better"**: ROE can be inflated by excessive debt (leverage). DuPont analysis reveals the source.
- **"A company with high revenue is doing well"**: Revenue means nothing without profitability. Many companies have high revenue but lose money.

## Practice Angles
- Calculate: given financial statement data, compute current ratio, ROE, debt-to-equity
- Perform DuPont analysis: decompose ROE into its three components
- Compare: two companies using ratio analysis -- which is a better investment?
- Identify: financial statement red flags from given data
- Explain: why cash flow from operations can differ significantly from net income
