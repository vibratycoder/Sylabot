# Capital Budgeting

## Overview
Capital budgeting is the process of evaluating and selecting long-term investments that are consistent with the firm's goal of maximizing shareholder wealth. These investments (new equipment, facilities, products, acquisitions) require large upfront outlays and generate cash flows over many years. The central question is: will the project's future cash flows be worth more than its cost?

The primary capital budgeting technique is Net Present Value (NPV), which discounts all project cash flows at the firm's required rate of return. Projects with positive NPV create value; negative NPV projects destroy value. Other techniques include the Internal Rate of Return (IRR), payback period, and profitability index, each with their own strengths and limitations.

## Key Terms
- **Capital Budgeting**: The process of planning and managing long-term investments. Also called capital expenditure analysis or project evaluation.
- **Net Present Value (NPV)**: The difference between the present value of a project's cash inflows and the present value of its cash outflows. NPV = sum of [CF_t / (1+r)^t] - Initial Investment.
- **Internal Rate of Return (IRR)**: The discount rate that makes NPV = 0. The project's effective annual return. Accept if IRR > required return.
- **Payback Period**: The number of years required to recover the initial investment from project cash flows. Ignores time value of money and cash flows beyond payback.
- **Discounted Payback Period**: Like payback, but uses discounted cash flows. Accounts for time value but still ignores cash flows beyond the payback point.
- **Profitability Index (PI)**: PV of future cash flows / Initial Investment. Accept if PI > 1. Equivalent to NPV per dollar invested.
- **Hurdle Rate (Required Return)**: The minimum acceptable rate of return for a project. Usually the firm's weighted average cost of capital (WACC) adjusted for project risk.
- **Incremental Cash Flows**: The additional cash flows generated by the project beyond what the firm would earn without it. Only incremental cash flows are relevant.
- **Sunk Cost**: A cost that has already been incurred and cannot be recovered. Sunk costs are irrelevant to capital budgeting decisions.
- **Opportunity Cost**: The value of the next-best alternative use of an asset. Must be included as a cost in project analysis.
- **Cannibalization (Erosion)**: When a new project reduces sales of existing products. This lost revenue must be subtracted from the new project's cash flows.
- **Independent Projects**: Projects whose cash flows are unaffected by the acceptance or rejection of other projects. Accept all independent projects with positive NPV.
- **Mutually Exclusive Projects**: Projects where accepting one means rejecting the other(s). Choose the project with the highest NPV.
- **Conventional Cash Flow Pattern**: One initial outflow followed by a series of inflows. IRR works well for conventional patterns.
- **Terminal Value**: The value of a project's cash flows beyond the explicit forecast period.

## Core Concepts

### NPV Decision Rule
**NPV = -CF_0 + CF_1/(1+r)^1 + CF_2/(1+r)^2 + ... + CF_n/(1+r)^n**

Decision rule:
- NPV > 0: Accept (project adds value)
- NPV < 0: Reject (project destroys value)
- NPV = 0: Indifferent (project earns exactly the required return)

Example: Project costs $10,000 today. Expected cash flows: Year 1: $4,000, Year 2: $4,000, Year 3: $5,000. Required return = 12%.
NPV = -10,000 + 4,000/1.12 + 4,000/1.12^2 + 5,000/1.12^3
NPV = -10,000 + 3,571 + 3,189 + 3,559 = $319
NPV > 0, so accept the project.

### IRR Decision Rule
Find the rate r that makes NPV = 0:
**0 = -CF_0 + CF_1/(1+IRR)^1 + CF_2/(1+IRR)^2 + ... + CF_n/(1+IRR)^n**

Decision rule:
- IRR > required return: Accept
- IRR < required return: Reject

For the example above, IRR is approximately 13.7% (found by trial and error or financial calculator). Since 13.7% > 12%, accept.

### NPV vs. IRR Conflicts
For independent projects with conventional cash flows, NPV and IRR always agree. Conflicts arise with:

1. **Mutually exclusive projects**: Two projects may have different rankings under NPV and IRR due to differences in scale or timing. ALWAYS prefer the NPV ranking because it directly measures value created.

2. **Non-conventional cash flows**: Multiple sign changes in cash flows can produce multiple IRRs or no IRR. NPV is reliable; IRR is not.

3. **Scale differences**: A small project can have a higher IRR but lower NPV than a large project. NPV is the correct measure because it measures total value added.

### Payback Period
Count years until cumulative cash flows equal the initial investment.

Example: Cost = $10,000. Year 1: $4,000, Year 2: $4,000, Year 3: $5,000.
Cumulative: Year 1: $4,000, Year 2: $8,000, Year 3: $13,000.
Payback = 2 + ($10,000 - $8,000) / $5,000 = 2.4 years.

Limitations: Ignores time value of money, ignores cash flows after payback, no clear decision criterion (what payback is "good"?).

### Profitability Index
**PI = PV of future cash flows / Initial Investment**

Example: PV of cash flows = $10,319; Initial cost = $10,000.
PI = $10,319 / $10,000 = 1.032
PI > 1, so accept. Useful for ranking projects under capital rationing (limited budget).

### Estimating Project Cash Flows
Relevant cash flows follow three principles:
1. **Incremental**: Only include cash flows that change because of the project
2. **After-tax**: Use after-tax cash flows because taxes are a real cost
3. **Include opportunity costs and externalities**: Cannibalization, alternative uses of assets

Operating Cash Flow = (Revenue - Costs) x (1 - Tax Rate) + Depreciation x Tax Rate

The depreciation tax shield (Depreciation x Tax Rate) is a cash inflow because depreciation reduces taxable income.

Initial investment = Asset cost + Shipping + Installation + Increase in net working capital
Terminal cash flow = After-tax salvage value + Recovery of net working capital

## Examples

### Example 1: Mutually Exclusive Projects
Project A: Cost $50,000, NPV = $12,000, IRR = 25%
Project B: Cost $200,000, NPV = $30,000, IRR = 18%
IRR says choose A. NPV says choose B. NPV is correct because B adds $30,000 in value vs. $12,000.

### Example 2: Including Opportunity Cost
Your firm owns a building it could rent for $50,000/year. If you use it for a new project instead, the $50,000/year foregone rent is an opportunity cost that must be subtracted from project cash flows.

### Example 3: After-Tax Cash Flow with Depreciation
A machine costs $100,000, generates $40,000/year in pre-tax operating income, is depreciated straight-line over 5 years, and the tax rate is 30%.
Annual depreciation = $100,000 / 5 = $20,000
Tax = ($40,000 - $20,000) x 0.30 = $6,000
After-tax operating cash flow = $40,000 - $6,000 = $34,000
Or equivalently: ($40,000) x (1-0.30) + $20,000 x 0.30 = $28,000 + $6,000 = $34,000

## Common Misconceptions
- **If IRR > required return, always accept**: Not when projects are mutually exclusive or cash flows are non-conventional. NPV should be the primary decision criterion.
- **Sunk costs should be included**: Never. A cost that has already occurred cannot be changed by the accept/reject decision.
- **Depreciation is a cash flow**: Depreciation itself is NOT a cash flow -- it is a non-cash accounting charge. However, the tax savings from depreciation (the depreciation tax shield) IS a relevant cash flow.
- **Payback period is a reliable measure**: It ignores time value of money and all cash flows after the payback date. It can lead to rejecting excellent long-term projects.
- **Financing costs (interest) should be in project cash flows**: No. Financing effects are captured in the discount rate (WACC). Including interest in cash flows AND discounting at WACC would double-count the cost of debt.
