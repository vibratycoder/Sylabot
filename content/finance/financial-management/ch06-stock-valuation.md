# Stock Valuation

## Overview
Stock valuation determines the intrinsic value of a company's equity. The fundamental approach values a stock as the present value of all expected future cash flows to shareholders. The most common models are the Dividend Discount Model (DDM) and its special cases: the zero-growth model and the Gordon Growth Model (constant growth). For firms that do not pay dividends, free cash flow models are used instead.

Understanding stock valuation is critical for investment decisions (is the stock overvalued or undervalued?), corporate finance (how do management decisions affect firm value?), and the cost of equity capital.

## Key Terms
- **Common Stock**: Ownership shares in a corporation that provide voting rights and residual claims on assets and earnings. Dividends are not guaranteed.
- **Preferred Stock**: A hybrid security with a fixed dividend. Preferred shareholders are paid before common shareholders but typically have no voting rights. Valued as a perpetuity.
- **Dividend**: A distribution of a portion of a company's earnings to shareholders, usually in cash.
- **Intrinsic Value**: The "true" or fundamental value of a stock based on analysis of its cash flows, growth, and risk. Compare to market price to make buy/sell decisions.
- **Dividend Discount Model (DDM)**: Values a stock as the present value of all future expected dividends. P_0 = sum of D_t / (1+r)^t.
- **Gordon Growth Model (Constant Growth DDM)**: Assumes dividends grow at a constant rate g forever. P_0 = D_1 / (r - g), where D_1 = next year's dividend, r = required return, g = constant growth rate.
- **Zero-Growth Model**: A special case where dividends never change. P_0 = D / r. This is a perpetuity.
- **Two-Stage Growth Model**: Assumes a period of supernormal growth followed by constant growth. Value = PV of dividends during high-growth period + PV of terminal value.
- **Terminal Value**: The value at the end of the explicit forecast period, capturing all subsequent cash flows. Often calculated using the Gordon model.
- **Free Cash Flow (FCF)**: Cash generated by operations minus capital expenditures. Used to value firms that do not pay dividends.
- **Required Rate of Return (r)**: The minimum return investors demand given the stock's risk level. From CAPM: r = R_f + Beta x (R_m - R_f).
- **Sustainable Growth Rate**: g = ROE x Retention Ratio. The rate at which a firm can grow using only internally generated funds.
- **Retention Ratio (Plowback Ratio)**: The fraction of earnings retained (not paid as dividends). Retention = 1 - Payout Ratio.
- **Market Capitalization**: Stock price x shares outstanding. The market's total valuation of the firm's equity.

## Core Concepts

### General DDM
**P_0 = D_1/(1+r)^1 + D_2/(1+r)^2 + D_3/(1+r)^3 + ...**

This infinite sum is impractical without a simplifying assumption about dividend growth.

### Zero-Growth Model (Perpetuity)
If dividends are constant forever (D_1 = D_2 = ... = D):
**P_0 = D / r**

Used for preferred stock valuation.

Example: Preferred stock pays $5/year dividend. Required return = 8%.
P_0 = $5 / 0.08 = $62.50

### Constant Growth (Gordon) Model
If dividends grow at constant rate g:
**P_0 = D_1 / (r - g)**

Where D_1 = D_0 x (1 + g). Only valid when r > g.

Example: Last dividend was $2.00, growth rate = 5%, required return = 12%.
D_1 = $2.00 x 1.05 = $2.10
P_0 = $2.10 / (0.12 - 0.05) = $2.10 / 0.07 = $30.00

If the stock trades at $25, it appears undervalued (intrinsic $30 > market $25).

### Implied Return from Gordon Model
Rearranging: **r = (D_1 / P_0) + g** = Dividend Yield + Capital Gains Yield

This shows total return has two components:
1. Dividend yield: the cash income relative to price
2. Capital gains yield: price appreciation, which equals the growth rate g

### Two-Stage Growth Model
For companies with temporary high growth (g_1) for T years, then constant growth (g_2) thereafter:

P_0 = PV of dividends during high-growth phase + PV of terminal value

Terminal value at year T: P_T = D_{T+1} / (r - g_2)
P_0 = sum from t=1 to T of [D_t / (1+r)^t] + P_T / (1+r)^T

Example: D_0 = $1, g_1 = 20% for 3 years, then g_2 = 5% forever. r = 15%.
Year 1: D_1 = $1.20, Year 2: D_2 = $1.44, Year 3: D_3 = $1.728
Terminal value at year 3: P_3 = D_4 / (r - g_2) = $1.728 x 1.05 / (0.15 - 0.05) = $1.8144 / 0.10 = $18.144
P_0 = 1.20/1.15 + 1.44/1.15^2 + 1.728/1.15^3 + 18.144/1.15^3
P_0 = 1.043 + 1.089 + 1.136 + 11.928 = $15.20

### Free Cash Flow Valuation
For firms that don't pay dividends, value the entire firm first:
Firm Value = PV of expected future free cash flows
Equity Value = Firm Value - Debt
Price per share = Equity Value / Shares Outstanding

### Relative Valuation (Multiples)
- P/E Ratio: Compare a stock's P/E to industry average. Higher P/E implies higher growth expectations.
- Price-to-Book: Market value vs. book (accounting) value.
- Price-to-Sales: Useful for firms without profits.
- EV/EBITDA: Enterprise value divided by EBITDA. Popular for comparing firms with different capital structures.

## Examples

### Example 1: Preferred Stock
A preferred stock pays a quarterly dividend of $1.50 (= $6.00/year). Required return = 10%.
P_0 = $6.00 / 0.10 = $60.00

### Example 2: Estimating Growth
A firm has ROE = 15% and pays out 40% of earnings as dividends.
Retention ratio = 1 - 0.40 = 0.60
Sustainable growth rate = 0.15 x 0.60 = 9%

### Example 3: Stock Overvaluation
Stock trades at $50. D_1 = $2.00, g = 4%, r = 10%.
Intrinsic value = $2.00 / (0.10 - 0.04) = $33.33
The stock appears overvalued by $16.67. A rational investor would sell or avoid buying.

## Common Misconceptions
- **A stock that doesn't pay dividends is worthless under DDM**: Not true. The DDM assumes eventual dividends (or terminal sale). In practice, free cash flow or relative valuation methods are used for non-dividend stocks.
- **Growth is always good for stock price**: Only growth that earns a return above the required rate of return adds value. Growing at a rate below the cost of capital actually destroys value.
- **The Gordon model works for any growth rate**: It only works when g < r. If g >= r, the formula gives nonsensical results (negative or infinite prices).
- **P/E ratio directly tells you if a stock is cheap or expensive**: P/E must be compared to growth expectations, risk, and industry peers. A "cheap" P/E may reflect poor growth prospects.
- **Intrinsic value is a precise number**: It depends entirely on assumptions about future dividends, growth, and required return. Small changes in inputs cause large changes in value.
