# Financial Statements and Ratio Analysis

## Overview
Financial statements are the primary means by which companies communicate their financial health to investors, creditors, and managers. The three core statements -- income statement, balance sheet, and statement of cash flows -- each reveal different aspects of firm performance. Ratio analysis transforms raw financial data into standardized metrics that enable comparison across firms, industries, and time periods.

Financial managers use ratio analysis for internal decision-making, while external analysts use it to assess creditworthiness, profitability, and investment potential. The DuPont system decomposes return on equity into its component drivers, providing insight into what is truly driving firm performance.

## Key Terms
- **Income Statement**: Reports revenues, expenses, and net income over a specific period. Shows profitability. Also called the profit and loss (P&L) statement.
- **Balance Sheet**: Reports assets, liabilities, and stockholders' equity at a specific point in time. The fundamental equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity.
- **Statement of Cash Flows**: Reports cash inflows and outflows from operating, investing, and financing activities over a period. Reconciles net income to actual cash generated.
- **Revenue (Sales)**: The total amount earned from selling goods or services before any expenses are deducted.
- **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)**: The direct costs of producing goods sold by a company (materials, direct labor, manufacturing overhead).
- **Gross Profit**: Revenue - COGS. Measures how efficiently a company produces its products.
- **Operating Income (EBIT)**: Earnings Before Interest and Taxes. Revenue - COGS - Operating Expenses. Measures core business profitability.
- **Net Income**: The "bottom line." Revenue minus all expenses, including COGS, operating expenses, interest, and taxes.
- **EBITDA**: Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. A proxy for operating cash flow.
- **Current Assets**: Assets expected to be converted to cash within one year (cash, accounts receivable, inventory).
- **Current Liabilities**: Obligations due within one year (accounts payable, short-term debt, accrued expenses).
- **Working Capital**: Current Assets - Current Liabilities. Measures short-term liquidity.
- **Retained Earnings**: Cumulative net income minus cumulative dividends paid. The portion of profits reinvested in the business.
- **Depreciation**: The systematic allocation of a long-term asset's cost over its useful life. A non-cash expense that reduces taxable income.
- **Common-Size Statement**: A financial statement where each line item is expressed as a percentage of a base figure (revenue for income statement, total assets for balance sheet).

## Core Concepts

### Liquidity Ratios
Measure the firm's ability to meet short-term obligations.

- **Current Ratio** = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
  - Above 1.0 means the firm can cover short-term debts. Industry norms vary (2.0 is a common benchmark).
- **Quick Ratio (Acid Test)** = (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities
  - More conservative because it excludes inventory, which may be slow to convert to cash.
- **Cash Ratio** = Cash / Current Liabilities
  - The most conservative liquidity measure.

### Efficiency (Activity) Ratios
Measure how effectively the firm uses its assets.

- **Inventory Turnover** = COGS / Average Inventory
  - Higher = selling inventory faster. Days in inventory = 365 / Inventory Turnover.
- **Accounts Receivable Turnover** = Revenue / Average Accounts Receivable
  - Higher = collecting from customers faster. Days sales outstanding = 365 / AR Turnover.
- **Total Asset Turnover** = Revenue / Total Assets
  - Measures how efficiently the firm uses all its assets to generate revenue.
- **Fixed Asset Turnover** = Revenue / Net Fixed Assets
  - How efficiently the firm uses its property, plant, and equipment.

### Leverage (Debt) Ratios
Measure the firm's use of debt financing and ability to service debt.

- **Debt Ratio** = Total Debt / Total Assets
  - What fraction of assets is financed by debt.
- **Debt-to-Equity Ratio** = Total Debt / Total Equity
  - How much debt per dollar of equity. Higher = more leveraged.
- **Equity Multiplier** = Total Assets / Total Equity = 1 + D/E ratio
- **Times Interest Earned (TIE)** = EBIT / Interest Expense
  - How many times over the firm can cover its interest obligations. Higher is safer.
- **Debt Service Coverage Ratio** = EBIT / (Interest + Principal Repayments)
  - More comprehensive than TIE because it includes principal payments.

### Profitability Ratios
Measure the firm's ability to generate profits.

- **Gross Profit Margin** = Gross Profit / Revenue
- **Operating Profit Margin** = EBIT / Revenue
- **Net Profit Margin** = Net Income / Revenue
- **Return on Assets (ROA)** = Net Income / Total Assets
  - How effectively the firm uses assets to generate profit.
- **Return on Equity (ROE)** = Net Income / Total Equity
  - The return earned on shareholders' investment. The most watched profitability metric.

### Market Value Ratios
Link accounting data to stock market performance.

- **Earnings Per Share (EPS)** = Net Income / Shares Outstanding
- **Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio** = Stock Price / EPS
  - High P/E may indicate growth expectations or overvaluation.
- **Market-to-Book Ratio** = Market Value per Share / Book Value per Share
- **Dividend Yield** = Annual Dividends per Share / Stock Price
- **Dividend Payout Ratio** = Dividends / Net Income

### DuPont Analysis
Decomposes ROE into three components to identify performance drivers:

**ROE = Net Profit Margin x Total Asset Turnover x Equity Multiplier**
**ROE = (Net Income / Revenue) x (Revenue / Total Assets) x (Total Assets / Equity)**

This shows ROE is driven by: (1) profit margins, (2) asset efficiency, and (3) financial leverage. A firm can improve ROE by improving any of these three components.

Extended 5-factor DuPont:
ROE = (Net Income/EBT) x (EBT/EBIT) x (EBIT/Revenue) x (Revenue/Assets) x (Assets/Equity)
= Tax Burden x Interest Burden x Operating Margin x Asset Turnover x Equity Multiplier

## Examples

### Example 1: Liquidity Analysis
Company A has current assets of $500,000 (including $200,000 inventory) and current liabilities of $300,000.
Current Ratio = $500,000 / $300,000 = 1.67
Quick Ratio = ($500,000 - $200,000) / $300,000 = 1.00
The current ratio looks healthy, but the quick ratio reveals the firm heavily depends on inventory to cover obligations.

### Example 2: DuPont Decomposition
Firm X: Net income $50M, Revenue $500M, Total Assets $400M, Equity $200M.
Net Profit Margin = $50M / $500M = 10%
Asset Turnover = $500M / $400M = 1.25
Equity Multiplier = $400M / $200M = 2.0
ROE = 10% x 1.25 x 2.0 = 25%
The 25% ROE is driven equally by solid margins, good asset usage, and moderate leverage.

### Example 3: Cross-Firm Comparison
Firm A: P/E = 25, Net Margin = 15%, ROE = 20%
Firm B: P/E = 10, Net Margin = 5%, ROE = 18%
Firm A trades at a premium (higher P/E) because of stronger margins. Firm B achieves similar ROE despite lower margins, likely through higher leverage or asset turnover -- which increases risk.

## Relationships
- Liquidity ratios relate to working capital management (Ch. 10)
- Profitability ratios connect to cost of capital -- more profitable firms may have lower cost of equity
- Leverage ratios connect to capital structure decisions (Ch. 9)
- DuPont analysis ties together margins, efficiency, and leverage into one framework
- Market ratios link to stock valuation (Ch. 6)
- Cash flow statement connects to capital budgeting (Ch. 7) and working capital (Ch. 10)

## Common Misconceptions
- **Higher ratios are always better**: Not true. A very high current ratio may indicate the firm is not efficiently using its assets. A very high inventory turnover might mean the firm stocks too little.
- **Ratios are meaningful in isolation**: A single ratio without context (industry benchmarks, trends, peer comparison) provides limited insight. Always compare against industry averages.
- **Net income equals cash flow**: Net income includes non-cash items like depreciation and accruals. A profitable firm can still run out of cash. Always check the cash flow statement.
- **Book value equals market value**: Book values are historical costs. Market values reflect current expectations. The market-to-book ratio captures this gap.
- **Leverage always increases ROE**: Leverage amplifies both gains and losses. If ROA > cost of debt, leverage increases ROE. If ROA < cost of debt, leverage decreases ROE.
