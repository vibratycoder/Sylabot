## Global IT

### Key Terms & Definitions
- **Global IT**: The strategic planning, implementation, and management of IT systems across international boundaries to support multinational business operations
- **IT Governance**: The framework that ensures IT investments support business goals, manage risks, and allocate resources effectively across an organization
- **IT Infrastructure**: The composite hardware, software, networks, and facilities that underpin an organization's IT services and operations
- **Offshoring**: Relocating IT operations or services to another country, typically to reduce costs or access specialized talent
- **Outsourcing**: Contracting IT functions to external providers rather than performing them in-house
- **Data Sovereignty**: The concept that data is subject to the laws and regulations of the country in which it is collected or stored
- **IT Platform**: The underlying technology stack (hardware, OS, middleware, databases) on which business applications are built and run
- **Cross-Border Data Flow**: The transfer of digital information across national boundaries, subject to varying regulatory requirements
- **IT Strategic Alignment**: Ensuring that IT investments, capabilities, and initiatives directly support the organization's overall business strategy
- **Digital Infrastructure Disparity**: The gap in technology infrastructure quality and availability between developed and developing regions

### Core Concepts (Q&A Format)
- Q: What are the four key issues in global IT management?
- A: (1) Linking global IT to global business strategy, (2) IT platforms, (3) international data sharing, and (4) cultural environments.

- Q: How does global IT provide competitive advantage?
- A: By increasing organizational control, enhancing coordination across regions, and opening access to new global markets.

- Q: Why is data sovereignty a challenge for global IT?
- A: Different countries have different laws governing data storage and transfer, so multinational firms must comply with varying regulations in each jurisdiction.

- Q: What role does IT play in connecting a multinational enterprise?
- A: IT serves as the connective system enabling dispersed teams to operate as a single cohesive unit through shared platforms, communication tools, and data systems.

- Q: What is the difference between offshoring and outsourcing?
- A: Offshoring moves operations to another country (may still be in-house); outsourcing contracts work to an external provider (may be domestic or foreign).

- Q: Why must global IT strategies account for cultural differences?
- A: Technology use, communication styles, and attitudes toward data sharing vary across cultures, affecting adoption rates and system effectiveness.

- Q: What are the main challenges of managing distributed IT teams?
- A: Time zone differences, language barriers, cultural misunderstandings, inconsistent infrastructure, and varying regulatory environments.

### Important Facts & Figures
- Global IT management emerged as a distinct discipline in the 1990s as businesses rapidly internationalized
- Cross-border data privacy laws (e.g., EU GDPR, China PIPL) can restrict how data is stored and transferred internationally
- Infrastructure disparities mean internet speeds and reliability vary dramatically between regions
- IT spending as a percentage of revenue typically ranges from 3-8% across industries globally
- The global IT services market exceeds $1 trillion annually
- Effective global IT requires balancing standardization (efficiency) with localization (cultural fit)
- Cloud computing has significantly reduced barriers to global IT deployment

### Common Exam Questions
- Q: Explain how global IT strategy must align with global business strategy. Provide an example.
- A: IT investments must directly support business goals such as market expansion or supply chain integration. Example: a retailer deploying a unified ERP system across 20 countries to standardize inventory management.

- Q: Identify and explain three major challenges of managing IT across international boundaries.
- A: (1) Cross-border data privacy regulations requiring compliance in each jurisdiction, (2) infrastructure disparities limiting system performance in developing regions, (3) cultural differences affecting technology adoption and communication patterns.

- Q: What is data sovereignty and why does it matter for global organizations?
- A: Data sovereignty means data is subject to the laws of the country where it resides. It matters because organizations must ensure compliance with local regulations when storing or transferring data across borders, or face legal penalties.

## Information Culture

### Key Terms & Definitions
- **Information Culture**: The values, norms, and practices within an organization or society that shape how information is created, shared, managed, and used
- **Knowledge Sharing**: The process of exchanging information, insights, expertise, and experiences between individuals or teams within an organization
- **Explicit Knowledge**: Knowledge that is documented and easily codified, such as policies, SOPs, manuals, and databases
- **Tacit Knowledge**: Experience-based insights, intuition, and skills that are difficult to articulate or document but critical for decision-making
- **Implicit Knowledge**: Know-how gained through practice and application, such as shortcuts or process tips, that can be transferred through observation
- **Knowledge Silo**: An organizational condition where information is trapped within a department or team and not shared with the broader organization
- **Knowledge Management (KM)**: The systematic process of capturing, organizing, sharing, and leveraging an organization's collective knowledge
- **Information Asymmetry**: A situation where one party in a transaction or relationship has more or better information than the other
- **Communities of Practice**: Groups of people who share a concern or passion for something and learn how to do it better through regular interaction
- **Organizational Learning**: The process by which an organization improves itself over time through gaining experience and using that experience to create knowledge

### Core Concepts (Q&A Format)
- Q: What three elements have a critical impact on knowledge sharing in organizations?
- A: (1) A knowledge-sharing culture, (2) information technology infrastructure, and (3) employee motivation.

- Q: How does trust affect information culture?
- A: In organizations where members trust each other, they are more willing to exchange knowledge and to embrace knowledge from others, creating a virtuous cycle of sharing.

- Q: What is the difference between explicit and tacit knowledge?
- A: Explicit knowledge is documented and easily shared (e.g., manuals); tacit knowledge is experience-based, intuitive, and difficult to articulate or transfer.

- Q: Why do knowledge silos form and how can they be broken?
- A: Silos form due to departmental boundaries, lack of incentives, or poor technology. They can be broken through cross-functional teams, collaboration platforms, leadership support, and incentive structures.

- Q: Why is information culture important in a global business context?
- A: What works in one country may fail in another; cultural norms around hierarchy, openness, and communication style affect how freely information flows.

- Q: What are the benefits of a strong information-sharing culture?
- A: Higher productivity, better employee engagement, improved morale, faster innovation, and enhanced problem-solving.

### Important Facts & Figures
- Organizations with strong knowledge-sharing cultures report up to 35% higher employee productivity
- Three types of organizational knowledge: explicit, tacit, and implicit
- Key methods for sharing knowledge: informal discussions, structured training, documented resources, collaboration platforms, and digital knowledge bases
- Leadership support is consistently identified as the single most important factor in fostering an information-sharing culture
- Cultural dimensions (e.g., Hofstede's power distance, individualism) directly influence information-sharing behavior across countries
- Knowledge management failures cost Fortune 500 companies an estimated $31.5 billion annually
- Technology alone cannot create an information culture; organizational values and incentives must also be aligned

### Common Exam Questions
- Q: Compare and contrast explicit and tacit knowledge. Give an example of each.
- A: Explicit knowledge is codified and documented (e.g., an employee handbook). Tacit knowledge is personal, experiential, and hard to formalize (e.g., a salesperson's intuition about when a deal will close).

- Q: Explain how cultural differences impact information sharing in multinational organizations.
- A: High power-distance cultures may discourage subordinates from sharing information upward; individualistic cultures may hoard knowledge for personal advantage; collectivist cultures may share more freely within in-groups but not with outsiders.

- Q: What factors must an organization address to build a strong information-sharing culture?
- A: Trust among employees, leadership support and modeling, incentive structures that reward sharing, accessible technology platforms, and clear processes for knowledge capture and dissemination.

## International Technology Adoption

### Key Terms & Definitions
- **Digital Divide**: The gap between populations that have access to modern information and communication technology and those that have limited or no access
- **Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)**: A framework proposing that perceived ease of use and perceived usefulness are the two primary factors determining technology adoption
- **Diffusion of Innovations**: Everett Rogers' theory explaining how new technologies spread through populations via communication channels over time among members of a social system
- **Digital Literacy**: The ability to use information and communication technologies to find, evaluate, create, and communicate information
- **Technology Transfer**: The process of moving technology, knowledge, and skills from one country or organization to another
- **Adoption Rate**: The speed at which a new technology is taken up by a population or market
- **Innovation Adopter Categories**: Rogers' five categories -- Innovators (2.5%), Early Adopters (13.5%), Early Majority (34%), Late Majority (34%), Laggards (16%)
- **Leapfrogging**: When developing countries skip intermediate technology stages and adopt the latest technologies directly (e.g., mobile banking without landlines)
- **ICT4D**: Information and Communication Technologies for Development -- using technology to improve economic and social conditions in developing countries
- **Regulatory Environment**: The laws, policies, and government actions that encourage or impede technology adoption in a country

### Core Concepts (Q&A Format)
- Q: What three conditions impact technology adoption at the country level?
- A: (1) Technological conditions (attributes and accessibility of the technology), (2) organizational conditions (country's framework, resources, communications), and (3) environmental conditions (market features, industries, legal/regulatory framework).

- Q: What does the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) predict?
- A: TAM predicts that perceived ease of use and perceived usefulness are the two primary factors that determine whether a user will adopt a technology.

- Q: What are Rogers' five adopter categories?
- A: Innovators (2.5%), Early Adopters (13.5%), Early Majority (34%), Late Majority (34%), and Laggards (16%).

- Q: What is technological leapfrogging? Give an example.
- A: When developing countries skip older technologies and adopt cutting-edge ones directly. Example: Sub-Saharan Africa largely skipped landline telephones and adopted mobile phones directly.

- Q: How does the digital divide manifest beyond simple access?
- A: It exists along dimensions of geography, education, age, income, and gender. The young are ~16 percentage points more likely to use the internet than the elderly; high-education individuals are ~15 points more likely than low-education individuals.

- Q: Why should adoption policy focus on meaningful use rather than mere access?
- A: Access alone does not guarantee benefits; people must have the skills, motivation, and support to use technology productively for it to create value.

### Important Facts & Figures
- The digital divide exists along dimensions of geography, education, age, income, gender, and firm size
- Youth (ages 16-24) are approximately 16 percentage points more likely to use the internet than the elderly (ages 55-74)
- High-education individuals are about 15 percentage points more likely to use the internet than low-education individuals
- Major barriers in developing countries: inadequate infrastructure, high costs, digital illiteracy, unsupportive regulatory frameworks
- Women in many developing countries face a digital gender divide compounding broader inequalities
- Key adoption drivers: market competition, cybersecurity readiness, ease of doing business, political stability, access to foreign knowledge
- Mobile phone adoption has been the single most impactful technology in reducing the global digital divide

### Common Exam Questions
- Q: Explain the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) and its two key constructs.
- A: TAM proposes that perceived usefulness (belief that the technology improves performance) and perceived ease of use (belief that using the technology is effortless) are the primary determinants of whether someone adopts a technology.

- Q: Identify four barriers to technology adoption in developing economies.
- A: (1) Inadequate digital infrastructure, (2) high costs of access, (3) low digital literacy among the population, (4) unsupportive or restrictive regulatory frameworks.

- Q: What is the digital divide and what dimensions does it encompass?
- A: The digital divide is the gap between those with access to modern ICT and those without. It spans geography, income, education, age, gender, and firm size.

- Q: Describe Rogers' Diffusion of Innovations theory and the five adopter categories.
- A: Rogers theorized that innovations spread through social systems via communication channels over time. Adopters fall into five categories by timing: Innovators (2.5%), Early Adopters (13.5%), Early Majority (34%), Late Majority (34%), and Laggards (16%).

## Databases

### Key Terms & Definitions
- **Database**: An organized collection of structured data managed electronically by a Database Management System (DBMS)
- **DBMS (Database Management System)**: Software that provides tools for creating, querying, updating, and administering databases securely and efficiently
- **Relational Database**: A database that organizes data into tables (relations) with rows and columns, using keys to establish relationships between tables
- **Table (Relation)**: A structured set of data organized in rows (records/tuples) and columns (fields/attributes)
- **Primary Key**: A column (or set of columns) that uniquely identifies each row in a table; cannot be NULL or duplicated
- **Foreign Key**: A column in one table that references the primary key in another table, creating a relationship between the two tables
- **SQL (Structured Query Language)**: The standard language for querying and manipulating relational databases
- **Normalization**: The process of organizing database tables to reduce redundancy and improve data integrity, typically through 1NF, 2NF, and 3NF
- **ACID Properties**: Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, Durability -- four properties that guarantee reliable database transactions
- **Data Integrity**: The accuracy, consistency, and reliability of data throughout its lifecycle, enforced through constraints and rules
- **Referential Integrity**: A rule ensuring that foreign key values always reference valid primary key values in the related table
- **Query**: A request for data from a database, typically written in SQL using the SELECT statement

### Core Concepts (Q&A Format)
- Q: What is the difference between a primary key and a foreign key?
- A: A primary key uniquely identifies each row in its own table and cannot be NULL. A foreign key is a column that references the primary key of another table, establishing a relationship between tables and can be NULL.

- Q: What is normalization and why is it important?
- A: Normalization is organizing tables to eliminate redundancy and ensure data dependencies are logical. It reduces storage, prevents update anomalies, and maintains data integrity.

- Q: What are the three normal forms?
- A: 1NF: all columns hold atomic (single) values. 2NF: meets 1NF and no non-key attribute depends on only part of a composite key. 3NF: meets 2NF and no non-key attribute depends on another non-key attribute (no transitive dependencies).

- Q: What are the ACID properties?
- A: Atomicity (all-or-nothing transactions), Consistency (transactions leave the database in a valid state), Isolation (concurrent transactions don't interfere), Durability (committed transactions survive system failures).

- Q: What are common data integrity constraints?
- A: NOT NULL (field must have a value), UNIQUE (no duplicate values), PRIMARY KEY (unique + not null), FOREIGN KEY (references another table's primary key), CHECK (validates data against a condition).

- Q: What is referential integrity?
- A: It ensures that a foreign key value in one table always corresponds to a valid primary key value in the referenced table, preventing orphaned records.

- Q: Name four common DBMS platforms.
- A: MySQL, PostgreSQL, Microsoft SQL Server, and Oracle Database.

### Important Facts & Figures
- SQL was developed at IBM in the 1970s and became an ANSI standard in 1986
- The relational model was proposed by Edgar F. Codd at IBM in 1970
- Common SQL commands: SELECT, INSERT, UPDATE, DELETE, CREATE TABLE, ALTER TABLE, DROP TABLE
- A well-designed relational database typically achieves at least Third Normal Form (3NF)
- Relational databases dominate business applications including ERP, CRM, e-commerce, and HR systems
- Primary keys can be natural (meaningful data like SSN) or surrogate (system-generated like auto-increment IDs)
- Joins (INNER, LEFT, RIGHT, FULL) combine data from multiple related tables based on key relationships
- Indexes speed up query performance but add overhead to write operations

### Common Exam Questions
- Q: Define primary key and foreign key. Explain how they create relationships between tables.
- A: A primary key uniquely identifies each row in a table. A foreign key in one table references the primary key of another table, creating a link. For example, an Orders table's CustomerID foreign key references the Customers table's CustomerID primary key.

- Q: Explain the three normal forms with an example for each.
- A: 1NF: A student table where each cell has one value (not multiple phone numbers in one cell). 2NF: Splitting a table so that non-key fields depend on the entire primary key, not just part of it. 3NF: Removing fields like ZipCode-to-City lookup into a separate table to eliminate transitive dependency.

- Q: What are the ACID properties and why are they important for database transactions?
- A: ACID (Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, Durability) ensures transactions are reliable. Without them, partial updates could corrupt data, concurrent users could overwrite each other's changes, and system crashes could lose committed work.

- Q: Write a basic SQL query to retrieve all customers from New York.
- A: SELECT * FROM Customers WHERE State = 'New York';

## Spreadsheets

### Key Terms & Definitions
- **Spreadsheet**: A computer application for organizing, analyzing, and storing data in a tabular grid of cells arranged in rows and columns
- **Workbook**: A spreadsheet file (e.g., an Excel .xlsx file) that can contain multiple worksheets
- **Worksheet**: A single tab or sheet within a workbook, consisting of a grid of cells
- **Cell**: The intersection of a row and a column, identified by its address (e.g., A1, B5); the basic unit for entering data
- **Formula**: An expression in a cell that performs a calculation, always beginning with an equals sign (=)
- **Function**: A predefined formula that performs a specific calculation (e.g., SUM, AVERAGE, COUNT, IF)
- **VLOOKUP**: Vertical Lookup -- searches the first column of a range and returns a value from a specified column in the same row
- **INDEX-MATCH**: A more flexible lookup combination; INDEX returns a value from a position, MATCH finds the position of a lookup value
- **Pivot Table**: A data summarization tool that aggregates large datasets dynamically through drag-and-drop, without requiring formulas
- **Conditional Formatting**: Rules that automatically change cell appearance (color, font, icons) based on cell values
- **Absolute Reference ($A$1)**: A cell reference that does not change when a formula is copied to another cell
- **Relative Reference (A1)**: A cell reference that adjusts automatically when a formula is copied to another cell

### Core Concepts (Q&A Format)
- Q: What is the difference between a formula and a function in Excel?
- A: A formula is any calculation expression starting with = (e.g., =A1+B1). A function is a predefined, named formula that performs a specific operation (e.g., =SUM(A1:A10)).

- Q: What are the four arguments of VLOOKUP?
- A: (1) lookup_value -- what you are searching for, (2) table_array -- the range containing the data, (3) col_index_num -- which column to return a value from, (4) range_lookup -- TRUE for approximate match, FALSE for exact match.

- Q: What is the advantage of INDEX-MATCH over VLOOKUP?
- A: INDEX-MATCH can look up values in any direction (not just left-to-right), is not affected by column insertions/deletions, and generally performs better on large datasets.

- Q: How does a pivot table work?
- A: A pivot table summarizes large datasets by letting users drag fields into rows, columns, values, and filters to dynamically aggregate data (sum, count, average) without writing formulas.

- Q: What is the difference between absolute and relative cell references?
- A: Relative references (A1) change when copied to other cells; absolute references ($A$1) remain fixed. Mixed references lock either the row ($A1) or column (A$1).

- Q: Name the seven basic Excel functions.
- A: SUM, AVERAGE, COUNT, IF, VLOOKUP, HLOOKUP, and CONCATENATE.

- Q: What is conditional formatting used for?
- A: To automatically highlight cells based on their values using colors, data bars, icons, or custom rules -- making patterns, outliers, and trends visually obvious.

### Important Facts & Figures
- Microsoft Excel was first released in 1985 for Macintosh and 1987 for Windows
- Excel supports over 450 built-in functions across categories: math, statistical, logical, text, date, lookup, and financial
- Maximum worksheet size: 1,048,576 rows by 16,384 columns (since Excel 2007)
- VLOOKUP is the most commonly tested Excel function in business courses and job interviews
- Pivot tables can summarize millions of rows of data in seconds without formulas
- Common chart types: bar, column, line, pie, scatter, area, and combo charts
- Data validation restricts what values can be entered in a cell (e.g., dropdowns, number ranges)
- Excel remains the most widely used business analysis tool worldwide

### Common Exam Questions
- Q: Write a VLOOKUP formula to find an employee's department from a lookup table in cells E2:G100.
- A: =VLOOKUP(A2, E2:G100, 3, FALSE) -- looks up the value in A2, searches column E, and returns the value from the 3rd column (G) with an exact match.

- Q: Explain the difference between absolute and relative references. When would you use each?
- A: Relative references adjust when copied (useful for repeating calculations across rows). Absolute references stay fixed (useful when referring to a constant like a tax rate in one cell). Example: =A1*$B$1 multiplies each row's value by the fixed rate in B1.

- Q: Describe what a pivot table does and give a business use case.
- A: A pivot table dynamically summarizes data by grouping and aggregating values. Use case: a sales manager dragging "Region" to rows, "Month" to columns, and "Revenue" to values to see total sales by region and month.

- Q: What are three advantages of using functions instead of manual calculations?
- A: (1) Speed -- functions compute instantly across large ranges, (2) accuracy -- eliminates manual calculation errors, (3) dynamic updating -- results recalculate automatically when source data changes.
