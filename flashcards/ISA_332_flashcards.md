## E-business

### Key Terms & Definitions
- **E-business**: The conduct of all business activities, transactions, and processes over the internet and digital networks, including supply chain management, customer service, and internal operations
- **E-commerce**: A subset of e-business focused specifically on buying and selling goods and services online
- **B2B (Business-to-Business)**: Transactions between two businesses, such as a manufacturer selling to a wholesaler
- **B2C (Business-to-Consumer)**: Transactions where a business sells directly to individual end consumers
- **C2C (Consumer-to-Consumer)**: Transactions between individual consumers facilitated by a third-party platform
- **C2B (Consumer-to-Business)**: Model where individuals sell products or services to companies (e.g., freelancers on Upwork)
- **Value Proposition**: The reason a customer should buy from a particular business over competitors
- **Revenue Model**: The strategy a business uses to generate income (e.g., direct sales, subscriptions, advertising, freemium)
- **Intranet**: A private internal network used within an organization for communication and collaboration
- **Extranet**: A controlled private network that allows access to authorized external partners, suppliers, or customers
- **Scalability**: The ability of an e-business system to handle growing amounts of work or traffic without performance degradation
- **Digital Storefront**: The online equivalent of a physical store where products/services are displayed and sold

### Core Concepts (Q&A Format)
- Q: What is the key difference between e-business and e-commerce?
- A: E-commerce focuses only on buying and selling online, while e-business encompasses ALL digitally conducted business operations including supply chain management, CRM, employee collaboration, and internal processes.

- Q: What are the four foundational e-business models?
- A: B2B (business-to-business), B2C (business-to-consumer), C2C (consumer-to-consumer), and C2B (consumer-to-business).

- Q: What five key ingredients define a business model?
- A: Value proposition, revenue model, market opportunity, competitive environment, and market strategy.

- Q: How does the brokerage model work in e-business?
- A: A brokerage model brings buyer and seller organizations together on the web, earning fees or commissions for facilitating transactions.

- Q: What strategic elements are essential for e-business success?
- A: SEO/SEM for traffic acquisition, mobile-friendly user experience, analytics-driven decision making, and scalability planning.

- Q: What is the difference between an intranet and an extranet?
- A: An intranet is a private internal network for employees only; an extranet extends controlled access to external partners, suppliers, or customers.

- Q: Name five common e-business revenue models.
- A: Direct sales, subscriptions, advertising, commissions/transaction fees, and freemium offerings.

### Important Facts & Figures
- The global B2B e-commerce market is roughly 5 times the size of B2C
- E-business uses internet, intranet, and extranet technologies
- Eight finite e-business models include: direct customer, full-service provider, intermediary, whole of enterprise, shared infrastructure, virtual community, value net integrator, and content provider
- Core e-business components include digital storefront, payment processing, supply chain integration, CRM, and data analytics
- The community model allows users to share data, opinions, and content (e.g., Wikipedia, Facebook)
- The subscription model charges recurring fees for ongoing access to services
- E-business has transformed every industry by enabling digital channels for revenue, engagement, and efficiency

### Common Exam Questions
- Q: Explain the relationship between e-business and e-commerce with examples. A: E-commerce is a subset of e-business. E-commerce = buying/selling (Amazon transactions). E-business = all digital operations including e-commerce plus supply chain, HR systems, CRM, and internal collaboration tools.
- Q: Compare and contrast the B2B and B2C models. A: B2B involves higher volume orders, longer sales cycles, contract-based relationships, and accounts for ~5x the B2C market. B2C features shorter sales cycles, impulse-driven purchasing, and targets individual consumers.
- Q: What is a value proposition in e-business and why does it matter? A: A value proposition explains why a customer should choose one business over competitors. It matters because it drives customer acquisition and differentiation in a crowded digital marketplace.
- Q: Identify and describe three specific e-business model types beyond B2B/B2C/C2C/C2B. A: Brokerage (connects buyers and sellers for fees), Subscription (charges recurring payments for access), and Community (users share content and data, monetized through ads or premium features).

## E-commerce models

### Key Terms & Definitions
- **B2B (Business-to-Business)**: Commerce between businesses; characterized by bulk orders, long sales cycles, and contract-based pricing
- **B2C (Business-to-Consumer)**: The most common model where businesses sell directly to end consumers (e.g., Amazon retail)
- **C2C (Consumer-to-Consumer)**: Individuals sell to other individuals via third-party platforms (e.g., eBay, Facebook Marketplace)
- **C2B (Consumer-to-Business)**: Individuals sell products or services to businesses (e.g., Upwork, stock photography sites)
- **Marketplace Model**: A platform that connects multiple sellers with buyers, earning revenue through commissions and listing fees
- **SaaS Model**: Software as a Service delivers applications via subscription with recurring revenue
- **Subscription Model**: Customers pay regularly (monthly/annually) for ongoing access to products or services
- **Disintermediation**: The removal of intermediaries (middlemen) from a supply chain through direct digital sales
- **Reintermediation**: The reintroduction of intermediaries in e-commerce (e.g., Amazon as a new middleman between manufacturers and consumers)
- **Platform Intermediation**: The role of C2C platforms acting as facilitators between individual buyers and sellers
- **D2C (Direct-to-Consumer)**: Brands selling directly to consumers without retailers or wholesalers as intermediaries
- **B2B2C (Business-to-Business-to-Consumer)**: A business sells to another business, which then sells to the end consumer

### Core Concepts (Q&A Format)
- Q: Why is the B2B market significantly larger than B2C?
- A: B2B transactions involve bulk/wholesale volumes at higher price points, recurring contracts, and every product passes through multiple B2B transactions in the supply chain before reaching a consumer.

- Q: What role does a marketplace platform play in e-commerce?
- A: It acts as an intermediary connecting multiple third-party sellers with buyers, providing the platform infrastructure and earning revenue through commissions, listing fees, and advertising.

- Q: What is the key advantage of C2C platforms for sellers?
- A: Low barrier to entry -- individuals can sell without needing their own website, payment processing, or marketing infrastructure.

- Q: How does the subscription model differ from traditional one-time purchase models?
- A: Subscriptions provide predictable recurring revenue, encourage long-term customer relationships, and shift the focus from acquisition to retention.

- Q: What is disintermediation and why is it significant in e-commerce?
- A: Disintermediation is the removal of middlemen from the supply chain. It allows manufacturers to sell directly to consumers, reducing costs but disrupting traditional distribution channels.

- Q: How does B2B2C work in practice?
- A: A manufacturer (B1) sells to a retailer or platform (B2), which then sells to the end consumer (C). Example: a brand selling through Amazon's marketplace.

- Q: What distinguishes D2C from B2C?
- A: D2C means the brand itself sells directly to consumers without any retail intermediaries, while B2C can include retailers who buy from manufacturers and resell to consumers.

### Important Facts & Figures
- The global B2B e-commerce market is approximately 5x larger than B2C
- eBay and Craigslist pioneered the C2C model in the early days of the internet
- The European Credit Card Dataset commonly used in e-commerce fraud research contains 284,807 transactions
- C2C platforms typically earn revenue through transaction fees and listing fees rather than inventory ownership
- Marketplace models (Amazon, Alibaba) have become dominant e-commerce platforms worldwide
- SaaS/subscription models provide recurring revenue which investors value highly due to predictability
- Account-based pricing in B2B differs fundamentally from fixed pricing in B2C

### Common Exam Questions
- Q: Compare B2B and B2C e-commerce models across four dimensions. A: Sales cycle (B2B longer, B2C shorter), order volume (B2B higher, B2C lower), pricing (B2B negotiated/contract-based, B2C fixed), decision-making (B2B rational/committee, B2C emotional/individual).
- Q: Explain how a marketplace model generates revenue without owning inventory. A: Marketplaces charge sellers commissions on each sale, listing fees, advertising/promoted placement fees, and subscription fees for premium seller accounts, while the sellers own and ship the inventory.
- Q: What is the difference between disintermediation and reintermediation? A: Disintermediation removes middlemen (manufacturer sells direct online). Reintermediation introduces new digital middlemen (Amazon/eBay become the new intermediary between sellers and buyers).
- Q: Why might a company choose a subscription model over a one-time purchase model? A: Predictable recurring revenue, higher customer lifetime value, ongoing relationship enabling upselling, lower churn through habit formation, and more accurate financial forecasting.

## Internet protocols

### Key Terms & Definitions
- **TCP (Transmission Control Protocol)**: A connection-oriented transport layer protocol that ensures reliable, ordered delivery of data packets between applications
- **IP (Internet Protocol)**: The network layer protocol responsible for addressing and routing data packets to their correct destination using IP addresses
- **TCP/IP Model**: The four-layer networking model (Application, Transport, Internet, Network Access) that forms the foundation of internet communication
- **HTTP (Hypertext Transfer Protocol)**: The application layer protocol governing how web pages and data are transferred between browsers and servers; operates on port 80
- **HTTPS**: HTTP with SSL/TLS encryption added for secure communication; operates on port 443
- **DNS (Domain Name System)**: The system that translates human-readable domain names into numerical IP addresses; uses port 53
- **SSL/TLS**: Security protocols that encrypt data transmitted between a client and server, ensuring confidentiality and integrity
- **UDP (User Datagram Protocol)**: A connectionless transport protocol that sends data without establishing a connection; faster but less reliable than TCP
- **FTP (File Transfer Protocol)**: An application layer protocol for transferring files between computers on a network
- **SMTP (Simple Mail Transfer Protocol)**: The protocol used for sending email messages between servers
- **Packet**: A small unit of data transmitted over a network, containing both payload data and header information for routing
- **Port Number**: A numerical identifier (0-65535) that specifies which application or service should receive incoming network data

### Core Concepts (Q&A Format)
- Q: What are the four layers of the TCP/IP model from top to bottom?
- A: Application Layer (HTTP, DNS, FTP, SMTP), Transport Layer (TCP, UDP), Internet Layer (IP), Network Access Layer (Ethernet, Wi-Fi).

- Q: How does TCP differ from UDP?
- A: TCP is connection-oriented, guarantees ordered and error-free delivery, and uses handshaking. UDP is connectionless, faster, but does not guarantee delivery or order -- used for streaming and gaming.

- Q: What is the three-way handshake in TCP?
- A: A connection establishment process: (1) Client sends SYN, (2) Server responds with SYN-ACK, (3) Client sends ACK. This ensures both sides are ready to communicate.

- Q: How does DNS resolution work?
- A: When you type a domain name, your computer queries DNS servers that look up the corresponding IP address. The process goes: local cache, recursive resolver, root server, TLD server, authoritative server, then returns the IP address.

- Q: Why is HTTPS important for e-business?
- A: HTTPS encrypts all data between browser and server using SSL/TLS, protecting sensitive information like credit card numbers, login credentials, and personal data from interception.

- Q: What is the difference between HTTP and HTTPS?
- A: HTTP transmits data in plain text (port 80), while HTTPS adds SSL/TLS encryption (port 443) to secure the data in transit. HTTPS is required for any site handling sensitive information.

- Q: Why does DNS use UDP rather than TCP for most queries?
- A: DNS queries are small and need fast responses. UDP's lack of connection overhead makes it faster for these brief lookups. TCP is used only for larger DNS responses (zone transfers).

### Important Facts & Figures
- HTTP accounts for over 90% of internet traffic
- HTTP operates on port 80; HTTPS on port 443; DNS on port 53; SSH on port 22; FTP on port 21; SMTP on port 25
- The TCP/IP model has 4 layers; the OSI model has 7 layers
- TCP uses a three-way handshake (SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK) to establish connections
- IP addresses come in two versions: IPv4 (32-bit, ~4.3 billion addresses) and IPv6 (128-bit, virtually unlimited)
- DNS functions as the "phone directory" of the internet
- SSL/TLS certificates are required for HTTPS and are issued by Certificate Authorities (CAs)
- Each TCP/IP layer adds its own header information to data (encapsulation)

### Common Exam Questions
- Q: Describe the TCP/IP model and explain the role of each layer. A: Network Access (physical data transmission), Internet (IP addressing and routing), Transport (TCP/UDP end-to-end communication), Application (user-facing protocols like HTTP, DNS, SMTP).
- Q: Explain how a web page is loaded using HTTP and DNS. A: Browser extracts domain from URL, DNS resolves domain to IP address, browser establishes TCP connection to server, sends HTTP GET request, server responds with HTML/CSS/JS, browser renders the page.
- Q: Compare TCP and UDP. When would you use each? A: TCP for reliable delivery (web browsing, email, file transfer). UDP for speed-sensitive applications where some packet loss is acceptable (video streaming, online gaming, VoIP).
- Q: Why is understanding internet protocols important for e-business? A: Every online transaction relies on these protocols. HTTP/HTTPS enables web commerce, DNS enables findability, TCP ensures reliable data transfer, and SSL/TLS secures sensitive business data.

## Digital marketing

### Key Terms & Definitions
- **SEO (Search Engine Optimization)**: The process of improving organic (unpaid) search visibility by optimizing website content, structure, and technical performance
- **SEM (Search Engine Marketing)**: A broader term encompassing SEO and paid search advertising; commonly refers to paid search (PPC) campaigns
- **PPC (Pay-Per-Click)**: An advertising model where advertisers pay each time a user clicks their ad
- **SERP (Search Engine Results Page)**: The page displayed by a search engine after a user submits a query, containing both organic and paid results
- **Content Marketing**: Creating and distributing valuable, relevant content to attract and retain a target audience
- **SMM (Social Media Marketing)**: Promoting products/services through organic posts and paid ads on social media platforms
- **Email Marketing**: Sending targeted, personalized messages to subscribers for lead nurturing and customer retention
- **Keywords**: The specific words and phrases users type into search engines; the foundation of both SEO and SEM strategies
- **Organic Traffic**: Website visitors who arrive through unpaid search engine results
- **Paid Traffic**: Website visitors who arrive through paid advertisements
- **Bounce Rate**: The percentage of visitors who leave a website after viewing only one page
- **Conversion Rate**: The percentage of visitors who complete a desired action (purchase, sign-up, download)

### Core Concepts (Q&A Format)
- Q: What is the key difference between SEO and SEM?
- A: SEO focuses on earning organic (free) search traffic through optimization; SEM is broader and includes paid search advertising (PPC). SEO is long-term; SEM provides immediate results.

- Q: What are the three main pillars of SEO?
- A: Technical SEO (site speed, mobile-friendliness, crawlability), On-page SEO (content, meta tags, keywords, internal links), and Off-page SEO (backlinks, domain authority, social signals).

- Q: Why do high-performing brands use multiple digital marketing channels?
- A: Different channels reach audiences at different stages of the buyer's journey. Combining SEO, SEM, content, social, and email creates multiple touchpoints, reduces dependency on one channel, and maximizes overall ROI.

- Q: How does keyword research drive digital marketing strategy?
- A: Keywords reveal what customers are searching for, their intent, and competition levels. This data informs content creation, ad targeting, and SEO optimization to match user demand.

- Q: What makes email marketing effective despite being one of the oldest digital channels?
- A: It offers direct access to subscribers, high personalization capability, low cost per contact, and consistently delivers one of the highest ROI among digital channels.

- Q: What is the difference between organic and paid search results on a SERP?
- A: Organic results are earned through SEO and appear based on relevance/authority. Paid results are advertisements purchased through PPC bidding and appear at the top/bottom of the SERP marked as "Ad."

- Q: What is A/B testing in digital marketing?
- A: Comparing two versions of a webpage, ad, or email to see which performs better, changing one variable at a time to identify what drives higher conversion rates.

### Important Facts & Figures
- Organizations across all sectors are shifting budgets from traditional to digital channels
- SEO results are long-term (3-6+ months to see impact); PPC delivers immediate traffic
- Email marketing consistently has one of the highest ROI of any digital marketing channel
- Google dominates the search engine market with approximately 90%+ market share globally
- Content marketing costs 62% less than traditional marketing while generating 3x more leads
- Social media platforms offer precise audience targeting based on demographics, interests, and behaviors
- Digital marketing skills are among the most in-demand across business sectors

### Common Exam Questions
- Q: Compare and contrast SEO and SEM as marketing strategies. A: SEO is organic/free, takes months, builds long-term authority, and earns trust. SEM is paid, provides immediate visibility, offers precise targeting, and stops when budget runs out. Best used together.
- Q: Explain the role of content marketing in a digital strategy. A: Content marketing builds long-term credibility and trust through valuable content (blogs, videos, ebooks). It supports SEO by generating keyword-rich pages, provides material for social and email channels, and nurtures leads through the sales funnel.
- Q: What metrics would you use to measure digital marketing success? A: Traffic metrics (sessions, unique visitors), engagement (bounce rate, time on site), conversion rate, cost per acquisition (CPA), return on ad spend (ROAS), email open/click rates, and customer lifetime value (CLV).
- Q: Why is an omnichannel digital marketing approach more effective than relying on a single channel? A: Customers interact across multiple platforms. Omnichannel ensures consistent messaging, captures users at different journey stages, reduces risk from algorithm changes on any single platform, and maximizes reach and engagement.

## Online advertising

### Key Terms & Definitions
- **PPC (Pay-Per-Click)**: Advertising model where the advertiser pays only when a user clicks on the ad
- **CPC (Cost Per Click)**: The actual price paid for each click in a PPC campaign
- **CPM (Cost Per Mille)**: The cost per 1,000 ad impressions; commonly used for display and brand awareness campaigns
- **CTR (Click-Through Rate)**: The ratio of users who click an ad to total users who view it, expressed as a percentage (clicks / impressions x 100)
- **CPA (Cost Per Acquisition)**: The total cost to acquire one customer through advertising
- **ROAS (Return on Ad Spend)**: Revenue generated per dollar spent on advertising (revenue / ad spend)
- **Programmatic Advertising**: Automated buying and selling of ad inventory through algorithms and real-time bidding
- **RTB (Real-Time Bidding)**: An auction-based system where ad impressions are bought and sold in milliseconds as a webpage loads
- **Display Advertising**: Visual ads (banners, images, videos) placed on websites and apps
- **Retargeting/Remarketing**: Showing ads to users who previously visited a website to encourage return and conversion
- **Impression**: A single instance of an ad being displayed on a user's screen
- **Conversion**: A completed desired action by a user after clicking an ad (purchase, sign-up, download)

### Core Concepts (Q&A Format)
- Q: How does programmatic advertising differ from traditional ad buying?
- A: Programmatic uses algorithms and real-time bidding to automate ad purchasing in milliseconds, enabling precise audience targeting at scale. Traditional buying involves manual negotiations, insertion orders, and less granular targeting.

- Q: What is the difference between CPM and CPC pricing?
- A: CPM charges per 1,000 impressions regardless of clicks (good for brand awareness). CPC charges only when someone clicks (good for driving traffic and conversions).

- Q: How does retargeting work and why is it effective?
- A: Retargeting places a cookie/pixel on visitors' browsers, then shows them relevant ads on other sites they visit. It is effective because it targets users who already showed interest, improving conversion rates.

- Q: What is Real-Time Bidding (RTB)?
- A: When a user loads a webpage, an auction occurs in milliseconds where advertisers bid for that specific impression based on the user's profile. The highest bidder's ad is displayed, all happening before the page finishes loading.

- Q: Why is CTR an important but incomplete metric?
- A: CTR measures engagement (clicks vs. impressions) but does not measure whether clicks led to actual conversions or revenue. A high CTR with low conversion rate wastes ad spend.

- Q: What is the difference between reach and frequency in advertising?
- A: Reach is the number of unique individuals who see an ad. Frequency is the average number of times each person sees it. Balancing both prevents ad fatigue while ensuring sufficient exposure.

- Q: How is ROAS calculated and what does it indicate?
- A: ROAS = Revenue from ads / Cost of ads. A ROAS of 4:1 means $4 revenue for every $1 spent. It indicates the efficiency and profitability of advertising campaigns.

### Important Facts & Figures
- Online advertising is the primary revenue model for Google and Meta (Facebook)
- Programmatic advertising accounts for the majority of digital display ad spending
- The average display ad CTR is approximately 0.1% across industries
- Retargeted visitors are significantly more likely to convert than first-time visitors
- Google Ads (formerly AdWords) is the largest PPC platform globally
- Video completion rate is a key metric for video advertising effectiveness
- Ad fraud (bots generating fake clicks/impressions) costs advertisers billions annually
- View-through conversions track users who saw an ad but converted later without clicking

### Common Exam Questions
- Q: Explain how PPC advertising works on a search engine. A: Advertisers bid on keywords. When a user searches that keyword, an auction determines which ads appear based on bid amount and quality score. Advertisers pay only when their ad is clicked. Position depends on bid x quality score.
- Q: Compare display advertising and search advertising. A: Display uses visual ads on websites targeting browsing users (push marketing, CPM pricing, brand awareness). Search targets users actively searching for keywords (pull marketing, CPC pricing, direct response/conversion).
- Q: What metrics should a marketer track to evaluate an online ad campaign? A: Impressions and reach (exposure), CTR (engagement), conversion rate (effectiveness), CPA (efficiency), ROAS (profitability), and frequency (ad fatigue management).
- Q: Explain programmatic advertising and its advantages over traditional ad buying. A: Programmatic automates ad buying through real-time bidding and algorithms. Advantages: precise audience targeting, real-time optimization, massive scale across millions of sites, lower costs through efficiency, and data-driven decision making.
- Q: What is retargeting and when should it be used? A: Retargeting shows ads to users who previously visited your site but did not convert. Use it when you have sufficient website traffic, want to recover abandoned carts, or need to keep your brand top-of-mind during long purchase decisions.

## Digital delivery

### Key Terms & Definitions
- **SaaS (Software as a Service)**: Software delivered over the internet on a subscription basis, with the vendor handling hosting, maintenance, and updates
- **IaaS (Infrastructure as a Service)**: Cloud-based computing resources (servers, storage, networking) provided on demand (e.g., AWS, Azure)
- **PaaS (Platform as a Service)**: A cloud platform providing tools and infrastructure for developers to build applications without managing underlying hardware
- **CDN (Content Delivery Network)**: A geographically distributed network of servers that caches and delivers content from locations closest to the user
- **Streaming**: Real-time delivery of media content (audio, video, games) without requiring permanent downloads
- **Digital Download**: One-time transfer of a complete digital file (software, ebook, music) to a user's device
- **PoP (Point of Presence)**: A data center within a CDN that serves content to nearby users, reducing latency
- **Latency**: The delay between a user's request and the delivery of content; lower latency means faster delivery
- **White-Label Reselling**: Selling another company's product or service under your own brand
- **API (Application Programming Interface)**: A set of protocols allowing different software systems to communicate and share data
- **Freemium**: A pricing model offering basic features for free while charging for premium features
- **Bitrate**: The amount of data processed per unit of time in media streaming; higher bitrate generally means higher quality

### Core Concepts (Q&A Format)
- Q: What are the three main cloud service models?
- A: SaaS (ready-to-use applications like Salesforce), PaaS (development platforms like Heroku), and IaaS (computing infrastructure like AWS EC2). Each represents a different level of abstraction and vendor management.

- Q: How does a CDN improve content delivery?
- A: A CDN caches content on geographically distributed servers (PoPs). When a user requests content, it is served from the nearest PoP rather than the origin server, reducing latency and improving load times.

- Q: What is the difference between streaming and digital downloads?
- A: Streaming delivers content in real time for immediate consumption without permanent storage (Netflix, Spotify). Downloads transfer the complete file for permanent local storage (buying a song on iTunes, downloading software).

- Q: Why has SaaS become the dominant software delivery model?
- A: SaaS eliminates installation/maintenance burden for users, provides automatic updates, offers subscription pricing (lower upfront cost), enables access from any device, and allows vendors to scale easily.

- Q: What are the main digital distribution channels?
- A: Self-serve subscriptions, app store marketplaces (Apple App Store, Google Play), API partnerships, white-label reselling, and direct download platforms (Steam).

- Q: How does adaptive bitrate streaming work?
- A: The streaming service automatically adjusts video/audio quality based on the user's internet speed and device capabilities, ensuring smooth playback by lowering bitrate during congestion and increasing it when bandwidth is available.

- Q: What role do APIs play in digital delivery?
- A: APIs allow different systems to communicate, enabling integrations (e.g., a payment gateway connecting to an e-commerce site), third-party app development, and automated data exchange between platforms.

### Important Facts & Figures
- SaaS is now the primary way companies deliver software applications
- CDN PoPs are distributed globally to minimize latency and maximize availability
- Netflix, Spotify, and YouTube are the leading examples of streaming delivery platforms
- Steam is the dominant digital download platform for PC gaming
- The app store model (Apple, Google) typically takes a 15-30% commission on sales
- Cloud services (SaaS, PaaS, IaaS) offer scalability, cost-effectiveness, and global accessibility
- Digital delivery has become the dominant distribution model across software, media, and education
- CDNs reduce bandwidth costs for content providers by distributing the load across multiple servers

### Common Exam Questions
- Q: Compare and contrast SaaS, PaaS, and IaaS with examples. A: SaaS delivers ready-to-use apps (Google Workspace, Salesforce). PaaS provides development platforms (Heroku, Google App Engine). IaaS offers raw computing resources (AWS EC2, Azure VMs). As you move from IaaS to SaaS, the vendor manages more and the customer manages less.
- Q: Explain how a CDN works and why it is important for e-business. A: A CDN distributes cached copies of content across global server nodes (PoPs). Users receive content from the nearest node, reducing latency and load times. This is critical for e-business because slow page loads increase bounce rates and decrease conversions.
- Q: Why has digital delivery disrupted traditional distribution? A: Digital delivery eliminates physical production/shipping costs, enables instant global distribution, allows automatic updates, supports subscription pricing for recurring revenue, and provides data analytics on usage patterns.
- Q: Describe the freemium model and explain when it is most effective. A: Freemium offers basic features for free and charges for premium features. It is most effective when the product has a large potential user base, the free version demonstrates clear value, and a meaningful percentage of free users convert to paid.
