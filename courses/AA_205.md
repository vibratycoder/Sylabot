### Applied analytics

Applied analytics is the strategic use of data, statistical methods, and computational techniques to transform raw information into actionable business insights. It bridges the gap between raw data collection and meaningful decision-making by applying rigorous analytical frameworks to real-world problems. The field encompasses the full analytics maturity spectrum, from understanding what has already occurred to recommending future courses of action.

- **Four types of analytics:** Descriptive analytics answers "What happened?" by summarizing historical data through dashboards and reports. Diagnostic analytics answers "Why did it happen?" using drill-down analysis, correlation, and segmentation to identify root causes. Predictive analytics answers "What will happen?" by applying regression, forecasting, and machine learning to project future outcomes. Prescriptive analytics answers "What should we do?" by combining predictive models with optimization techniques, business rules, and simulations to recommend optimal actions.
- **Key concepts:** Statistical analysis, machine learning, predictive modeling, data-driven decision making, business intelligence, A/B testing, hypothesis testing, and optimization
- **Common tools:** Python (pandas, scikit-learn), R, Tableau, Power BI, SQL, TensorFlow, Excel, and SAS
- **The analytics pipeline:** Data collection, cleaning, exploratory analysis, modeling, validation, deployment, and monitoring form a continuous cycle of improvement
- **Core skills:** Statistical literacy, critical thinking, domain expertise, data wrangling, and the ability to communicate findings to non-technical stakeholders

**Practical relevance:** Organizations across virtually every industry rely on applied analytics to guide major decisions, from optimizing pricing strategies and predicting customer churn to detecting fraud and allocating resources. The ability to move beyond descriptive reporting toward predictive and prescriptive insights gives businesses a significant competitive advantage in data-rich environments.

### Data visualization

Data visualization is the graphical representation of data and information using visual elements like charts, graphs, and maps to make complex data more accessible, understandable, and actionable. Effective visualizations must accurately convey the data without misleading or distorting the underlying information. The field draws heavily on the foundational work of Edward Tufte, a Yale professor widely regarded as the leading authority on data visualization design.

- **Tufte's core principles:** Graphical integrity demands that visuals tell the truth; the "Lie Factor" (ratio of visual effect size to data effect size) should stay between 0.95 and 1.05. The data-ink ratio should be maximized by eliminating "chart junk," meaning decorative elements that add no informational value. Visual representations should be directly proportional to the numerical quantities they depict.
- **Design guidelines:** Show data variation rather than design variation; use clear labeling and annotations placed directly on the graphic; employ small multiples to display large datasets across many dimensions; pursue high data density within reason
- **Chart types and when to use them:** Bar charts for comparing categories, line charts for trends over time, scatter plots for relationships between variables, histograms for distributions, heat maps for density patterns, and small multiples for faceted comparisons
- **Data encoding methods:** Position, aligned lengths, angles, area, brightness, color hue, and shape each carry different levels of perceptual accuracy, with position being the most precise
- **Common pitfalls:** Truncated axes that exaggerate differences, 3D effects that distort proportions, dual-axis charts that imply false correlations, and excessive color palettes that obscure rather than clarify

**Practical relevance:** Data visualization is the primary means of communicating analytical findings to stakeholders. Strong visuals enable viewers to discover insights, compare data side by side, and identify patterns that would be invisible in raw numerical tables, making visualization a critical skill for any analytics professional.

### Text mining

Text mining (also called text data mining or text analytics) is the process of extracting meaningful information and insights from large amounts of unstructured text data using natural language processing (NLP), machine learning, and statistical techniques. It sits at the crossroads of data analysis, machine learning, and NLP, transforming documents, social media posts, and web content into structured, analyzable data. Given that an estimated 80% of business data exists in unstructured text form, text mining is an essential capability for modern analytics.

- **Core techniques:** Sentiment analysis determines the emotional tone of text (positive, negative, neutral, or specific emotions like joy or anger). Topic modeling discovers abstract themes across document collections. Text classification assigns predefined categories to documents. Named entity recognition identifies people, organizations, locations, and dates. Document clustering groups similar texts without predefined labels.
- **Preprocessing pipeline:** Language identification, tokenization (splitting text into words or phrases), stemming and lemmatization (reducing words to root forms), stop word removal (filtering common words like "the" and "is"), part-of-speech tagging, and syntax parsing. Proper preprocessing is critical because it directly affects the quality of downstream analysis.
- **Relationship to NLP:** While NLP is the broader field concerned with enabling computers to understand and generate human language, text mining specifically focuses on extracting patterns and actionable knowledge from text corpora. The two disciplines frequently share techniques such as word embeddings, transformer models, and statistical language models.
- **Data sources:** Customer reviews, social media posts, news articles, emails, support tickets, research papers, legal documents, and web pages
- **Evaluation metrics:** Precision, recall, F1-score, and accuracy for classification tasks; coherence scores for topic models

**Practical relevance:** Text mining enables organizations to unlock insights at scale from unstructured data sources. Real-world applications include brand sentiment monitoring on social media, automated document categorization in legal and compliance workflows, customer feedback analysis for product improvement, and competitive intelligence gathering from news and industry reports.

### Data mining

Data mining is the process of discovering patterns, correlations, and anomalies in large datasets using methods at the intersection of machine learning, statistics, and database systems. It is the core analysis step within the broader Knowledge Discovery in Databases (KDD) process. Unlike simple querying or reporting, data mining applies sophisticated algorithms to uncover hidden structures and relationships that are not immediately apparent in raw data.

- **Core methods:** Association rule mining discovers relationships between variables (e.g., market basket analysis revealing that customers who buy bread often buy butter). Classification assigns data points to predefined categories using algorithms like decision trees, naive Bayes, and support vector machines. Clustering groups similar data points without predefined labels using methods like k-means and hierarchical clustering. Regression predicts continuous numerical outcomes. Anomaly detection identifies unusual observations that deviate significantly from expected patterns.
- **KDD process (5 stages):** Selection (choosing relevant data), preprocessing (cleaning and handling missing values), transformation (normalizing and reducing dimensionality), data mining (applying algorithms to extract patterns), and interpretation/evaluation (validating and presenting discovered knowledge)
- **CRISP-DM framework:** The Cross-Industry Standard Process for Data Mining, published in 1999, remains the most widely used methodology at approximately 43% adoption. Its six iterative phases are business understanding, data understanding, data preparation, modeling, evaluation, and deployment. Unlike KDD's sequential approach, CRISP-DM emphasizes iteration, where outcomes from any phase can loop back to earlier stages.
- **Alternative methodology -- SEMMA:** Developed by SAS Institute, SEMMA (Sample, Explore, Modify, Model, Assess) offers a streamlined, statistically rigorous approach focused on the technical modeling workflow
- **Key evaluation metrics:** Accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, ROC curves, and cross-validation for assessing model quality and generalizability

**Practical relevance:** Data mining helps organizations extract actionable knowledge from massive datasets across industries. Common applications include customer segmentation for targeted marketing, fraud detection in financial transactions, market basket analysis in retail, predictive maintenance in manufacturing, and clinical decision support in healthcare.

### Cross-disciplinary analytics

Cross-disciplinary analytics applies data science methods across multiple fields and domains, integrating knowledge from computer science, mathematics/statistics, and specific application areas. Data science is itself defined as an interdisciplinary field that synthesizes statistics, informatics, computing, communication, management, and sociology to study data and its environments. The strength of cross-disciplinary analytics lies in its collaborative nature, enabling experts from diverse disciplines to generate insights that no single field could produce alone.

- **Application domains:** Healthcare combines medical expertise with machine learning to develop personalized treatment plans based on a patient's genetic makeup and health history. Environmental science uses data analytics for climate modeling, pollution tracking, ecosystem management, and biodiversity monitoring. Public policy leverages analytics for crime pattern identification, traffic optimization, transit planning, fair housing analysis, and education financing. Finance applies cross-disciplinary methods for risk modeling, algorithmic trading, and regulatory compliance. Marketing integrates behavioral psychology with predictive analytics for customer journey optimization.
- **Three pillars of data science:** Computer science and technology (data engineering, programming, systems), mathematics and statistics (modeling, inference, probability), and domain expertise (subject-matter knowledge that gives context to analytical results). Effective cross-disciplinary work requires competence across all three pillars.
- **Key challenges:** Communication barriers arise because different disciplines use their own jargon, methodologies, and ways of interpreting data. Integrating heterogeneous data sources with varying formats, scales, and quality standards is technically demanding. Building shared mental models across team members with fundamentally different training backgrounds requires deliberate effort.
- **Core skills:** Domain expertise, statistical literacy, data communication and storytelling, collaborative problem-solving, comfort with ambiguity, and the ability to translate between technical and non-technical audiences

**Practical relevance:** Cross-disciplinary analytics drives innovation by connecting analytical methods with real-world domain problems. For example, combining genomic data with clinical records enables precision medicine, while integrating satellite imagery with socioeconomic data supports more effective disaster relief planning and urban development strategies.
