### Marketing analytics

Marketing analytics is the practice of tracking, measuring, and analyzing data across all marketing efforts to evaluate performance against quantitative goals. It encompasses the collection, management, and application of marketing data to inform strategic decision-making and optimize marketing spend. The discipline draws on descriptive, predictive, and prescriptive analytics to provide a complete picture of past performance, future trends, and recommended actions.

- **Descriptive Analytics** surfaces historical performance data (e.g., web traffic, conversion rates) to explain what happened in past campaigns and establish performance baselines.
- **Predictive Analytics** uses statistical models and machine learning to forecast future outcomes such as customer churn likelihood, demand trends, and campaign response rates.
- **Prescriptive Analytics** combines descriptive and predictive insights to generate actionable recommendations, such as optimal budget allocation or channel mix adjustments.
- **Attribution Modeling** measures the contribution of each marketing touchpoint along the customer journey, ranging from simple first-touch/last-touch models to advanced multi-touch and algorithmic approaches.
- **A/B and Multivariate Testing** systematically compare variations of marketing elements (headlines, images, CTAs) to identify statistically significant performance differences.
- **Data Integration** involves consolidating information from web analytics tools, CRM systems, social media platforms, surveys, and point-of-sale systems to build a unified view of customer interactions.

**Practical Applications:** Organizations use marketing analytics to allocate budgets more effectively across channels, personalize customer experiences at scale, and demonstrate the financial impact of marketing activities to executive stakeholders.

### Customer segmentation

Customer segmentation is the process of dividing a customer base into distinct groups that share similar characteristics, behaviors, or needs. It enables businesses to tailor marketing strategies, product offerings, and service delivery to specific audience segments rather than applying a one-size-fits-all approach. Modern segmentation leverages both traditional categorical methods and advanced machine learning techniques to uncover meaningful patterns within large datasets.

- **Demographic Segmentation** organizes customers by attributes such as age, gender, income level, education, and marital status, providing a foundational layer for targeting strategies.
- **Behavioral Segmentation** groups customers based on purchasing patterns, product usage frequency, brand interactions, and engagement levels across channels.
- **Psychographic Segmentation** classifies customers by lifestyle, values, attitudes, and interests, enabling deeper emotional resonance in messaging and positioning.
- **RFM Analysis (Recency, Frequency, Monetary)** is a proven quantitative framework that scores customers based on how recently they purchased, how often they buy, and how much they spend, identifying high-value segments.
- **Cluster Analysis** applies unsupervised machine learning algorithms such as K-means and hierarchical clustering to automatically identify natural groupings within customer data without predefined categories.
- **Latent Class Analysis** uses probabilistic statistical models to identify unobserved subgroups within a population, reducing researcher bias in segment definition.

**Practical Applications:** Retailers use customer segmentation to deliver personalized product recommendations and targeted promotions, while B2B firms apply it to prioritize sales outreach and customize account-based marketing strategies for high-value prospects.

### Campaign analysis

Campaign analysis is the discipline of tracking, measuring, and optimizing the performance of marketing campaigns across multiple channels with a revenue-first perspective. It ties every dollar of marketing spend to measurable outcomes such as customer acquisition, retention, and lifetime value. Effective campaign analysis combines real-time monitoring with post-campaign evaluation to continuously improve marketing effectiveness and inform future strategy.

- **Key Performance Metrics** include return on ad spend (ROAS), cost per acquisition (CPA), conversion rate, click-through rate (CTR), and engagement rate, each providing a different lens on campaign effectiveness.
- **Multi-Touch Attribution** assigns credit across all customer touchpoints rather than relying solely on first-touch or last-touch models, providing a more accurate picture of which channels and tactics drive conversions.
- **A/B Testing and Experimentation** involves running controlled experiments on campaign elements such as creative assets, audiences, and messaging to identify statistically significant performance improvements.
- **Cohort Analysis** tracks groups of customers who share a common characteristic (e.g., acquisition date) over time to measure long-term campaign impact on retention and lifetime value.
- **SMART Goal Setting** ensures campaigns are evaluated against Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound objectives, aligning metrics with desired business outcomes.
- **Cross-Channel Analysis** evaluates how campaigns perform across paid search, social media, email, display, and offline channels to optimize the overall marketing mix.

**Practical Applications:** E-commerce companies use campaign analysis to reallocate ad spend from underperforming channels to high-converting ones in real time, while brand marketers apply post-campaign analysis to refine creative strategies and audience targeting for future launches.

### Marketing ROI

Marketing ROI (Return on Investment) measures the financial return generated by marketing activities relative to their cost, using the core formula: MROI = (Marketing Value - Marketing Cost) / Marketing Cost, expressed as a percentage. It serves as the primary metric for justifying marketing budgets, comparing channel effectiveness, and demonstrating marketing's contribution to overall business profitability. Comprehensive ROI measurement requires integrating both short-term revenue metrics and long-term value indicators such as customer lifetime value (CLV).

- **Basic ROI Calculation** divides net marketing profit by total marketing cost, but advanced approaches also factor in organic growth baselines, time lag effects, and incremental revenue to avoid overstating marketing impact.
- **Attribution Models** range from simple (first-touch, last-touch) to sophisticated (linear, time-decay, algorithmic), each distributing conversion credit differently across touchpoints and directly affecting ROI calculations.
- **The Four-Legged Stool Framework** (BCG) triangulates marketing ROI through four complementary lenses: marketing mix modeling, incrementality experiments, customer insights, and execution metrics.
- **Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)** expands ROI analysis beyond immediate revenue by calculating the total net profit a customer generates over the entire relationship, capturing long-term marketing impact.
- **Incrementality Testing** uses controlled experiments (holdout groups, geographic splits) to isolate the true causal effect of marketing activities, distinguishing correlation from causation in ROI measurement.
- **Integrated Measurement** combines digital and traditional channel data, quantitative financial metrics and qualitative brand indicators, and short-term and long-term impacts for a holistic ROI picture.

**Practical Applications:** CMOs use marketing ROI frameworks to defend and optimize budget allocations in quarterly business reviews, while analysts apply incrementality testing to prove the causal revenue impact of specific campaigns before scaling spend.
