### Data mining

Data mining is the computational process of discovering patterns, correlations, and anomalies within large datasets to predict outcomes and generate actionable knowledge. It sits at the intersection of machine learning, statistics, and database systems, forming the core analysis step of the Knowledge Discovery in Databases (KDD) process.

- **Core techniques:** Classification, clustering, association rule mining, regression analysis, anomaly detection, time-series analysis
- **Process frameworks:** CRISP-DM (Cross-Industry Standard Process) is the leading methodology, involving business understanding, data understanding, preparation, modeling, evaluation, and deployment
- **KDD pipeline:** Data selection, preprocessing, transformation, data mining, interpretation/evaluation

**Practical relevance:** Data mining enables organizations to extract previously unknown and potentially useful information from massive datasets. In the context of decision-making, it transforms raw data into comprehensible structures that support evidence-based strategies across business, healthcare, finance, and scientific research.

### Pattern discovery

Pattern discovery is the process of detecting regularities, structures, and meaningful relationships within data, including associations, classifications, sequential patterns, clusters, and trends. It integrates statistical methods, machine learning algorithms, and optimization strategies to recognize recurrent patterns and generate predictive insights.

- **Pattern types:** Clusters (natural groupings), associations (co-occurrence relationships), sequences (ordered patterns), trends (directional changes over time), anomalies (deviations from expected behavior)
- **Key techniques:** Association rule mining, Bayesian classification, decision tree analysis, neural networks, genetic algorithms, deviation and trend analysis
- **Integration with ML:** Modern pattern discovery combines data mining with machine learning to extract meaningful information from vast and complex datasets

**Practical relevance:** Pattern discovery forms the cornerstone of modern data science, enabling organizations to uncover hidden relationships in data that drive actionable business insights. It is applied in fraud detection, customer behavior analysis, market research, and scientific discovery across virtually every domain.

### Decision making

Data-driven decision making (DDDM) is the practice of using facts, metrics, and data to guide strategic business decisions that align with organizational goals and objectives. It replaces intuition and guesswork with evidence-based analysis, helping organizations evaluate probabilities, assess trade-offs, and allocate resources more effectively.

- **Core components:** Analytical tools and statistical methods for trend identification, data governance policies for quality and security, dashboards and visualizations for clear communication
- **Analytics progression:** Descriptive (what happened), diagnostic (why), predictive (what will happen), prescriptive (what to do)
- **Key challenges:** Building data-driven culture (only 32.4% of organizations report success despite 98.6% aspiring to it), avoiding technology-first approaches without cultural support

**Practical relevance:** Data-driven organizations consistently outperform peers by using analytics to understand past events, diagnose causes, and forecast future outcomes. Applications span healthcare (patient risk prediction), finance (portfolio optimization), marketing (campaign targeting), and operations (resource allocation and efficiency improvement).

### Problem solving

Analytical problem solving applies structured frameworks and systematic methodologies to decompose complex challenges into manageable components, analyze root causes, and develop evidence-based solutions. It moves beyond intuition by using data and logical reasoning to drive decisions.

- **Structured process:** Define the problem, analyze root causes, generate possible solutions, evaluate and select the best option, implement with follow-up learning
- **Key frameworks:** CRISP-DM (data mining projects), DMAIC (Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control), McKinsey's hypothesis-driven approach, Five Whys (root cause analysis)
- **Analytical tools:** MECE principle (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) for problem decomposition, hypothesis trees, issue trees, Pareto analysis

**Practical relevance:** Structured problem-solving skills are essential for analytics professionals who must translate business questions into analytical approaches. These frameworks ensure that solutions are systematic, repeatable, and evidence-based rather than ad hoc, leading to more reliable outcomes and clearer communication with stakeholders.

### Analytical methods

Analytical methods encompass the statistical and computational techniques used to examine, summarize, and draw conclusions from data. They are broadly divided into descriptive statistics (summarizing data characteristics) and inferential statistics (drawing conclusions about populations from samples).

- **Descriptive analysis:** Measures of central tendency (mean, median, mode), dispersion metrics (range, variance, standard deviation), frequency distributions
- **Inferential techniques:** Hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, t-tests (comparing group means), ANOVA (comparing multiple groups), chi-square tests (categorical relationships)
- **Advanced methods:** Regression analysis, principal component analysis (PCA) for dimensionality reduction, time-series analysis, correlation analysis
- **Selection criteria:** Method choice depends on the study objective, data type and distribution, and nature of observations (paired/unpaired)

**Practical relevance:** Selecting the appropriate analytical method is critical for producing valid and meaningful results. These techniques form the foundation for all data-driven insights, from basic business reporting to advanced predictive modeling, and understanding their assumptions and limitations ensures sound analytical conclusions.
