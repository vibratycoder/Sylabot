# AA 651: Analytics Capstone

**Credits:** 3  
**Level:** graduate  
**Prerequisites:** AA 630, AA 640, AA 645

## Course Description
Opportunity to apply the knowledge and skills acquired to realistic problems that involve large data sets. Students work on business partner projects, develop multiple predictive models using Python, and present findings professionally.

### Capstone project

A graduate analytics capstone is a culminating experience where students apply the full range of knowledge and skills acquired throughout their program to solve realistic problems involving large, complex datasets. These projects mirror professional data science engagements, requiring students to work through the complete analytics lifecycle from problem scoping through model deployment and results communication. Capstones are widely used at leading institutions (Harvard, UChicago, Johns Hopkins, UVA) as the definitive measure of graduate readiness.

- **End-to-end methodology:** Projects follow a structured data science workflow including problem definition, data collection from multiple sources, exploratory data analysis, preprocessing and feature engineering, model building, evaluation, and final reporting
- **Large-scale data handling:** Students work with datasets of sufficient size to qualify as "big data," gaining experience with data pipelines, raw data wrangling, and scalable processing techniques that reflect real industry conditions
- **Multiple model development:** Capstones require building and comparing several predictive models, training algorithms on prepared datasets, calculating baseline accuracy scores, and validating performance using held-out test data
- **Professional deliverables:** Students produce technical documentation, analytical reports detailing methodology and challenges, interactive dashboards, and formal presentations of findings to faculty and industry stakeholders
- **Real-world data sources:** Datasets are typically sourced from industry partners, government agencies, NGOs, or academic research, exposing students to the messiness and complexity of authentic data
- **Team collaboration:** Many capstones involve team-based work that develops project management, communication, and collaborative problem-solving skills essential for professional analytics roles

**Practical Applications:** Graduate capstone projects serve as portfolio-building experiences that demonstrate job-readiness to employers. They provide evidence of a candidate's ability to independently manage a full analytics project, work with stakeholders, and deliver actionable insights under real-world constraints.

### Predictive modeling

Predictive modeling is the process of using statistical algorithms and machine learning techniques to forecast future outcomes based on historical data. In Python, the primary ecosystem for predictive modeling includes scikit-learn, NumPy, and pandas, which together provide tools for data manipulation, model training, evaluation, and deployment. Predictive models fall into two broad categories: regression models (predicting continuous numerical outcomes) and classification models (predicting categorical labels).

- **Regression techniques:** Linear regression for continuous outcomes with linear relationships, polynomial regression for curved relationships, ridge and lasso regression for regularization to prevent overfitting, and gradient boosting regressors for complex non-linear patterns
- **Classification techniques:** Logistic regression for binary classification with interpretable coefficients, support vector machines (SVM) for high-dimensional data with clear separation margins, random forests for robust ensemble-based classification, and neural networks for complex pattern recognition in large datasets
- **Model development workflow:** Data splitting (train/test), feature selection and engineering, model training, hyperparameter tuning (grid search, cross-validation), performance evaluation using metrics such as RMSE, MAE, accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, and AUC-ROC
- **Decision trees and ensembles:** Decision trees offer interpretability and handle both regression and classification; ensemble methods (random forests, gradient boosting, XGBoost) combine multiple weak learners to achieve higher accuracy and robustness against overfitting
- **Model selection criteria:** Choose regression when the target variable is continuous and classification when it is categorical; consider the trade-off between model interpretability and predictive power based on the business context and stakeholder needs
- **Python implementation:** Scikit-learn provides a consistent API (fit, predict, score) across all algorithms, making it straightforward to compare models; pipelines automate preprocessing and modeling steps for reproducible workflows

**Practical Applications:** Predictive modeling drives business decisions across industries, from customer churn prediction and credit risk scoring in finance to demand forecasting in supply chain management. Mastery of multiple modeling techniques in Python enables analysts to select the best approach for each unique business problem and dataset.

### Business partner projects

Business partner projects are collaborative engagements where analytics students work directly with external organizations to solve real business problems using data-driven methods. These partnerships mirror the consulting model used by firms like BCG, PwC, and specialized analytics consultancies, where data scientists extract, transform, and interpret client data to generate actionable business recommendations. This experiential format bridges academic learning and professional practice by introducing students to the dynamics of client-facing analytics work.

- **Project structure:** Students receive a business problem and dataset from an industry partner, define the analytical scope collaboratively, apply appropriate methods (machine learning, statistical analysis, visualization), and deliver findings in a professional format with actionable recommendations
- **Client communication:** Working with business partners requires translating technical concepts into business language, managing stakeholder expectations, providing regular progress updates, and framing results in terms of costs, benefits, and strategic impact rather than model metrics alone
- **Data governance and ethics:** Real client engagements involve navigating data privacy concerns, signing non-disclosure agreements, handling sensitive information responsibly, and ensuring compliance with organizational data policies
- **Consulting skills development:** Students develop competencies in requirements gathering, problem scoping, project management, deadline-driven delivery, and professional relationship building that are critical for analytics careers
- **Cross-functional collaboration:** Business partner projects often require working across departments (marketing, finance, operations) to understand data context, validate assumptions, and ensure analytical outputs align with organizational goals
- **Deliverable standards:** Professional-grade outputs include executive summaries, technical documentation, interactive dashboards, and formal presentations tailored to both technical and non-technical audiences within the partner organization

**Practical Applications:** Business partner projects provide direct exposure to the consulting and client-engagement model that dominates the analytics industry. Students who complete these projects graduate with proven ability to manage stakeholder relationships, deliver under real constraints, and produce work that meets professional standards.

### Professional presentation

Professional presentation in analytics is the discipline of communicating data-driven findings to business audiences through structured narratives, compelling visualizations, and clear recommendations. Data storytelling -- the combination of data, visualization, and narrative -- has become a required skill in analyst job descriptions across industries. Research from Harvard Business School highlights that effective data storytelling puts insights into context and inspires action from decision-makers.

- **Data storytelling framework:** Combine three core elements -- accurate data, purposeful visualizations, and a compelling narrative -- to transform raw analytical outputs into persuasive business communication; studies show 63% of audiences remember stories while only 5% recall individual statistics
- **Narrative structure:** Apply classic storytelling elements (characters, setting, conflict, resolution) to analytical findings; introduce the business problem, present what the data revealed, and conclude with clear recommended actions supported by evidence
- **Visualization best practices:** Create legible, simple, and convincing charts; visualize every comparison made in remarks; use annotations to guide viewer attention; avoid chart junk and unnecessary complexity that distracts from the core message
- **Audience adaptation:** Tailor presentations to the audience's expertise level; executive audiences need high-level strategic implications and ROI framing, while technical teams require methodology details, model performance metrics, and reproducibility information
- **Slide design principles:** Limit each slide to one core idea, lead with the key insight rather than the methodology, use consistent formatting, and ensure all visual elements serve the argument rather than decorating it
- **Delivery and engagement:** Treat presentations as two-way dialogues; anticipate questions about methodology, limitations, and business implications; practice concise responses to challenges; and demonstrate confidence in both the analysis and its practical relevance

**Practical Applications:** Many companies now hire specifically for "data storyteller" positions, recognizing that analytical value is only realized when findings effectively influence decisions. Professionals who master presentation skills consistently advance faster because their work is visible, persuasive, and directly tied to organizational outcomes.
