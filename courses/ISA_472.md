### Cryptography

Cryptography is the science of encoding and decoding information to secure it from unauthorized access, ensuring that data transmitted by a sender can only be read by the intended receiver. Symmetric and asymmetric encryption are the two foundational building blocks of modern cryptographic systems.

**Key Concepts:**
- **Symmetric encryption:** Uses a single shared key for both encryption and decryption. Common algorithms include AES, DES, and Blowfish. Faster and more efficient for large data volumes but requires a secure method to share the key between parties.
- **Asymmetric encryption:** Uses a public key for encryption and a private key for decryption, eliminating the key-distribution challenge. More computationally intensive but enables secure communication without pre-shared secrets.
- **Hybrid approach:** Modern systems frequently combine both methods -- asymmetric encryption securely shares a symmetric key, which then encrypts the actual data quickly and efficiently.
- **Common applications:** File encryption, VPNs, secure data storage, digital signatures, SSL/TLS for web communications, and email encryption.

**Practical Relevance:** Cryptography underpins virtually all aspects of IT security, from securing network communications and protecting stored data to enabling digital signatures and authentication. Understanding the tradeoffs between symmetric and asymmetric approaches is essential for designing secure systems that balance performance with protection.

### Network security

Network security encompasses the technologies, processes, and policies used to monitor, detect, and prevent unauthorized access, misuse, or threats to computer networks and their resources. Intrusion Detection and Prevention Systems (IDPS) are central components that analyze network traffic for signs of malicious activity.

**Key Concepts:**
- **IDS vs. IPS:** An Intrusion Detection System (IDS) monitors and alerts on suspicious events, while an Intrusion Prevention System (IPS) can also take action to block attacks in progress from reaching targeted systems.
- **IDPS types:** Four primary categories exist -- network-based (monitors all network traffic at strategic points), host-based (monitors activity on individual hosts), wireless (monitors wireless network traffic), and network behavior analysis (NBA).
- **Detection methods:** Signature-based detection matches traffic against known attack patterns; anomaly-based detection establishes normal behavior baselines and flags deviations, enabling detection of novel attacks without existing signatures.
- **Defense in depth:** Network security employs layered protections including firewalls, VPNs, network segmentation, encryption, and monitoring tools working together.

**Practical Relevance:** Network security is foundational to organizational cybersecurity posture. As networks grow more complex with cloud services, remote work, and IoT devices, the ability to detect and prevent intrusions in real time becomes increasingly critical for protecting sensitive data and maintaining business continuity.

### Access control

Access control is the security mechanism that regulates who or what can view, use, or modify resources in a computing environment. It involves authentication (verifying a user's identity) and authorization (granting appropriate permissions based on that identity). Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) is the dominant model used by organizations today.

**Key Concepts:**
- **RBAC (Role-Based Access Control):** Assigns access rights based on predefined roles rather than individual users. NIST defines three core rules: role assignment, role authorization, and permission authorization. Over 94% of companies have used RBAC at some point.
- **Other models:** Access Control Lists (ACLs) define rules per individual user, while Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC) dynamically determines permissions based on multiple contextual factors at the time of request.
- **Authentication vs. authorization:** Authentication verifies identity by checking credentials against a directory, while authorization checks roles and grants permissions through the organization's access control scheme.
- **Principle of least privilege:** Users should receive only the minimum access necessary to perform their job functions, reducing the potential impact of compromised accounts.

**Practical Relevance:** RBAC is preferred for large organizations due to its scalability and ease of management compared to user-level ACLs. Effective access control is a cornerstone of security compliance frameworks (SOX, HIPAA, PCI DSS) and is essential for protecting sensitive data, enforcing separation of duties, and maintaining audit trails.

### Firewalls

A firewall is a network security device that monitors and controls incoming and outgoing network traffic based on predefined security rules, acting as a barrier between trusted internal networks and untrusted external networks. Firewalls range from simple packet filters to sophisticated stateful inspection and next-generation systems.

**Key Concepts:**
- **Packet filtering firewalls:** Operate at OSI Layers 3-4, analyzing packets based on IP addresses and port numbers. They are simple and fast but cannot inspect packet payloads for malicious code, making them vulnerable to address spoofing.
- **Stateful inspection firewalls:** Maintain a dynamic state table of active network connections, providing context-aware filtering that tracks the state of each session. This enables more intelligent decisions than stateless packet filtering.
- **Proxy firewalls:** Act as intermediaries between internal and external networks, inspecting traffic at the application layer for deeper security analysis.
- **Next-generation firewalls (NGFW):** Combine traditional firewall capabilities with advanced features like deep packet inspection, intrusion prevention, and application-level awareness.

**Practical Relevance:** Firewalls remain a fundamental first line of defense in network security architecture. Modern organizations typically deploy multiple firewall types in a layered approach, with stateful and next-generation firewalls handling perimeter security while host-based firewalls protect individual endpoints. Understanding firewall types and their limitations is essential for designing effective network security architectures.

### Host hardening

Host hardening (or system hardening) is the process of securing a computer system by reducing its attack surface -- the total number of points where an attacker could potentially gain access. NIST defines it as reducing the attack surface by patching vulnerabilities and turning off nonessential services.

**Key Concepts:**
- **Patch management:** Keeping software patched and up-to-date is critical, as the majority of system compromises exploit known vulnerabilities that have not been patched. Patches should be applied quickly and automatically.
- **Service and software removal:** Disable or remove unused packages, services, and open ports so fewer components are exposed to potential threats.
- **Access controls:** Remove or disable nonessential accounts (default guest and administrator accounts), implement proper identity management, and enforce strong authentication.
- **Encryption and logging:** Encrypt local storage using tools like BitLocker or FileVault, and maintain comprehensive logging for monitoring and incident response.

**Practical Relevance:** Host hardening is a foundational security practice required by compliance mandates such as PCI DSS and HIPAA, and cyber insurers increasingly demand it. When a system's attack surface is smaller, the risk of exploitation, malware injection, or attacker intrusion is significantly reduced. Hardening requires ongoing effort as new vulnerabilities are continuously discovered and systems evolve over time.

### Application security

Application security encompasses the measures, practices, and tools used to protect software applications from vulnerabilities and threats throughout their development lifecycle. The OWASP (Open Web Application Security Project) Foundation provides the leading industry standards for identifying and mitigating application-level security risks.

**Key Concepts:**
- **OWASP Top 10:** The reference standard for the most critical web application security risks. The 2025 update emphasizes software supply chain security, secure configuration, and resilience across the entire software development lifecycle.
- **Testing methodologies:** Static Application Security Testing (SAST) analyzes code without running it; Dynamic Application Security Testing (DAST) tests running applications; Interactive Application Security Testing (IAST) combines both approaches.
- **DevSecOps:** Integrates security practices into DevOps activities, embedding automated security testing throughout the software development lifecycle rather than treating security as an afterthought.
- **OWASP ASVS:** The Application Security Verification Standard provides a basis for testing web application security controls and a list of requirements for secure development.

**Practical Relevance:** Adopting the OWASP Top 10 is considered the most effective first step toward building a development culture focused on producing secure code. As modern applications increasingly depend on third-party libraries and APIs, application security has expanded beyond isolated coding flaws to encompass the security of the entire software ecosystem.

### Data protection

Data protection involves the strategies, technologies, and policies used to safeguard digital information from unauthorized access, corruption, or loss. It spans encryption, access controls, privacy regulations, and breach response, with the EU's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) serving as the most influential modern data privacy framework.

**Key Concepts:**
- **GDPR fundamentals:** The EU's personal data protection law, enacted May 2018, imposes unified rules on any organization processing personal data originating from the EU, regardless of where the organization is located. Non-compliance penalties can reach 20 million euros or 4% of global annual revenue.
- **Encryption as a safeguard:** GDPR identifies encryption as a protective measure that renders personal data unintelligible when a breach occurs. It is considered the best way to protect data during transfer and a key method for securing stored data.
- **Required security measures:** Organizations must implement pseudonymization, encryption, access controls, and monitoring, and must report breaches to supervisory authorities within 72 hours.
- **Data subject rights:** Individuals have rights to manage their personal data through Data Subject Requests (DSRs), and organizations must perform Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs) for high-risk processing.

**Practical Relevance:** Data protection is a legal and ethical imperative for modern organizations. With regulations like GDPR, CCPA, and HIPAA imposing strict requirements, organizations must implement comprehensive data protection programs that address encryption, access controls, breach notification, and ongoing compliance monitoring across all systems that handle sensitive information.

### Incident response

Incident response is the organized approach to addressing and managing the aftermath of a security breach or cyberattack, with the goal of limiting damage, reducing recovery time and costs, and preventing future incidents. The NIST Cybersecurity Framework provides the most widely adopted structured process for incident response.

**Key Concepts:**
- **Phase 1 -- Preparation:** Establishing policies, plans, teams, and tools that form the backbone of response capability before any incident occurs.
- **Phase 2 -- Detection and Analysis:** Identifying potential incidents through monitoring and analyzing the scope, impact, and nature of the threat.
- **Phase 3 -- Containment, Eradication, and Recovery:** Isolating affected systems to prevent spread, removing the threat from the environment, and restoring normal operations.
- **Phase 4 -- Post-Incident Activity:** Conducting formal reviews to evaluate the response, documenting findings, and incorporating lessons learned into updated response plans, training, and toolsets.

**Practical Relevance:** The NIST CSF 2.0 integrates incident response into broader cybersecurity risk management, helping organizations prepare for incidents, reduce their frequency and impact, and improve detection and recovery effectiveness. A well-documented and rehearsed incident response plan is essential for minimizing business disruption and is often required by compliance frameworks and cyber insurance policies.

### Risk management

IT risk management is the structured process of identifying, assessing, mitigating, and monitoring risks that could impact an organization's information systems and technology infrastructure. The NIST Risk Management Framework (RMF) and frameworks like ISO 27001, COBIT, and FAIR provide standardized approaches for managing cybersecurity risks.

**Key Concepts:**
- **NIST RMF lifecycle:** A comprehensive process with seven steps -- Prepare, Categorize, Select, Implement, Assess, Authorize, and Monitor -- providing end-to-end risk management for information systems.
- **Risk assessment process:** Establishing scope and objectives, identifying risks that could impact organizational objectives, assessing likelihood and potential impact, and comparing risks against defined tolerances to prioritize decision-making.
- **Risk mitigation strategies:** Implementing the most cost-effective controls to reduce risk to an acceptable level while minimizing impact on operations. Options include risk avoidance, transfer (e.g., insurance), acceptance, and reduction.
- **Major frameworks:** NIST CSF for cybersecurity risk management, ISO 27001 for information security management systems, COBIT for IT governance, and FAIR for quantitative risk analysis.

**Practical Relevance:** Risk management provides the overarching context for all IT security decisions, helping organizations prioritize their security investments based on the likelihood and potential impact of threats. Effective risk management is not a one-time activity but a continuous cycle of assessment and improvement that aligns security efforts with business objectives and regulatory requirements.
