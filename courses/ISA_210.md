### Data collection

Data collection is the systematic process of gathering information from various sources to be used for analysis and decision-making. In data science, collection methods range from traditional surveys to automated techniques like web scraping and API calls, each suited to different types of data and research objectives.

- **APIs (Application Programming Interfaces)**: Provide structured, real-time access to data from platforms like social media, financial services, and government databases
- **Web scraping**: Automated extraction of data from websites; useful for unstructured data like reviews, pricing, and public content, though legal and ethical considerations apply
- **Surveys and forms**: Collect primary data directly from respondents, offering customizable questions and built-in analytics
- **Existing datasets**: Public repositories (e.g., Kaggle, government open data portals) and organizational databases provide ready-made data for analysis
- **Best practice**: Combine multiple methods -- use APIs for stable structured data and scraping for diverse unstructured data not available through APIs

**Practical relevance**: The quality of any data science project depends on the quality of its data. Understanding the strengths, limitations, and ethical considerations of different collection methods ensures that analysts start with reliable, representative data.

### Exploratory data analysis

Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) is an approach to analyzing datasets that emphasizes summarizing their main characteristics through statistical graphics, visualization, and summary statistics. Pioneered by John Tukey in the 1970s, EDA encourages analysts to let the data reveal its structure before applying formal models or hypothesis tests.

- **Statistical summaries**: Mean, median, variance, standard deviation, and range reveal central tendency, spread, and distribution shape
- **Visualization techniques**: Histograms (distribution), scatter plots (relationships), box plots (outliers and spread), and bar charts (categorical comparisons)
- **Analysis types**: Univariate (single variable), bivariate (two variables), and multivariate (multiple variables using correlation matrices and heatmaps)
- **Key objectives**: Detect patterns and trends, identify anomalies and outliers, test assumptions, and generate hypotheses for further investigation

**Practical relevance**: EDA is an essential first step in any data science project. Before building models or drawing conclusions, analysts must understand their data's structure, quality, and relationships. Skipping EDA often leads to flawed models and misleading results.

### Machine learning intro

Machine learning (ML) is the process of training software models to make predictions or identify patterns from data without being explicitly programmed for each task. ML algorithms learn from historical data to improve their performance over time, and they fall into two primary categories based on the type of training data used.

- **Supervised learning**: Uses labeled datasets (input-output pairs) to train models for classification (categorizing data) and regression (predicting numerical values). Examples include spam detection, sentiment analysis, and price forecasting.
- **Unsupervised learning**: Works with unlabeled data to discover hidden patterns and structures. Common techniques include clustering (grouping similar data points) and dimensionality reduction. Examples include customer segmentation and anomaly detection.
- **Key distinction**: Supervised learning requires labeled training data; unsupervised learning finds structure in data without labels
- **Common algorithms**: Linear regression, decision trees, k-nearest neighbors (supervised); k-means clustering, principal component analysis (unsupervised)

**Practical relevance**: Machine learning powers recommendation engines, fraud detection, medical diagnosis, and countless other applications. An introductory understanding of ML concepts helps data science students appreciate when and how to apply these techniques to real-world business problems.

### Text mining

Text mining is the process of extracting meaningful patterns, trends, and insights from unstructured text data. Closely related to Natural Language Processing (NLP), text mining transforms human language into structured formats that can be analyzed computationally, enabling organizations to derive value from documents, emails, social media posts, and other text sources.

- **Key techniques**: Sentiment analysis (detecting emotional tone), text classification (categorizing documents), named entity recognition (identifying people, places, organizations), and topic modeling (discovering themes)
- **Text preprocessing**: Tokenization (splitting text into words), stemming/lemmatization (reducing words to root forms), stop word removal, and part-of-speech tagging
- **NLP relationship**: NLP focuses on understanding and generating human language; text mining focuses on extracting actionable insights from text data
- **Applications**: Customer feedback analysis, market intelligence, search engine optimization, chatbot development, automated summarization, and social media monitoring

**Practical relevance**: With the vast majority of business data existing as unstructured text, text mining enables organizations to unlock insights from customer reviews, support tickets, news articles, and internal documents that would be impossible to analyze manually at scale.

### Data ethics

Data ethics is the branch of ethics that addresses the responsible collection, processing, and use of data, ensuring privacy, security, fairness, transparency, and accountability. As data science techniques become more powerful and pervasive, ethical considerations are essential to prevent harm and maintain public trust.

- **Fairness and equity**: Benefits and burdens of data-driven systems must be distributed fairly, with attention to protecting vulnerable populations and avoiding perpetuation of historical inequalities
- **Privacy and consent**: Individuals have a reasonable expectation that their data will be protected; informed consent is required before data collection
- **Bias mitigation**: Algorithms can contain intentional or unintentional bias; techniques like re-sampling, re-weighting, and fairness-aware ML help reduce discriminatory outcomes
- **Transparency and accountability**: Organizations must clearly communicate how data is collected, stored, used, and shared

**Practical relevance**: Data ethics is not just a theoretical concern -- biased algorithms and privacy violations have real consequences including discrimination, financial harm, and erosion of public trust. Data scientists must integrate ethical thinking into every stage of their work, from data collection through model deployment.

### Data science pipeline

The data science pipeline is a structured workflow that guides a project from initial business question to deployed solution. The most widely adopted framework is CRISP-DM (Cross-Industry Standard Process for Data Mining), which defines six iterative phases that structure the entire data science lifecycle.

- **Business Understanding**: Define project objectives, success criteria, and how results will be used by stakeholders
- **Data Understanding**: Identify, collect, and explore available data sources; assess data quality and relevance
- **Data Preparation**: Clean, transform, and structure data for modeling (typically 50-80% of total project effort)
- **Modeling**: Select and apply appropriate algorithms; tune parameters and build candidate models
- **Evaluation**: Assess model performance against business criteria; ensure predictions are actionable and reliable
- **Deployment**: Integrate the model into production systems, plan for monitoring, and support user adoption

**Practical relevance**: The CRISP-DM pipeline is cyclical, not linear -- lessons from later phases often trigger returns to earlier stages. Understanding this structured yet flexible workflow helps data scientists manage projects systematically, avoid common pitfalls, and deliver results that genuinely address business needs.
