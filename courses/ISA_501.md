### Linear algebra

Linear algebra is the branch of mathematics dealing with vectors, matrices, and linear transformations. It provides the mathematical framework underlying most machine learning algorithms, data transformations, and dimensionality reduction techniques used in modern analytics.

- **Key concepts:** Vectors and vector spaces, matrices and matrix operations (addition, multiplication, transpose, inverse), eigenvalues and eigenvectors, determinants, linear transformations
- **Matrix operations:** Dot product, cross product, matrix decomposition (SVD, LU, QR), solving systems of linear equations (Ax = b)
- **Dimensionality reduction:** Principal Component Analysis (PCA) relies on eigenvalue decomposition to reduce feature dimensions while preserving variance
- **Practical relevance:** Linear algebra underpins neural networks (weight matrices), recommendation systems (matrix factorization), computer vision (image transformations), and regression models. NumPy provides efficient implementations of all core linear algebra operations in Python.

### Probability

Probability is the mathematical study of uncertainty and randomness, quantifying the likelihood of events occurring. It forms the theoretical foundation for statistical inference, machine learning algorithms, and decision-making under uncertainty in data science.

- **Key concepts:** Sample spaces and events, probability axioms, conditional probability, Bayes' theorem, independence, random variables (discrete and continuous)
- **Distributions:** Normal (Gaussian), binomial, Poisson, uniform, exponential; each models different types of real-world phenomena
- **Bayes' theorem:** P(A|B) = P(B|A) * P(A) / P(B); foundational for Bayesian inference, spam filtering, medical diagnosis, and probabilistic classifiers
- **Law of large numbers and Central Limit Theorem:** As sample sizes grow, sample means converge to population means and become approximately normally distributed
- **Practical relevance:** Probability is essential for understanding model uncertainty, evaluating A/B test results, building Bayesian models, and interpreting confidence intervals. It enables data scientists to quantify risk and make informed decisions under uncertainty.

### Statistics

Statistics is the science of collecting, organizing, analyzing, interpreting, and presenting data. It provides the methods and frameworks for drawing conclusions from data, testing hypotheses, and making predictions, forming the analytical backbone of data science.

- **Descriptive statistics:** Measures of central tendency (mean, median, mode), dispersion (variance, standard deviation, IQR), skewness, kurtosis, frequency distributions
- **Inferential statistics:** Confidence intervals, hypothesis testing (t-tests, chi-square, ANOVA), p-values, significance levels, Type I and Type II errors
- **Correlation and association:** Pearson correlation, Spearman rank correlation, covariance; distinguishing correlation from causation
- **Sampling methods:** Random sampling, stratified sampling, cluster sampling; understanding sampling bias and its effects on inference
- **Practical relevance:** Statistics is fundamental to every stage of data analytics, from exploratory data analysis and quality control to A/B testing, survey analysis, and model evaluation. Statistical literacy enables professionals to critically evaluate claims and make evidence-based decisions.

### Calculus

Calculus is the mathematical study of continuous change, encompassing differentiation (rates of change) and integration (accumulation). In data science, calculus provides the mathematical engine that powers optimization algorithms used to train machine learning models.

- **Differentiation:** Derivatives measure instantaneous rates of change; partial derivatives extend this to functions of multiple variables; the chain rule is essential for backpropagation in neural networks
- **Gradient descent:** An optimization algorithm that uses derivatives to iteratively minimize a loss function by moving in the direction of steepest descent; learning rate controls step size
- **Integration:** Computes areas under curves, cumulative distributions, and expected values; integral calculus underpins probability density functions
- **Multivariable calculus:** Gradients, Jacobians, and Hessians describe how functions change across multiple dimensions; critical for understanding optimization landscapes
- **Practical relevance:** Calculus is the mathematical foundation of model training in machine learning. Gradient descent and its variants (SGD, Adam, RMSprop) rely on partial derivatives to update model parameters. Understanding calculus helps practitioners diagnose training issues and tune optimization hyperparameters.
