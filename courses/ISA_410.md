### Robotics

**Definition:** Robotics is a branch of technology that deals with the design, construction, operation, and programming of robots -- machines capable of performing tasks autonomously or semi-autonomously. It integrates computer science, mechanical engineering, electrical engineering, and mathematics.

- **Key components** include control systems (the "brain" using PLCs and algorithms), actuators/motors (converting electrical energy to motion), sensors (perceiving the environment), end effectors (tools for interaction like grippers), and power supplies
- **Kinematics** studies robot motion without considering forces; forward kinematics determines end-effector position from joint parameters, while inverse kinematics computes joint parameters needed for a desired position
- **Degrees of freedom (DOF)** define how many independent movements a robot can make; more DOF enables greater dexterity
- **Robot types** include articulated arms, mobile robots, humanoids, collaborative robots (cobots), and aerial drones
- **ROS (Robot Operating System)** is the standard open-source middleware framework for robot software development

**Practical relevance:** Robotics is transforming manufacturing, healthcare, logistics, agriculture, and exploration. Understanding fundamental robotics concepts is essential for working with platforms like TurtleBot 3, NAO, and Pepper used in ISA 410, and for careers in automation and intelligent systems.

### Machine learning for robotics

**Definition:** Machine learning for robotics applies supervised, unsupervised, and reinforcement learning techniques to enable robots to perceive their environment, make decisions, and improve task performance through experience rather than explicit programming.

- **Reinforcement learning (RL)** enables robots to learn through trial and error guided by reward signals, widely used for control, motion planning, navigation, and manipulation tasks
- **Deep reinforcement learning** combines deep neural networks with RL, allowing robots to learn directly from high-dimensional sensory inputs (images, point clouds) to control outputs in an end-to-end manner
- **Robot perception** uses ML to process sensor data (camera feeds, LiDAR scans) for object recognition, scene understanding, and environment mapping using architectures like CNNs
- **Sim-to-real transfer** trains models in simulated environments (e.g., NVIDIA Isaac) before deploying on physical robots, addressing the challenge of collecting real-world training data
- **Key challenges** include data demands, hardware limitations, dynamic environments requiring generalization, and the gap between simulation and real-world performance

**Practical relevance:** ML is what transforms rigid machines into adaptive systems. Reinforcement learning enables robots to tackle tasks that are difficult to program explicitly, such as dexterous manipulation and autonomous navigation. This integration of ML and robotics is central to ISA 410's hands-on curriculum.

### Turtlebot 3

**Definition:** TurtleBot 3 is a small, affordable, programmable ROS-based mobile robot developed by ROBOTIS, designed for education, research, and prototyping. It is one of the most widely used platforms for learning robotics and ROS.

- **Models:** Available as Burger (compact, 138mm x 178mm x 192mm) and Waffle Pi (includes a camera for visual recognition); both feature 360-degree LiDAR, 9-axis IMU, and precise encoders
- **Core capabilities** include SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping), autonomous navigation, and manipulation, making it suitable for a wide range of robotics applications
- **Payload capacity** of up to 15 kg (expandable to 30 kg) despite its compact form factor
- **Open source** hardware, firmware, and software allow full customization; users can modify mechanical components and upgrade sensors and computers
- **ROS integration** provides access to the full ROS ecosystem of packages for navigation, perception, planning, and visualization

**Practical relevance:** TurtleBot 3 is the standard educational platform for learning ROS, SLAM, and autonomous navigation. In ISA 410, students use it for hands-on projects involving mobile robot programming, sensor integration, and machine learning-based navigation, building practical skills directly applicable to robotics careers.

### NAO robot

**Definition:** NAO is an autonomous, programmable humanoid robot developed by Aldebaran (now under SoftBank Robotics). Standing 58 cm tall with 25 degrees of freedom, NAO can walk, dance, speak, and interact with humans through advanced sensory and communication capabilities.

- **Sensors** include 2 HD cameras for face/object recognition, 4 omnidirectional microphones and 2 speakers for natural voice interaction, an IMU with accelerometer and gyroscope for stability, and 4 ultrasonic sensors for spatial positioning
- **Programming** is supported through multiple interfaces: Choregraphe (visual drag-and-drop), Python, C++, and JavaScript SDKs, and the NAOqi operating system (Linux-based)
- **25 degrees of freedom** enable human-like movements including walking, waving, sitting, and gestural communication
- **Applications** span education (teaching programming and AI), research (human-robot interaction studies), healthcare (therapy for autistic children, elderly care), and competition (RoboCup soccer)
- **Accessibility** ranges from simple visual programming for beginners to complex embedded module development for advanced users

**Practical relevance:** NAO is one of the most widely deployed humanoid robots in education and research worldwide. In ISA 410, students program NAO to explore human-robot interaction, motion planning, speech recognition, and computer vision, gaining experience with a platform used across academic institutions globally.

### Pepper robot

**Definition:** Pepper is a 120 cm (4 ft) semi-humanoid social robot developed by Aldebaran and manufactured by SoftBank Robotics, designed specifically for human interaction. It is the first mass-produced robot capable of recognizing human emotions through facial expression and voice tone analysis.

- **Emotion recognition** uses AI to analyze facial expressions and voice tones, allowing Pepper to understand and adapt its behavior to provide personalized interactions
- **Sensory systems** include 4 microphones, 2 HD cameras, a 3D depth sensor, a gyroscope, touch sensors in head and hands, and recognition of 15 languages
- **Physical features** include an expressive face with LED eyes, articulated arms and hands, a 10.1-inch chest-mounted touchscreen tablet, and 3 omnidirectional wheels for smooth movement on flat surfaces
- **Deployment environments** include retail stores, hotels, hospitals, airports, schools, and cruise ships for customer service, greeting, information delivery, and entertainment
- **Limitations** include inability to walk, climb stairs, or navigate uneven terrain; production was paused in 2020-2021

**Practical relevance:** Pepper represents the frontier of social robotics and human-robot interaction design. In ISA 410, working with Pepper provides hands-on experience in programming social behaviors, emotion-aware responses, and multimodal interaction -- skills relevant to the growing field of service robotics and customer-facing AI systems.

### Sensor integration

**Definition:** Sensor integration (sensor fusion) is the process of combining data from multiple sensors to produce a more accurate, reliable, and complete understanding of the environment than any single sensor could provide alone. It is a critical capability for autonomous robotic systems.

- **LiDAR** emits laser pulses to generate 3D point clouds with centimeter-level spatial accuracy, performing well in varied lighting but producing sparse data with limited semantic content
- **Cameras** provide dense 2D pixel arrays rich in texture and color information for semantic understanding, but are susceptible to lighting changes and motion blur
- **IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit)** measures angular velocity and linear acceleration at high frequency, providing orientation and motion data for stabilization and dead reckoning
- **Fusion approaches** include LiDAR-IMU SLAM, Visual-IMU SLAM, LiDAR-Visual SLAM, and full LiDAR-IMU-Visual SLAM, each combining sensor strengths to compensate for individual weaknesses
- **Calibration** (extrinsic and intrinsic) is critical; even small calibration errors can cause significant misalignment in cross-modal data fusion

**Practical relevance:** Sensor fusion is essential for autonomous driving, drone navigation, and robotic manipulation. In ISA 410, students integrate data from LiDAR, cameras, and IMUs on robots like TurtleBot 3, learning how multi-sensor systems enable reliable perception in real-world conditions where individual sensors would fail.

### Autonomous systems

**Definition:** Autonomous systems are robots or vehicles capable of operating and making decisions independently in real-world environments without continuous human control. They rely on perception, planning, and control subsystems working together to navigate, avoid obstacles, and complete tasks.

- **SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping)** enables a robot to build a map of an unknown environment while simultaneously tracking its own position within that map, essential when GPS is unavailable (indoors, underground)
- **Path planning** algorithms compute optimal routes from start to destination while avoiding obstacles and respecting constraints; approaches range from graph-based (A*, Dijkstra) to sampling-based (RRT, PRM)
- **Navigation methods** are either map-based (using a pre-existing or SLAM-built map) or mapless (reactive approaches using sensor data in real time)
- **SLAM types** include visual SLAM (cameras), LiDAR SLAM (laser sensors), and multi-sensor SLAM combining cameras, IMUs, GPS, and LiDAR for enhanced accuracy
- **Autonomy stack** integrates SLAM with path planning, obstacle avoidance, behavior prediction, and motion control into a complete system

**Practical relevance:** Autonomous systems power self-driving cars, delivery robots, warehouse automation, and exploration drones. In ISA 410, students implement SLAM and navigation algorithms on TurtleBot 3, gaining hands-on experience with the core technologies that enable autonomous operation in robotics applications.
