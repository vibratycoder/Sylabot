### Independent research

Independent research in information systems involves a self-directed investigation conducted by an undergraduate student that makes an original intellectual or creative contribution to the discipline. It typically follows a capstone format where students define a research question, determine methodology, collect and analyze data, and produce a substantial paper or project.

**Key Concepts:**
- **Research process:** Includes research methodology selection, literature review, data collection, analysis, and presentation of findings -- demonstrating mastery of the subject area.
- **Critical thinking and synthesis:** Capstone research integrates and applies the knowledge and skills acquired throughout the degree program, requiring students to think critically, communicate effectively, and work independently.
- **Original contribution:** Unlike coursework, independent research contributes to scholarly discourse in information systems rather than simply learning about a topic not covered in existing courses.
- **Project formats:** May include research papers, software development projects, data analysis studies, system design proposals, or other applied IS work.

**Practical Relevance:** Independent research develops skills highly valued in both industry and graduate education, including problem formulation, systematic investigation, analytical reasoning, and professional communication. The experience of managing a sustained research project from conception to completion mirrors the kind of self-directed work expected of senior IS professionals and prepares students for advanced study or leadership roles.

### Faculty-directed study

Faculty-directed study is an academic arrangement where a student pursues an area of inquiry or topic of interest not generally offered through approved courses, working under the supervision and mentorship of a faculty member. The level of faculty involvement ranges from considerable supervision (directed study) to more autonomous student-led work (independent study).

**Key Concepts:**
- **Mentorship structure:** A student and faculty member agree upon a topic, scope, methodology, and credit hours. The faculty mentor provides guidance, feedback, and assessment throughout the project.
- **Broad learning experiences:** May encompass laboratory work, research projects, creative productions, faculty-supervised peer-mentor experiences, or academically supervised applied projects.
- **Distinction from coursework:** Directed study allows exploration of specialized topics beyond the standard curriculum, providing depth in areas aligned with the student's career interests or graduate school aspirations.
- **Faculty discretion:** Faculty members may accept or decline requests to supervise directed studies based on their expertise, availability, and the quality of the student's proposal.

**Practical Relevance:** Faculty-directed study provides a structured yet flexible framework for senior students to develop specialized expertise in niche areas of information systems and analytics. The one-on-one mentorship relationship builds professional skills including project planning, scholarly communication, and the ability to receive and incorporate expert feedback -- competencies essential for both industry careers and graduate-level academic work.
