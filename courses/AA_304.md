# AA 304: Managing Information for Applied Analytics

**Credits:** 3  
**Level:** undergraduate

### Information management

Information management is the discipline of collecting, organizing, maintaining, and governing an organization's data assets throughout their lifecycle. It encompasses data governance frameworks that ensure data quality, security, accessibility, and compliance through clearly defined policies and roles.

- **Core principles:** Accountability (designated data owners), data accuracy (validation and quality checks), accessibility (timely secure access for authorized users), integrity (consistency across the data lifecycle)
- **Governance components:** Policies and procedures, data quality standards, security protocols, compliance monitoring, audit trails
- **Key activities:** Data cataloging, metadata management, lifecycle management, privacy protection, regulatory compliance

**Practical relevance:** Effective information management ensures that organizations can trust their data for decision-making. Proper governance reduces risk, maintains regulatory compliance, and enables reliable analytics by ensuring data remains accurate, consistent, and accessible throughout its entire lifecycle.

### Data acquisition

Data acquisition is the process of gathering, collecting, and importing data from various sources for storage, analysis, and decision-making. It involves transforming real-world signals and information into digital formats, using both primary collection methods (direct gathering) and secondary sources (pre-existing datasets).

- **Collection methods:** Surveys, APIs, web scraping (BeautifulSoup, Scrapy, Selenium), IoT sensors, transaction logs, CRM systems, third-party data providers
- **Acquisition approaches:** Continuous (real-time monitoring), event-driven (triggered by specific conditions), multi-channel (simultaneous collection from multiple sources)
- **Data types:** Primary data (experiments, observations, simulations) and secondary data (existing databases, public datasets, purchased data)

**Practical relevance:** Data acquisition is the foundational step in any analytics workflow. The quality and comprehensiveness of acquired data directly determines the reliability of all downstream analysis, making proper collection methods and source selection critical for successful analytics projects.

### Data storage

Data storage encompasses the technologies and architectures used to persist, organize, and retrieve data for analysis. Modern storage solutions range from traditional relational databases to cloud-based data warehouses and data lakes, each optimized for different use cases and data types.

- **Key technologies:** Relational databases (SQL-based), data warehouses (structured analytics), data lakes (semi/unstructured data), cloud storage platforms
- **Cloud data warehouses:** Snowflake (separates compute/storage/services), Google BigQuery (serverless, auto-scaling), Amazon Redshift, Azure Synapse
- **Architecture concepts:** Columnar storage, massively parallel processing (MPP), elastic compute, separation of storage and compute layers

**Practical relevance:** Choosing the right storage technology is critical for analytics performance. Cloud data warehouses have become the standard for modern analytics, offering elastic scalability (pay only for what you use), near-limitless storage, and the ability to consolidate data from multiple sources without managing physical infrastructure.

### Text analysis

Text analysis uses computational methods to systematically extract, quantify, and study information from text data. It encompasses techniques from natural language processing, machine learning, and linguistics to perform tasks such as sentiment analysis, document classification, and information extraction.

- **Core approaches:** Lexicon-based (rule/dictionary-driven), machine learning-based (trained classifiers), deep learning-based (neural networks), and hybrid methods
- **Key techniques:** Sentiment analysis (opinion mining), document classification, topic modeling, named entity recognition, text summarization
- **Feature extraction:** Bag-of-Words (word frequency vectors), TF-IDF (term importance relative to document context), word embeddings
- **Algorithms:** Naive Bayes, Support Vector Machines (SVMs), neural networks for classification tasks

**Practical relevance:** Text analysis is widely applied to customer reviews, survey responses, social media content, and healthcare records. Organizations use it to monitor brand sentiment, categorize support tickets, extract key information from documents, and automate content analysis at scale.

### Social media analytics

Social media analytics is the collection, analysis, and reporting of data from social media platforms to measure performance, understand audiences, and guide business decisions. It transforms social channel data into actionable insights about engagement, reach, sentiment, and return on investment.

- **Key metrics:** Engagement rate, reach, impressions, click-through rate, conversion rate, sentiment score, follower growth
- **Analytics types:** Performance analytics, audience analytics, competitive analysis, sentiment analysis, social listening, paid social analytics, influencer analytics
- **Tools:** Platform-native analytics (built into each social network), third-party tools (Sprout Social, Hootsuite, Meltwater) for cross-platform monitoring

**Practical relevance:** Social media analytics enables organizations to prove ROI on social marketing efforts, understand audience behavior, monitor brand health, and make data-informed content decisions. It is essential for modern marketing strategies, customer service optimization, and competitive intelligence.

### Bioinformatics

Bioinformatics is an interdisciplinary field that develops and applies computational methods to understand biological data, particularly large and complex datasets such as genomic sequences. It integrates principles from biology, computer science, statistics, and mathematics to collect, store, analyze, and interpret biological information.

- **Core activities:** Genome sequencing analysis, protein structure prediction, gene identification, single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) detection, evolutionary analysis
- **Techniques used:** Pattern recognition, data mining, machine learning algorithms, statistical modeling, sequence alignment, visualization
- **Related field:** Computational biology (emphasizes theoretical methods and mathematical modeling, while bioinformatics focuses on informatics and statistics)

**Practical relevance:** Bioinformatics is essential for modern biological research and personalized medicine. It enables researchers to analyze vast genomic datasets, identify disease-related genetic variants, develop targeted therapies, and advance drug discovery through computational analysis rather than purely experimental methods.

### Decision support

A Decision Support System (DSS) is an information system that aids organizational decision-making by integrating data from multiple sources and applying analytical techniques to provide descriptive, predictive, and prescriptive insights. Modern DSS platforms incorporate big data, AI, and machine learning to analyze both structured and unstructured data.

- **Types:** Data-driven DSS (uses data mining to predict outcomes), model-driven DSS (built on decision models for scenario analysis), knowledge-driven DSS (uses rules and expert knowledge)
- **Key capabilities:** Scenario analysis, forecasting, what-if modeling, real-time dashboards, automated recommendations
- **Modern evolution:** Integration of unstructured data (social media, reviews), advanced analytics (predictive and prescriptive), cloud computing, and AI/ML capabilities

**Practical relevance:** Decision support systems improve decision quality by uncovering hidden patterns and correlations that manual analysis might miss. They are used across industries for strategic planning, resource allocation, risk assessment, and operational optimization, helping managers make evidence-based decisions rather than relying on intuition alone.
